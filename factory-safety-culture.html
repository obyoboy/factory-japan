Japanese Factory Safety Culture:
KY Activities, Hiyari-Hatto & 5S Explained

Last updated: March 2026  |  11 min read

Japan's manufacturing industry has one of the lowest workplace accident rates in the world. This doesn't happen by accident — it's the result of a deeply embedded safety culture that every worker on the floor participates in, every single day.

As a foreign worker, understanding this safety system isn't just about following rules. It's about protecting yourself, earning the trust of your colleagues, and keeping your job. This guide explains the key safety concepts you'll encounter in a Japanese factory — in plain English.

1. The Foundation: Safety Is Everyone's Responsibility

In many countries, "safety" means the safety officer posts some signs, and workers try not to do anything too dangerous. Japanese factories operate on a completely different principle:

Every worker is personally responsible for their own safety and the safety of those around them.

This isn't just a slogan. In a Japanese factory, you will be expected to:

  • Actively identify hazards before they cause accidents
  • Report near-misses and unsafe conditions — not just actual injuries
  • Participate in daily safety activities, not just read a notice board
  • Never take shortcuts that compromise safety, even when production is behind

If you see something unsafe and say nothing, that is considered a failure of your responsibility — regardless of your rank or how long you've been there.

2. KY活動 (KY Katsudō): Hazard Prediction Activities

KY stands for 危険予知 (kiken yochi) — danger prediction. KY activities are short group exercises, usually held at the start of each shift, where workers identify potential hazards in their upcoming work before anyone gets hurt.

How a KY Meeting Works

A typical KY activity takes 3–5 minutes and follows a structured format:

  1. Look at the work situation — often shown as a drawing or described by the team leader
  2. Identify the dangers — each worker contributes: "The floor near the machine might be wet," "The part is heavy and could slip"
  3. Agree on countermeasures — "We'll check the floor before starting," "Use two hands when lifting"
  4. Confirm with a group call — everyone responds together, often with a raised finger point: "Yoshi!" (よし！) — meaning "confirmed, let's do it safely"
The Finger-Pointing Habit (指差し確認 / Yubisashi Kakunin) You may notice Japanese workers pointing their fingers and calling out loud when checking equipment or conditions. This is called yubisashi kakunin — pointing and calling confirmation. Research shows that this physical + verbal combination reduces errors by up to 85% compared to just looking. When in doubt, do it too.

As a Foreign Worker in KY Meetings

Even if your Japanese is limited, you are expected to participate. You can:

  • Listen and try to understand the main hazard being discussed
  • Say "Hai" (はい) — "understood/yes" — at appropriate moments
  • Join the group response at the end: raise your hand/finger and say "Yoshi!"
  • After a few weeks, try to name one hazard in simple Japanese — even just pointing and saying the word will be appreciated

3. ヒヤリハット (Hiyari-Hatto): Reporting Near-Misses

This is one of the most important — and most foreign — safety concepts for workers from outside Japan.

ヒヤリハット (hiyari-hatto) refers to near-miss incidents: moments when something almost went wrong but didn't result in injury. The name comes from Japanese words meaning a startled chill (hiyari) and a close call (hatto).

Why Near-Misses Matter: The Heinrich Triangle

Japanese safety culture is heavily influenced by Heinrich's Law, which states that for every serious injury, there are approximately:

  • 29 minor injuries
  • 300 near-miss incidents

The logic: if you eliminate the 300 near-misses, you prevent the serious injury from ever happening. This is why Japanese factories treat near-miss reporting as more important than accident reporting — by the time an accident happens, you've already failed.

What Counts as a Hiyari-Hatto

Situation Report It?
A part fell off the conveyor and almost hit someone Yes
You slipped on a wet floor but didn't fall Yes
A tool was left in a walkway — nobody tripped yet Yes
You felt confused about which button to press and almost pressed the wrong one Yes
A machine made an unusual sound but then stopped Yes
You cut yourself and needed a bandage Yes — this is an actual injury, report immediately

Many factories ask workers to fill out a short hiyari-hatto report form (ヒヤリハット報告書). In some factories, submitting reports earns you small recognition or points toward a safety award. Reporting is never punished — the culture specifically rewards honesty about near-misses.

Warning In some countries, workers avoid reporting incidents because they fear blame or punishment. Japanese factory culture is the opposite: hiding a near-miss is the problem, not reporting it. A worker who conceals a hiyari-hatto and someone later gets injured at the same spot faces serious consequences.

4. 5S: The Physical Foundation of Safety

You may have heard about 5S before, but many foreign workers don't realize just how directly it connects to safety. A disorganized workspace isn't just ugly — it's how people get hurt.

5S Step Safety Connection
整理 Seiri (Sort) Remove tools/parts that don't belong — clutter causes trips and confusion
整頓 Seiton (Set in order) Everything has a place — no searching under pressure = no rushed mistakes
清掃 Seisō (Shine) Clean floors = no slip hazards; clean machines = easier to spot leaks or damage
清潔 Seiketsu (Standardize) Everyone follows the same procedures = fewer surprises
躾 Shitsuke (Sustain) Habits don't slip under pressure — safety becomes automatic

During safety audits, inspectors look directly at 5S compliance as a leading indicator of accident risk. A factory that maintains 5S is statistically much safer than one that doesn't.

5. PPE: Personal Protective Equipment — No Exceptions

Japanese factories are strict about PPE (保護具 / hogugu). The rules are typically posted at the entrance to each zone, and they are enforced.

Common PPE you may be required to wear:

  • 安全靴 (Anzen gutsu) — Steel-toed safety shoes. Required in almost every factory. Your company will usually provide these or tell you where to buy them.
  • ヘルメット (Herumetto) — Hard hat, required in areas with overhead hazards
  • 保護メガネ (Hogo megane) — Safety glasses, required near cutting, grinding, or chemical operations
  • 耳栓 (Mimisen) — Earplugs, required in high-noise areas
  • 手袋 (Tebukuro) — Gloves, but note: some operations prohibit gloves (e.g., near rotating machinery where gloves can catch)
Important Never assume PPE is optional because a coworker isn't wearing it. Check the posted rules for your specific zone. If you're unsure, ask your team leader. "I didn't know" is not an acceptable defense after an injury.

6. Machine Safety: Lockout and Stop Signals

One of the most dangerous moments in any factory is when a machine is being repaired, cleaned, or adjusted. Japanese factories use a system called ロックアウト / タグアウト (lockout/tagout) to prevent machines from being accidentally started during maintenance.

Key rules that apply to all workers:

  • Never remove a lockout tag or device placed by another worker — ever
  • If a machine is tagged or locked, assume it's unsafe to operate until the technician removes the lock themselves
  • Never reach into a machine that is still powered on, even briefly
  • If a machine stops unexpectedly, do not try to restart it yourself — call your team leader

7. What to Do When You Get Injured

Accidents happen even in the safest factories. Knowing what to do in advance means you won't panic:

  1. Stop work immediately and secure the area if needed
  2. Tell your team leader right away — even for minor cuts or bruises
  3. Get first aid — the location of the first aid kit (救急箱 / kyūkyūbako) should be shown to you on day one
  4. Complete an incident report — your team leader will guide you through this
  5. Don't hide it. Even small injuries that go unreported can become serious — and unreported injuries may not be covered by workers' compensation (労災 / rōsai)
Know This Word: 労災 (Rōsai) Labor accident compensation (労災保険 / rōsai hoken) covers medical costs and lost wages when you are injured at work. It applies to all workers in Japan, including dispatch workers and foreign nationals. To use it, the injury must be officially reported. Always report injuries — it protects you.

Summary: The Safety Culture Mindset

Japanese factory safety isn't about fear of punishment. It's about a shared understanding that every person's careful behavior protects everyone. The KY meetings, the hiyari-hatto reports, the 5S cleaning — they're not bureaucratic hassle. They're the reason people go home safe at the end of every shift.

As a foreign worker, embracing this culture actively — not just going through the motions — will earn you deep respect from your Japanese coworkers and supervisors. It signals that you understand what the work is really about.
