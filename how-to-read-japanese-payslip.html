How to Read Your Japanese Factory Payslip
(and Understand Overtime Pay)

Last updated: March 2026  |  10 min read

Your first Japanese payslip looks like a wall of numbers and kanji. You expected a certain amount — but what landed in your bank account is noticeably less. You are not alone. Almost every foreign factory worker goes through this moment of confusion. This guide walks you through every section of a Japanese payslip, explains what gets deducted and why, and shows you exactly how overtime pay is calculated so you can check the numbers yourself.

1. What Is a Payslip in Japan?

In Japan, your payslip is called a 給与明細書 (kyūyo meisaisho) — literally "salary detail document." By law, your employer must provide one every pay period. Most factories pay monthly (月給 / gekkyū), though some dispatch (派遣 / haken) arrangements pay weekly or bi-weekly.

The payslip is usually divided into three sections:

  • 支給 (shikyū) — Earnings: everything your employer pays you
  • 控除 (kōjo) — Deductions: everything taken out before you receive the money
  • 差引支給額 (sashihiki shikyūgaku) — Net pay: the actual amount deposited into your bank account
Tip: Take a photo of every payslip you receive and store it somewhere safe. If there is ever a dispute about your wages, you will need these records. Japanese labor law requires employers to keep payroll records for three years — but you should keep your own copies too.

2. Earnings Section (支給): What You Are Paid

The earnings side lists every component of your gross pay. Not all of these will appear on every payslip — it depends on your contract and the month.

Japanese Term Reading Meaning
基本給 kihonkyū Base salary — your fixed monthly or hourly rate
残業手当 zangyō teate Overtime allowance — extra hours beyond the standard workday
深夜手当 shinya teate Late-night allowance — work between 22:00 and 05:00
休日手当 kyūjitsu teate Holiday work allowance — working on designated rest days
皆勤手当 kaikin teate Perfect attendance bonus — paid if you have zero absences
通勤手当 tsūkin teate Commuting allowance — contribution toward transport costs
職能手当 shokunou teate Skill or role allowance — paid for specific certified skills
Tip: Your 基本給 (kihonkyū) is the foundation for calculating overtime. If your base rate is low, even with many overtime hours your total will be lower than you expect. Always confirm your hourly base rate in your contract before you start work.

3. Deductions Section (控除): What Gets Taken Out

This is the section that surprises most foreign workers. Japan has mandatory social insurance contributions, and they add up to a significant percentage of your gross pay. The deductions are not punishment — they give you access to healthcare, pension, and unemployment benefits.

Japanese Term Reading Meaning Approx. Rate (Employee Share)
健康保険 kenkō hoken Health insurance — covers medical costs at hospitals and clinics ~5% of salary
厚生年金 kōsei nenkin Employee pension — builds your retirement pension 9.15% of salary
雇用保険 koyō hoken Employment insurance — pays you if you lose your job 0.6% of salary
所得税 shotoku zei Income tax — withheld at source each month Varies (5%–33%)
住民税 jūmin zei Resident tax — local tax based on previous year's income ~10% of previous year income
寮費 ryōhi Dormitory fee — if you live in company housing Varies by facility

Together, 健康保険, 厚生年金, and 雇用保険 are called 社会保険 (shakai hoken) — social insurance. Your employer pays an equal or greater share on top of your contribution. The rates are set by law and applied to your standard monthly wage (標準報酬月額 / hyōjun hōshū getsugaku).

Warning: 住民税 (jūmin zei / resident tax) is not deducted in your first year of working in Japan. It kicks in from June of the following year, based on what you earned the previous year. Many workers are caught off guard when their payslip suddenly shows a new deduction in June. Budget for it in advance.

4. Overtime Pay (残業代 / zangyōdai): Rules and Calculation

Japan's Labor Standards Act (労働基準法 / rōdō kijunhō) requires employers to pay a premium rate for overtime work. The rules are strict, and factories must follow them for all workers — including foreign workers and dispatch workers.

The standard work limit is 8 hours per day and 40 hours per week. Any hours beyond this threshold must be paid at a premium rate.

Situation Japanese Term Minimum Premium Rate
Overtime (beyond 8h/day or 40h/week) 時間外労働 / jikangai rōdō +25% (×1.25)
Overtime exceeding 60h/month 月60時間超の時間外 +50% (×1.50)
Work on a designated rest day (法定休日) 法定休日労働 / hōtei kyūjitsu rōdō +35% (×1.35)
Late-night work (22:00–05:00) 深夜労働 / shinya rōdō +25% (×1.25)
Overtime AND late-night combined 時間外 + 深夜 +50% (×1.50)
Rest day work AND late-night combined 休日 + 深夜 +60% (×1.60)

How to Calculate Your Overtime Pay

First, find your base hourly rate (基礎時間給 / kiso jikankyū). If you are paid monthly, divide your base salary by the number of prescribed working hours in the month.

Formula:

Hourly rate = Base monthly salary ÷ Monthly prescribed hours
Overtime pay per hour = Hourly rate × 1.25
Total overtime pay = Overtime pay per hour × Number of overtime hours

Example: Your base salary is ¥200,000/month and the prescribed hours are 160h/month.

  • Hourly rate: ¥200,000 ÷ 160 = ¥1,250/hour
  • Overtime rate (×1.25): ¥1,250 × 1.25 = ¥1,562.50/hour
  • If you worked 20 overtime hours: ¥1,562.50 × 20 = ¥31,250
Tip: Keep a personal log of your working hours every day — start time, end time, and any breaks. Compare your log to the overtime hours shown on your payslip each month. Discrepancies of even a few hours per month add up to significant lost wages over a year.

5. Haken vs. Direct Hire: How Payslips Differ

If you work through a 派遣会社 (haken gaisha) — a staffing or dispatch agency — your payslip comes from the agency, not from the factory where you physically work. This is an important distinction.

Item Direct Hire (直接雇用 / chokusetsu koyō) Dispatch / Haken (派遣)
Who pays you The factory directly The dispatch agency
Employer on payslip Factory company name Agency company name
Social insurance Through factory Through agency (if full-time haken)
Bonus (賞与 / shōyo) Often included Rarely included; may appear as a different allowance
Allowances Set by factory policy Set by agency contract — may differ from factory workers
Overtime calculation base Factory-set base salary Agency-set hourly or monthly rate
Warning: As a haken worker, you cannot directly negotiate wages with the factory. Your contract is with the agency. If you believe your overtime is being underpaid, you must raise it with your agency's担当者 (tantōsha / person in charge), not with the factory floor supervisor. Going directly to the factory management can create a contract dispute between the agency and the factory.

6. "Why Is My Pay So Low?" — A Checklist

Before assuming there is a mistake, work through this checklist. Most shortfalls have a clear explanation once you know where to look.

# What to Check Where to Find It on the Payslip
1 Is your 基本給 (base salary) correct? 支給 section, first line
2 Does the overtime hours count match your own records? 残業手当 line — often shows hours and rate separately
3 Did 住民税 (resident tax) start being deducted this June? 控除 section — new entry appearing for the first time
4 Are you paying 寮費 (dormitory fee)? 控除 section — deducted automatically if you live in company housing
5 Were you absent or late? Did it reduce your 皆勤手当? 支給 section — 皆勤手当 missing or reduced
6 Was this a short month (fewer working days)? Compare the 勤務日数 (working days count) if shown
7 Did shakai hoken rates change? (Reviewed every September) Compare 健康保険 and 厚生年金 amounts to last month
8 Is there an unexplained 貸付金返済 (loan repayment) deduction? 控除 section — check if you signed any advance or loan agreement
Tip: If you find a discrepancy and cannot resolve it by checking the payslip yourself, contact your company's 給与担当 (kyūyo tantō — payroll department) or your dispatch agency's 担当者. Bring your own time-log records. In Japan, politely asking for clarification in writing (email or LINE message) is always appropriate and creates a record of the conversation.
Warning: Unpaid overtime — called サービス残業 (sābisu zangyō), literally "service overtime" — is illegal but unfortunately still happens. If your supervisor tells you to work extra hours "off the clock" or asks you not to record certain hours, this is a labor law violation. You can report it to the local 労働基準監督署 (rōdō kijun kantokusho / Labor Standards Inspection Office) anonymously and free of charge. Foreign workers have the same rights as Japanese workers under the Labor Standards Act.

8. Final Thoughts: You Have the Right to Understand Your Pay

Reading your payslip is not just about checking numbers — it is about understanding your legal rights as a worker in Japan. The system is complex, but it is also transparent once you know the vocabulary. Every deduction has a legal basis, and every premium rate is set by law.

Start by confirming your base hourly rate and comparing your recorded hours to the overtime figure on your payslip each month. Once you make this a habit, you will quickly notice if anything is off — and you will have the records to back up your case if you need to raise a concern.

You work hard on the factory floor. Make sure you are paid correctly for every hour of it.
