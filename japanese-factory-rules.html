Unwritten Rules of Japanese Factories:
What Nobody Tells You on Day One

Last updated: March 2026  |  12 min read

When you start at a Japanese factory, your supervisor will explain the official rules: wear your safety gear, clock in on time, follow the production procedures. But there's another whole layer of rules — the ones that are never written down, never explained, and never forgiven if you break them.

These unwritten rules are what separate foreign workers who thrive for years from those who don't get their contract renewed. This article walks through the most important ones, drawn from the real experience of living and working on the factory floor in Japan.

1. The 5-Minute Rule: Early Is On Time

This one surprises almost every newcomer. Your contract says the shift starts at 8:00. You show up at 7:58. You're two minutes early. In any other country, that's fine.

In a Japanese factory, you are late.

The unspoken standard is 5分前行動 (go-fun mae kōdō) — acting 5 minutes before. By 7:55, you should already be:

  • Changed into your work uniform and safety shoes
  • Standing at your station or in the line-up area
  • Ready for the morning briefing (朝礼 / chōrei)

This means if your shift starts at 8:00, you need to arrive at the factory building by at least 7:45 to have time to change. Veteran workers at many factories arrive 15–20 minutes before their shift as a matter of habit.

Real Example A Vietnamese worker at an automotive parts factory in Aichi arrived exactly at 8:00 every day for two weeks. He was technically "on time." At his first monthly evaluation, his team leader marked him down for poor attitude. He had no idea why — nobody had told him about the 5-minute rule. Don't let this happen to you.

2. 朝礼 (Chōrei): The Morning Assembly

Most Japanese factories start each shift with a short standing meeting called 朝礼 (chōrei). This typically lasts 5–10 minutes and includes:

  • A safety reminder or announcement from the team leader
  • Today's production targets and any special instructions
  • Sometimes a group stretch (ラジオ体操 / radio taiso) to warm up the body
  • Occasionally, a "one-point lesson" on a safety topic

During chōrei, you are expected to stand straight, face forward, and pay attention. Looking at your phone, yawning loudly, or talking to a coworker while the leader is speaking is considered extremely disrespectful. Even if you don't understand every word, maintain the posture and focus.

3. 報連相 (Hourensou): The Golden Rule of Workplace Communication

報連相 (Hourensou) is one of the most important concepts in Japanese work culture. It stands for:

Japanese Term What It Means
報告 (Hōkoku) Report Tell your supervisor about completed tasks, results, and mistakes
連絡 (Renraku) Contact / Inform Share information that affects your team — schedule changes, delays, absences
相談 (Sōdan) Consult Ask before you act when you're unsure; don't make decisions alone

Foreign workers often get into trouble not because they did something wrong, but because they didn't tell anyone about it. In Japanese factory culture, silence is not neutral — it is a problem.

Examples of hourensou in practice:

  • You finish your task early → report to hanchō: "I've finished, what should I do next?"
  • You found a defective part → immediately inform your team leader, don't just set it aside quietly
  • You're going to be 10 minutes late → call before your shift, not after
  • You're not sure how to handle an unusual situation → ask before you attempt it
Warning The biggest mistake foreign workers make is trying to solve problems on their own without telling their supervisor. In Japan, this is seen as concealing information (隠す / kakusu), which destroys trust. Always report, always communicate — even bad news.

4. Cleaning Is Everyone's Job — No Exceptions

At the end of every shift, the last 5–10 minutes are reserved for cleaning (清掃 / seisō). This is not the job of the janitor or the lowest-ranking worker. Everyone cleans, from the newest part-timer to the section chief.

Each worker has a designated cleaning zone. You will be shown yours on your first day. The expectation is:

  • Sweep or wipe down your area completely
  • Return all tools to their designated spots
  • Dispose of waste in the correct bins (Japan has strict waste separation)
  • Wipe down machines if required

Do not clock out or start changing before the cleaning period is over. Workers who disappear before cleaning finishes are noticed — and remembered.

5. Never Waste Material — Mottainai Culture

Japan has a deep cultural concept called もったいない (mottainai) — the feeling of regret over waste. In a factory context, this translates to strict expectations around material use:

  • Don't use more consumables (tape, gloves, rags) than you need
  • Don't throw away anything that can still be used
  • If you make a mistake and waste material, report it — hiding it makes it much worse
  • Treat company tools and equipment with care

Some factories track consumable usage per worker. Going through supplies faster than your coworkers without a good reason will be noticed.

6. The Break Room Has Its Own Rules

Break time (休憩 / kyūkei) seems like free time, but there are real expectations:

  • Be back before the bell. When the break-end signal sounds, you should already be walking back to your station — not finishing your coffee
  • Don't eat at someone else's table. In many factories, teams sit together in specific areas. Sitting with a different group without reason can feel intrusive
  • Clean up after yourself. Always. Leave the table cleaner than you found it
  • Smoking rules are strict. Smoking is only permitted in designated smoking areas (喫煙所 / kitsuen-jo), and only during official break times
  • Napping is often tolerated but only in designated rest areas, never on the production floor or at your workstation

7. Don't Modify Your Process Without Permission

In some countries, showing initiative by improving your own workflow is praised. In a Japanese factory, changing your procedure without approval is a serious problem.

Every process on the line has been documented, tested, and approved as the "standard operation" (標準作業 / hyōjun sagyō). Changing it — even if your way seems better — can affect quality, safety, and the work of everyone else on the line.

If you have a better idea, the correct path is:

  1. Continue doing it the standard way
  2. Bring your idea to your team leader as a kaizen suggestion
  3. Let it go through the proper review process
Tip Submitting a formal kaizen suggestion (改善提案 / kaizen teian) — even a simple one — is one of the best ways to earn respect as a foreign worker. It signals that you understand the system and want to contribute, not just follow orders.

8. Know When to Work in Silence

Many foreign workers come from cultures where chatting at work is normal and even encouraged. Japanese factory floors are usually much quieter. This doesn't mean your coworkers dislike you — it means they are concentrating.

During production, talking should be limited to work-related communication. Joking around, playing music through earphones, or having long personal conversations during working hours will mark you as someone who doesn't take the work seriously.

Save the social interaction for break time — and even then, read the room. Some Japanese coworkers use break time to rest quietly, and that's their right.

9. Overtime: The Unspoken Expectation

This is a sensitive one. Officially, overtime (残業 / zangyō) is voluntary. In practice, at many Japanese factories — especially smaller ones — leaving immediately when your shift ends while others are still working is seen as a lack of team spirit.

The reality varies by factory. Larger factories and those with foreign dispatch workers tend to be more relaxed about this. But if you work in a smaller or more traditional factory, notice what your coworkers do:

  • If most people stay 15–30 minutes after shift, plan for that
  • If a particular day is clearly very busy, offering to stay shows good character
  • Always confirm overtime with your team leader — don't just assume
Important You should always be paid for overtime. If you are regularly working past your shift without overtime pay, that is a labor law violation. Track your hours and consult your dispatch agency or the local Labor Standards Bureau (労働基準監督署) if needed.

None of these rules are designed to make your life difficult. They exist because Japanese manufacturing depends on precision, teamwork, and trust. When every person on the floor follows the same standards, the whole system runs smoothly. Once you internalize these norms, you'll find that working in a Japanese factory becomes much more predictable — and even rewarding.
