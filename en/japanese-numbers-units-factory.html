Japanese Numbers and Units for Factory Workers: A Practical Cheat Sheet

Last updated: April 2026  |  8 min read

Your 班長 (hanchō / team leader) says "ni-jū-go kiro" and points at a box. The display on the machine reads "4000 RPM." The morning announcement mentions a defect rate target of "50 PPM." Numbers are everywhere on the factory floor — in measurements, machine settings, production targets, and daily instructions. If you can't parse them quickly, you slow down the line and lose confidence. This guide covers the numbers, counters, and units you actually need to function in a Japanese factory.

1. Basic Numbers: 1 to 10,000

Japanese numbers follow a logical system once you see the pattern. Learn the base numbers and you can construct any number.

4四shi / yonUse "yon" in factory — see Tip below
5五go5S, 5 minutes

7七shichi / nanaUse "nana" to avoid confusion with ichi

9九ku / kyū9 pieces, 9 o'clock
10十jū10 units, 10 minutes
100百hyaku100 pieces target
1,000千sen1,000 yen, 1,000 parts
10,000万man10,000 = 1万 (ichiman)

For compound numbers: 21 = ni-jū-ichi (2×10+1), 35 = san-jū-go, 250 = ni-hyaku-go-jū, 1,500 = sen-go-hyaku. Japanese counts in units of 10,000 (万/man), not 1,000, so 50,000 = go-man, 100,000 = jū-man.

Tip The number 4 (四) is read "yon" in modern factory speech, not "shi" — because "shi" sounds like 死 (death). Similarly, 9 is often read "kyū" rather than "ku" because "ku" sounds like 苦 (suffering). You'll hear both, but when you speak, use "yon" and "kyū" to be safe and clear.

4. Temperature

Temperature in Japanese factories is always Celsius (摂氏 / sesshi or °C). The word for degrees is 度 (do).

  • 20°C = ni-jū do
  • 180°C = hyaku-hachi-jū do (common oven/furnace setting)
  • -10°C = マイナス jū do (minus jū do)
  • Normal room temperature target: ni-jū-go do (25°C)

5. Time Expressions on the Factory Floor

Hours use 時 (ji), minutes use 分 (fun/pun), seconds use 秒 (byō).

  • 8:00 AM = hachi-ji (8 o'clock)
  • 8:30 = hachi-ji san-juppun
  • 15 minutes = jū-go fun
  • Cycle time of 45 seconds = yon-jū-go byō
  • Lunch break: 12:00–13:00 = jū-ni-ji kara jū-san-ji made

For shift times, you'll often see 24-hour clock: 22:00 = ni-jū-ni-ji (night shift start).

6. Defect Rates and Production Numbers

Quality and production metrics use specific vocabulary you'll hear in morning meetings (朝礼 / chōrei):

  • PPM (parts per million): Read as "pī-pī-emu" — target is often 50 PPM or less
  • 不良率 (furyō ritsu): Defect rate — "furyō ritsu ichi-pāsento" = 1% defect rate
  • 達成率 (tassei ritsu): Achievement rate — "tassei ritsu kyū-jū-go pāsento" = 95% achieved
  • 生産数 (seisan-sū): Production count — "kyō no seisan-sū wa sen-go-hyaku" = today's production is 1,500
Warning When a supervisor gives you a measurement or count you're not sure about, always ask: 「もう一度お願いします」(mō ichi-do onegaishimasu / please say it once more) or 「何個ですか？」(nan-ko desu ka / how many pieces?). Getting the number wrong — putting 30 parts in a box that should have 20, or setting a machine to 180°C instead of 80°C — causes defects, rework, and potentially safety issues. Never guess a number. Always confirm.

8. Summary: Numbers Are a Core Factory Skill

You don't need to master Japanese grammar to work in a Japanese factory, but you do need to understand numbers reliably. Practice the base numbers (1–10, 100, 1000, 10000), learn the four most common counters (ko, hon, mai, dai), and get comfortable with metric units read in Japanese. When you can hear "ni-jū-go kiro" and immediately think "25 kilograms," you'll keep up with the line, avoid mistakes, and earn trust from your team.
