Quality Check Basics: What Foreign Workers Do on the Inspection Line

Last updated: 2026-04-09  |  6 min read

Quality checking, or 品質検査 (hinshitsu kensa), is one of the most common tasks assigned to foreign workers in Japanese factories. Understanding what to look for and how to report problems correctly is essential to doing your job well and avoiding costly mistakes.

1. What Is Quality Checking in a Japanese Factory?

Quality checking — called 品質検査 / hinshitsu kensa in Japanese — means examining products to make sure they meet the factory's standards before they are packaged or shipped. In many factories, this is a key role assigned to foreign workers on the line (ライン / rain).

Your job is simple in concept: look at each item, compare it to the standard (基準 / kijun), and decide if it passes (合格 / gōkaku) or fails (不合格 / fugoukaku). But doing this quickly and accurately, every hour of every shift, takes focus and practice.

Tip On your first days, ask your hanchō (班長 / team leader) to show you actual sample products — both passing and failing ones. This "limit sample" (限度見本 / gendo mihon) is your most important reference tool.

2. Common Types of Defects You Will Look For

Defects vary by factory and product type, but most inspection tasks involve checking for one or more of the following:

Defect TypeJapanese TermWhat to Look For
Scratchキズ (kizu)Surface marks or cuts on the product
Dent / Deformation変形 (henkei)Bent, crushed, or misshapen parts
Missing part欠品 (kekkin)Component not attached or absent
Color defect色むら (iro mura)Uneven color, staining, or discoloration
Size error寸法不良 (sunpō furyō)Item is too large, too small, or misaligned
Contamination異物混入 (ibuttsu konyū)Foreign material found inside or on the product

Always check your line's specific defect list (不良項目 / furyō kōmoku) — not all defects apply to every product.

3. How to Inspect: The Basic Process

Most visual inspection jobs follow a set routine. Here is the typical flow:

Step 1 — Position yourself correctly. Stand or sit at the correct distance from the inspection area. Lighting (照明 / shōmei) matters — if you cannot see the product clearly, tell your hanchō.

Step 2 — Check each item systematically. Don't rush. Inspect all required surfaces in order: top, bottom, sides. Many factories have a checklist (チェックリスト) or inspection sheet (検査表 / kensa hyō) you must follow.

Step 3 — Decide: pass or fail. Compare to the limit sample. If unsure, do not guess — ask your team leader or set the item aside as a query (保留 / horyū).

Step 4 — Handle rejected items correctly. Place failed items into the designated defect box (不良箱 / furyō bako) or mark them with a rejection sticker (不合格シール). Never mix rejected items back into the good product flow.

Warning Never pass a product you are unsure about just because the line is moving fast. Shipping a defective product can cause a serious customer complaint (クレーム / kurēmu) and may lead to your entire line being held responsible. When in doubt, stop and ask.

4. Recording and Reporting Defects

Finding defects is only half the job — you must also record and report them correctly. Each factory has its own system, but common methods include:

  • Writing the count on a tally sheet (正の字 / sei no ji method — a tally mark system)
  • Entering numbers into a tablet or computer terminal
  • Calling out to your hanchō immediately if the defect rate suddenly increases

If you notice a sudden spike in defects — for example, 5 bad items in a row — this is called a 連続不良 (renzoku furyō) and you must alert your team leader right away. This could indicate a problem with the machine upstream.

Tip Learn the phrase 「不良が多いです」(Furyō ga ōi desu) — "There are many defects." This is often enough to get your hanchō's attention quickly when something goes wrong on the line.

5. Working at Line Speed Without Making Mistakes

One of the hardest parts of inspection work is maintaining accuracy while keeping up with the line speed (ライン速度 / rain sokudo). Here are some strategies that experienced workers use:

  • Develop a rhythm. Inspect in the same order every time so your eyes move automatically.
  • Focus your eyes, not your hands. Your hands handle the product; your eyes should already be on the next item.
  • Take your breaks seriously. Eye fatigue is real — inspection accuracy drops significantly in the last hour before a break.
  • Don't skip items. It is better to pause the line briefly than to let an unchecked product pass.

If the line speed is too fast for you to check properly, you have the right to tell your hanchō. Rushing inspection is a quality risk, and good team leaders know this.

6. Handling the Gray Zone — Items That Are Hard to Judge

Not every defect is obvious. Some items fall into a gray zone where you are not sure if they pass or fail. This is called a 判定困難品 (hantei konnan hin) — a difficult-to-judge item.

In most factories, the correct procedure is:

  1. Set the item aside in a separate tray or area, clearly labeled 「保留」(horyū — on hold).
  2. Call your hanchō or quality leader (品質リーダー / hinshitsu rīdā) to make the final decision.
  3. Do not dispose of or rework the item yourself.
Warning Never make up your mind on a borderline item based on what you think your boss wants. Passing a product to avoid slowing the line — and later having a customer complaint traced back to your inspection — is a serious workplace issue in Japan. Always escalate unclear cases.

8. Why Quality Checking Matters — Your Role in the Bigger Picture

In Japanese manufacturing, quality is everything. The concept of ものづくり (monozukuri — the art of making things) means that every worker on the line is personally responsible for the quality of what they produce. When you catch a defect, you are not just doing your job — you are protecting the customer, the company's reputation, and your colleagues' work.

Many experienced workers say that after a few months on inspection, they start to see quality differently — in everything they buy and use outside of work too. That attention to detail is a skill that stays with you.
