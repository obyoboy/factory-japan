Forklift Safety Rules Every Factory Worker Must Know in Japan

Last updated: 2026-04-09  |  6 min read

Forklifts are one of the leading causes of serious injury in Japanese factories. Knowing where they move, when to stop, and how to make yourself visible could save your life.

1. Why Forklift Safety Is Taken Seriously in Japan

In Japanese factories, forklifts — called fōkurifuto (フォークリフト) — are essential for moving heavy goods quickly. But they are also responsible for a significant number of workplace injuries every year, many of them fatal. Unlike vehicles on a road, forklifts operate in tight, busy spaces alongside workers on foot. The driver's visibility is limited, and the machine can weigh several tons.

Japanese factories follow strict rules set by the Rōdō Anzen Eisei Hō (労働安全衛生法), the Industrial Safety and Health Act. Violations can result in serious penalties for your employer — and serious injuries for you. Your hanchō (班長 / team leader) will likely cover these rules during orientation, but understanding them yourself gives you real protection.

Tip If you are unsure whether a path is a forklift route, ask your hanchō before walking through it. Never assume a path is safe just because no forklift is visible right now.

2. Forklift Traffic Zones: Yellow Lines Are Not Decoration

Most Japanese factories paint yellow lines on the floor to separate forklift lanes from pedestrian walkways. These lines are called tsūro hyōji (通路表示). They are not suggestions — crossing them improperly is a major safety violation.

  • Yellow-striped zones: Shared zones where forklifts and people may cross. Extra caution required.
  • Single yellow line: Boundary between forklift lane and pedestrian lane. Stay on your side.
  • Red zones: Danger areas. Do not enter unless you have specific authorization.

Always walk in the designated pedestrian lane. If your factory has overhead mirrors at intersections, use them — look before you cross any aisle.

Yellow/black stripesCaution zone / shared areaStop, check, then proceed carefully

3. Blind Spots and Intersections: The Most Dangerous Moments

Forklift accidents most often happen at intersections inside the factory, where the driver cannot see around a rack or wall. In Japan, many factories require forklift drivers to sound the horn — kuraxon (クラクション) — before entering every intersection. As a pedestrian, you must also play your part.

When approaching any factory intersection:

  1. Slow down and stop completely before the line.
  2. Look left and right using the overhead mirror if available.
  3. Listen for horn sounds or engine noise.
  4. Make eye contact with the driver if you see a forklift.
  5. Only cross when you are certain the path is clear.
Warning Never assume a forklift driver can see you. Even if you can see the forklift, the driver may not see you — especially if you are walking close to shelving or pallets. Always wait for a clear signal before crossing.

4. What to Do When a Forklift Is Nearby

When a forklift is operating near you, the safest action is to stop moving and wait. Do not try to rush past it, and do not walk directly behind a reversing forklift. Reversing is when drivers have the least visibility, and the backup alarm — called a bakkuon (バックオン) or keitai onkyō keikōki — should prompt you to immediately step back to a safe zone.

Key behaviors to follow:

  • Stop and stand still when a forklift is passing nearby.
  • Never walk behind a reversing forklift, even slowly.
  • Never try to overtake a slow-moving forklift in an aisle.
  • Do not use your smartphone or wear earphones while near active forklift zones.
  • If a forklift is carrying a raised load, stay well clear — loads can fall.

5. PPE and Visibility: Making Sure Drivers Can See You

In many Japanese factories, wearing a anzen chokki (安全チョッキ / safety vest) is mandatory in areas where forklifts operate. These high-visibility vests make it easier for drivers to spot you, especially in low-light warehouses or when you are moving quickly.

Standard PPE (Personal Protective Equipment / hogo gu 保護具) in forklift zones may include:

Tip If your safety vest is damaged or faded, report it to your hanchō and request a replacement immediately. A vest that is hard to see defeats its purpose — and in a forklift zone, your visibility is your protection.

6. Reporting Near Misses: Why It Matters in Japan

In Japanese workplace culture, reporting a hiyari hatto (ヒヤリ・ハット) — a near miss or close call — is considered a responsibility, not an admission of fault. The term comes from the feeling of almost being hit: hiyari (a cold chill of fear) and hatto (a sudden gasp). These reports help the factory identify dangerous patterns before someone gets seriously hurt.

If you almost have an accident with a forklift:

  • Report it to your hanchō the same day.
  • Fill in any required hiyari hatto repōto (ヒヤリ・ハットレポート) form.
  • Be specific about where and when it happened.
  • Do not feel embarrassed — these reports are taken seriously and valued.
Warning Staying silent after a near miss does nothing to protect you or your coworkers. If the same risky situation repeats, the next outcome could be far worse. Speak up — it is the right thing to do.

Workers new to Japanese factories sometimes unknowingly break forklift safety rules because they come from environments with different practices. Here are the most common mistakes and how to avoid them:

Understanding these risks and avoiding these habits will mark you as a reliable, safety-conscious worker — exactly the kind of person that Japanese factories want to keep and promote.
