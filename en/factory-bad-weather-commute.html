What to Do When Bad Weather Affects Your Factory Commute in Japan

Last updated: 2026-04-13  |  6 min read

Bad weather can make a normal factory commute in Japan slow, stressful, and sometimes dangerous. The safest choice is not to guess—check the latest transport and weather updates, then contact your workplace early if you may be late or unable to arrive.

1. Start by checking the real risk, not just the forecast

When the sky looks bad, many workers feel pressure to leave home as usual and “see what happens.” That is risky. In Japan, heavy rain, typhoons, snow, strong winds, and lightning can all affect trains, buses, bicycles, and walking routes. The first step is to check reliable information from the Japan Meteorological Agency (気象庁 / kishōchō), your train operator, and your company notices. A simple weather app is not enough if your route may be flooded or a train line may be suspended.

For factory workers, commuting problems matter because late arrivals can affect shift starts, machine handovers, and safety checks. If you are already tired, wet, or stressed before work begins, mistakes become more likely. Make the decision early: if conditions look unsafe, do not wait until you are already outside.

Tip Save the official weather page, train app, and your company contact number on your phone before the rainy season or winter arrives.

2. Know which weather situations are most likely to cause delays

Not every rainy day causes trouble, but some conditions are especially disruptive. Typhoons often bring suspended train service, road closures, and very strong gusts. Heavy snow can slow buses, make platforms slippery, and cause local line delays. Flooding may block sidewalks and underpasses, while lightning can make outdoor travel dangerous. Even if your own neighborhood seems fine, another part of your route may be affected.

In factory jobs, shift timing is often strict. That means a one-hour delay may create a bigger issue than it would in a flexible office job. If the weather warning level is rising, think about the whole journey: home to station, station to workplace, and the return trip after your shift ends. Weather can also change during the day, so do not only check the morning forecast and assume everything will stay the same.

TyphoonTrain suspensions, bus cancellations, early workplace closure

3. Contact your workplace before the shift starts

If you think you may arrive late, contact your workplace as early as possible. Do not wait until the scheduled start time. In many factories, the supervisor, shift leader, or hanchō (班長 / hanchō) needs time to adjust assignments, cover the line, or confirm whether overtime or safety checks can still happen as planned. A short, clear message is best.

You do not need a long explanation. Say what is happening, where you are, and whether you expect a delay. If your train is stopped, mention the line name. If roads are blocked or your bicycle is unsafe, say that too. If your Japanese is limited, use simple language and the key facts. If your workplace uses a company chat app, follow that method. If they prefer a phone call, call instead of texting.

Warning Do not simply disappear or arrive late without reporting it. In a factory, unexplained absence can be treated as a serious attendance problem and may damage trust with your supervisor.

4. Use a simple message format that your supervisor can understand quickly

During bad weather, the person receiving your message may also be dealing with many workers reporting delays. Keep it short, polite, and direct. The goal is not perfect grammar; the goal is clear information. A useful structure is: who you are, what is happening, how late you expect to be, and when you will update them again if needed.

Example: “Good morning. This is Maria from Line 2. The train on the XYZ Line has stopped because of heavy rain. I will be late by about 30 minutes. I will contact you again when I get a new update.”

If you are not sure how long the delay will last, say that honestly. Do not promise an arrival time unless you have checked live transport updates. If you may not be able to come in at all, say that clearly so the workplace can prepare. This is especially important for night shifts, early-morning shifts, and jobs with limited staff.

5. Decide whether you should wait, reroute, or stay home

Sometimes the best option is to wait a little and travel later. In other cases, a different train line, bus, or station entrance may be safer. But there are also times when traveling at all is a bad idea. If your route has floodwater, black ice, fallen objects, or official transport suspensions, do not force the commute just to prove effort. Safety comes first.

If your company has a policy for delayed arrival, follow it. Some workplaces allow staff to come in after transport restarts. Others ask workers to stay home until conditions improve. A few factories may ask workers to use paid leave, special weather leave, or a substitute shift. Because rules differ, ask your supervisor in advance what happens during severe weather. That way, you will not need to guess in the moment.

Tip If your factory is in an industrial area with poor public transport, keep one backup route in mind, such as a different station, a shuttle bus, or a walking route that avoids flood-prone underpasses.

6. Protect yourself on the way to work

If you must travel in bad weather, slow down and reduce risk. Wear shoes with good grip, keep both hands free when possible, and avoid using an umbrella in strong wind if it makes walking unstable. If you cycle, consider whether biking is safe at all. Wet roads, reduced visibility, and gusts can turn a short trip into a dangerous one. If you walk through low areas, be alert for water accumulation and open drainage covers.

Bring basic items that help in emergencies: a compact raincoat, towel, charged phone, portable charger, and a small waterproof bag for documents. If your factory requires a locker key, ID card, or timecard, protect those items from rain. A wet phone can make it hard to update your workplace or check train information. If conditions worsen while you are already on the road, stop and reassess instead of pushing forward blindly.

7. Prevent attendance and pay problems after the weather improves

Once you arrive, confirm how your lateness will be recorded. Some workplaces count weather-related delay separately from ordinary tardiness, while others do not. Ask whether you need to submit proof, such as a train delay certificate or an official transport notice. Keep screenshots of rail alerts and weather warnings in case your supervisor asks later.

It is also smart to ask how bad-weather absences affect pay, shift bonuses, and attendance records. In Japan, factory attendance can influence trust and future scheduling. If the company has a clear policy, learn it before the next storm. If the policy is unclear, ask politely during a calm day rather than during an emergency. The more you understand now, the less stress you will feel later.

Warning Never assume that “weather is a good excuse” means you can skip reporting. Even when the cause is natural, unreported absence can still lead to disciplinary trouble or loss of shifts.

8. Build a bad-weather routine before the next storm

The easiest way to handle commute problems is to prepare in advance. Check your route before severe weather season. Save emergency contacts. Learn your company’s rule for late arrivals, absences, and shift changes. If you work early mornings or late nights, think about what happens when trains stop running after you leave home or before you return. Preparation turns a stressful emergency into a manageable inconvenience.

It also helps to talk with co-workers who live nearby. They may know which routes flood first, which bus is reliable, or which station exit stays safest during heavy rain. Over time, you will build a practical commuting plan that fits your area and your factory schedule. Bad weather is never convenient, but with early checks, quick communication, and basic preparation, you can avoid unnecessary panic and protect both your safety and your job record.
