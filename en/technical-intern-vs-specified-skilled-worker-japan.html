Technical Intern Trainee vs Specified Skilled Worker in Japan:
What's the Difference?

Last updated: April 2026  |  8 min read

You've heard both terms. 技能実習生 (gino jisshūsei / Technical Intern Trainee) and 特定技能 (tokutei ginō / Specified Skilled Worker). Maybe you're on one of these visas right now, or you're back home trying to decide which path to take to Japan. Either way, understanding the difference is not just useful — it directly affects your rights, your pay, and how long you can stay.

These two programs sound similar on the surface, but they work very differently. This article breaks down both side by side, covers how to move from one to the other, and gives you an honest assessment of which is better for factory workers.

1. The Technical Intern Training Program (TITP) — 技能実習制度

The Technical Intern Training Program, known in Japanese as 技能実習制度 (gino jisshū seido), was officially created to transfer Japanese technology and skills to workers from developing countries. In practice, it became one of Japan's main pipelines for bringing in low-wage factory labor.

The program has three visa categories:

  • 技能実習1号 (1-gō): First year. You are learning basic skills under supervision. Most workers arrive on this status.
  • 技能実習2号 (2-gō): Years 2–3. You've passed a basic skills test and continue with the same employer in the same job type.
  • 技能実習3号 (3-gō): Years 4–5. Available only in certain industries and requires a higher-level skills test. You may be able to change to a different employer within the same job type.

The total maximum duration under TITP is 5 years. After that, you must leave Japan or transition to a different visa status.

One of the defining features of TITP is the role of the 監理団体 (kanri dantai / supervising organization). This is a non-profit body that acts as the intermediary between your employer in Japan and the sending organization in your home country. They are legally responsible for monitoring your working conditions. In reality, the quality of this oversight varies enormously — some kanri dantai are diligent, others are not.

A critical restriction under TITP: you cannot freely change employers. You are tied to your designated employer and your assigned job type. If you leave, your visa status is at risk. This is one of the most criticized aspects of the program.

Tip If you are a TITP trainee and your kanri dantai is not responding to your concerns, you can contact the Organization for Technical Intern Training (OTIT) — 外国人技能実習機構 (Gaikokujin Gino Jisshu Kiko) — directly. They have a multilingual hotline and are responsible for oversight of the entire program.

2. Specified Skilled Worker — 特定技能

The Specified Skilled Worker (SSW) program, 特定技能 (tokutei ginō), was introduced in 2019 to address Japan's severe labor shortage more directly. Unlike TITP, SSW is designed from the start as a work visa — not a training program.

There are two levels:

  • 特定技能1号 (1-gō): Up to 5 years total. Covers 12 eligible industries, including manufacturing (素形材・産業機械・電気電子情報関連製造業 — foundry, industrial machinery, and electronics manufacturing). Family members generally cannot accompany you to Japan on this status.
  • 特定技能2号 (2-gō): Indefinitely renewable. As of the 2023 expansion, this now applies to most of the same 12 industries. Family members can accompany you, and this status can eventually lead to permanent residency.

The key practical differences with SSW:

  • You can change employers within the same industry without losing your visa status, as long as you follow the proper procedure.
  • There is no supervising organization required for SSW1 (though registered support organizations — 登録支援機関, tōroku shien kikan — may assist you).
  • Your wages must be equivalent to those paid to Japanese workers in the same role. There is no legal two-tier system.

4. Rights and Protections

Both TITP and SSW workers are covered by Japanese labor law — the Labor Standards Act (労働基準法, Rōdō Kijun Hō) applies to both. This means minimum wage, overtime pay, and rest day requirements apply to you regardless of your visa category.

In practice, however, enforcement has been very different. The TITP program has a well-documented history of abuse: passport confiscation, unpaid wages, forced labor conditions, and workers unable to leave bad employers due to the visa restrictions. The introduction of OTIT in 2017 improved oversight, but problems persist.

SSW workers have more autonomy. Because they can change employers, they have a way out of bad working conditions that TITP workers often do not. This is a meaningful difference in day-to-day reality.

Both visa holders have the right to:

  • Receive the national minimum wage or higher
  • Access workers' compensation insurance (労災保険, rōsai hoken)
  • Be enrolled in social insurance (health insurance and pension)
  • Report labor violations to the Labor Standards Inspection Office (労働基準監督署, rōdō kijun kantoku sho)
Warning If you are a technical intern and your supervisor or kanri dantai is withholding your passport, forcing you into work outside your designated training plan, or threatening you if you try to leave — contact OTIT (外国人技能実習機構) immediately at their multilingual support line: 0120-250-168 (toll-free in Japan). These are serious legal violations. You have the right to report them without fear of deportation.

5. How to Move from TITP to SSW

This is one of the most common questions among technical interns — and the good news is that there is a clear pathway.

Option A — Complete TITP 2-gō or 3-gō: If you successfully complete the Technical Intern Training 2-gō (or 3-gō), you are eligible to apply for SSW1 without taking the skills evaluation exam. Completion of 2-gō in the relevant job type is treated as proof of ability. This is the most common route for factory workers.

Option B — Pass the Skills Evaluation Test: Even if you are still on TITP or are outside Japan, you can take the 技能評価試験 (ginō hyōka shiken / skills evaluation test) for your target industry. If you pass this plus the Japanese language test (日本語能力試験 N4 level or equivalent), you qualify for SSW1. The tests are held in Japan and in several sending countries.

Application process: Once you meet the eligibility requirements, your employer (or a registered support organization) will help you file a status-of-residence change application at the nearest immigration bureau. Processing typically takes 1–3 months. Plan ahead — do not let your current visa expire while waiting.

Important note: you cannot convert from TITP to SSW while your training is still in progress. You must complete the program first, or your employer and kanri dantai must agree to an early termination under specific conditions.

6. Which Is Better for Factory Workers?

The honest answer: SSW gives you more freedom, and for most workers who have the option, it is the better status to aim for.

That said, TITP may be your entry point into Japan — particularly if you are coming from a country with an established sending organization network (Vietnam, Philippines, Indonesia, Myanmar, etc.). Many workers arrive on TITP and then transition to SSW after their first few years.

If your goal is to build a long-term career in Japan — including eventually bringing your family and applying for permanent residency — then SSW2 is the path you want to be on. SSW1 can lead to SSW2 with a higher-level skills test, and SSW2 is indefinitely renewable with a route toward permanent residency after 10 years of total residence.

If you are currently on TITP and your working conditions are reasonable, focus on completing 2-gō cleanly and then applying for SSW1. That transition is your single most important career move in Japan.

Warning Do NOT change employers or take up side work without a proper visa status change. Working for an unauthorized employer — even temporarily, even for just a few hours — is a serious immigration violation. It can result in detention, deportation, and a ban from re-entering Japan for up to 10 years. Always check with your kanri dantai or a registered support organization before making any changes.

Summary

TITP and SSW are both pathways into Japan's manufacturing sector, but they work very differently. TITP is structured as a training program with strict employer ties and a supervising organization; SSW is a straightforward work visa with more freedom to change employers and a clearer route to long-term residency.

Know your current status. Know what your rights are under that status. And if your goal is to stay in Japan and build a stable life, start planning your path to SSW2 from day one. The workers who do best in Japan are not always the fastest or the strongest — they are the ones who understand the system and navigate it carefully.
