Important Questions to Ask Before Accepting a Factory Job in Japan

Last updated: 2026-04-11  |  6 min read

Getting a factory job offer in Japan is exciting — but saying yes too quickly can lead to regret. Before you sign anything or commit, there are important questions you must ask your employer or staffing agency.

1. Why You Must Ask Questions Before Accepting

Many foreign workers arrive at a Japanese factory only to discover that the job is completely different from what they were told. The salary is lower than expected, the dormitory costs more than they imagined, or the shifts are far more demanding. These surprises are often the result of not asking enough questions before accepting the job offer.

In Japan, once you sign a contract and start working, it can be very difficult to change the terms. Some workers feel pressure to accept whatever is offered because they need the income. But spending 10–20 minutes asking the right questions upfront can save you months of frustration.

This guide covers the most important questions to ask your employer or staffing agency (haken gaisha / 派遣会社) before you commit to a factory job in Japan.

Tip Always ask for answers in writing — by email or a formal offer letter. Verbal promises are hard to prove if a dispute comes up later.

2. Questions About Pay and Overtime

Money is the reason most people take a factory job, so this is where you need the clearest answers. Do not assume anything — even small details matter.

Question to AskWhy It Matters
What is my hourly wage (jikkyū / 時給)?Confirms your base rate before any deductions
Is overtime (zangyō / 残業) paid at a higher rate?By law, overtime over 8 hours/day must be paid at 125% or more
Are there night shift premiums (yakin teate / 夜勤手当)?Night work typically pays an extra 25% — confirm if this applies
How often is payday (kyūryō bi / 給料日)?Monthly pay is common; some companies pay twice a month
How will I receive my pay — bank transfer or cash?Bank transfers are standard; you may need a Japanese bank account
Are there any pay deductions I should know about?Social insurance, dormitory fees, and meal costs are often deducted

It is completely normal to ask these questions. A legitimate employer or agency will answer them clearly. If someone avoids your questions or gives vague answers about pay, treat that as a warning sign.

3. Questions About Your Work Schedule

Factory work in Japan often involves shift systems (kōtai kinmu / 交替勤務). Understanding your schedule before you start is essential for planning your life and rest.

  • What are the working hours? Ask for the exact start and end times, not just a general range.
  • Is it a day shift, night shift, or rotating shift? Rotating shifts (two-shift or three-shift) change every few weeks and can be hard on your body and social life.
  • How many days per week will I work? Confirm whether weekends are included.
  • How much notice will I get before the schedule changes? Some factories post schedules only a few days in advance.
  • Is there mandatory overtime? Some factories require you to stay late on short notice.
Warning Be cautious if a company promises high earnings but cannot tell you clearly how many hours you will actually work. Vague answers about hours often mean excessive mandatory overtime with little personal time.

4. Questions About Housing and Transportation

If the factory provides a dormitory (ryō / 寮) or company housing, this can be very convenient — but it comes at a cost. Make sure you know exactly what you are paying for.

QuestionWhat to Clarify
Is housing provided? Is it mandatory?Some companies require you to live in company housing
How much is deducted for housing per month?Fees vary widely — from ¥10,000 to over ¥50,000/month
Is the dormitory shared or private room?Shared rooms are cheaper but less private
What utilities are included?Electricity, water, and internet may or may not be included
How far is the dormitory from the factory?Some dorms are very remote with limited access to shops or public transport
Is transportation provided, or do I commute on my own?Some factories offer shuttle buses (kōjō basu / 工場バス)

Also ask whether you can leave the company housing freely if you want to rent your own apartment later. Some contracts tie housing to employment, meaning if you quit the job, you must also leave the housing immediately.

5. Questions About Your Contract and Employment Type

Understanding your employment status (koyō keitai / 雇用形態) is critical in Japan. There are several types of employment, and your rights and benefits differ significantly between them.

  • Am I a direct employee (seishain / 正社員) or a dispatch worker (haken / 派遣)? Dispatch workers are employed by the staffing agency, not the factory directly.
  • Is this a fixed-term contract? Ask how long the contract is and whether it will be renewed.
  • What happens if I want to quit? In Japan, a notice period of at least 2 weeks is standard, but contracts may specify longer.
  • Is there a probationary period (shikikan / 試用期間)? During this period, the company may have more flexibility to end your employment.
  • Will I receive a copy of my contract in a language I understand? You have the right to receive your contract in writing.
Tip Ask for a written copy of your contract before your first day of work. Never start a job without receiving your signed contract — this protects both you and the employer.

6. Questions About Benefits and Insurance

Japanese law requires most employers to enroll full-time workers in social insurance (shakai hoken / 社会保険), which includes health insurance (kenkō hoken / 健康保険) and pension (nenkin / 年金). Make sure you know what coverage you will receive.

  • Will I be enrolled in shakai hoken from day one? This covers hospital visits and gives you a pension contribution history.
  • Is there work injury insurance (rōsai hoken / 労災保険)? This is legally required and covers injuries that happen at work.
  • Are there any paid holidays (yūkyū kyūka / 有給休暇)? After 6 months of continuous employment, workers are legally entitled to at least 10 days.
  • Is there a bonus (bōnasu / ボーナス)? Not all factories pay bonuses, especially for dispatch or fixed-term workers.

7. Questions About Rules, Culture, and Daily Life

Factory work in Japan has specific workplace rules and an unspoken culture that can be a shock for new workers. Asking about this upfront helps you prepare mentally.

  • Is there a dress code or uniform? Most factories provide a uniform (sagyōgi / 作業着). Ask if you need to purchase it or if it is provided.
  • Are mobile phones allowed during work hours? Many factories have strict phone restrictions on the production floor.
  • Is Japanese language ability required? Ask what level of Japanese you need and whether there is any language support for foreign workers.
  • Are there any other foreign workers at the factory? Knowing whether there is a support network can make adjustment much easier.
  • What happens if I get sick and cannot come to work? Understand the procedure for reporting absences (kekkin renraku / 欠勤連絡).
Warning If the recruiter or employer seems annoyed or dismissive when you ask about rules, benefits, or daily life, that is a serious red flag. Good employers welcome these questions because informed workers settle in better and stay longer.

8. Red Flags to Watch Out For

Most factories and staffing agencies in Japan are legitimate, but some situations carry real risk — especially for foreign workers who are not yet familiar with their rights.

Red FlagWhat It May Mean
Refuses to give you a written contractUnprotected employment — avoid
Cannot clearly explain how much you will take home each monthHidden deductions or fees
Requires you to hand over your residence card (zairyū kādo / 在留カード) or passportIllegal — employers cannot hold your ID documents
Housing deduction is extremely high relative to salaryWage exploitation through housing costs
Pressures you to sign immediately without time to readPossible predatory contract terms
Promises very high pay but is vague about hours or locationPossible bait-and-switch job offer

If you encounter any of these situations, do not feel obligated to accept. You can contact the Labor Standards Inspection Office (rōdō kijun kantokusho / 労働基準監督署) for free advice, even if you are not yet employed at the company. Many offices have multilingual support or translation services available.
