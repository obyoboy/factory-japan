How to Call in Sick Properly to a Japanese Factory or Dispatch Company

Last updated: 2026-04-05  |  6 min read

If you are too sick to work, calling in the right way matters. In a Japanese factory or dispatch company, a short, clear message can protect your standing and help your team adjust quickly.

1. Why the first message matters

When you cannot go to work because you are sick, the first message you send can shape how smoothly the day goes. In a Japanese factory setting, supervisors and team leaders often need to adjust the line, reassign jobs, or call a replacement quickly. If you wait too long, the impact spreads to your teammates and can create unnecessary trouble.

For workers at a dispatch company, the process can feel even more sensitive because you may need to inform both the dispatch agency and the factory contact. That does not mean you need to overexplain. What matters most is to contact the right person early, say clearly that you are unwell, and give the basic information they need.

Tip Call as soon as you realize you cannot make it in. A short, early message is better than a long explanation sent after your shift has already started.

In Japan, being late with a sick call can be seen as careless, even if your illness is real. Showing responsibility is often more important than giving many details. A calm, direct notice helps people trust you and makes it easier for everyone to respond.

2. Who to contact and in what order

The right contact depends on how you are assigned. If you work directly for a factory, you usually contact your supervisor, shift leader, or hanchō / 班長 first. If you are dispatched, you may need to contact your dispatch coordinator as well as the factory contact, depending on company rules. Some workplaces want you to call only the agency, while others require both.

Check the rule before you need it. Many companies explain the process during orientation, in the work handbook, or in the contact list saved in your phone. If you are unsure, ask while you are healthy so you are not guessing later.

SituationUsually contact
Direct factory employeeSupervisor or hanchō / 班長
Dispatch workerDispatch coordinator first, then factory contact if required
Night shift or early morningThe person listed for absence reporting

Do not assume texting a coworker is enough. A coworker may forget to pass on the message, and that can cause confusion. If your company expects a phone call, make the call. If they allow text or chat, send the message there only if that is the approved method.

3. What to say in a sick call

Your message should be short, respectful, and clear. The basic structure is simple: greet them, say you are unwell, say you cannot work today, and mention whether you can be reached later. If you already know the problem, you can mention it briefly, but do not make the call into a medical report.

Examples in English:

“Good morning. This is [name]. I’m sorry, but I’m sick and cannot come to work today. I would like to report my absence.”

“I have a fever and won’t be able to work my shift today. I apologize for the trouble.”

If you are calling in Japanese, keep it simple and polite. A common pattern is: “おはようございます。○○です。体調が悪く、今日は出勤できません。申し訳ありません。” This is enough in many workplaces. If needed, you can add that you are seeing a doctor or that you will update them later.

Warning Do not disappear without contacting anyone. In many factories, an unexplained absence can be treated as a serious violation and may hurt future shifts or employment status.

Try not to overshare private details. Saying “I have stomach pain” or “I have a fever” is usually enough. You do not need to list every symptom unless your company asks for it.

4. Phone call, text, or app: which is better?

Some workplaces still prefer a phone call because it is immediate and hard to miss. Others accept LINE, SMS, email, or a company app. If your workplace has a preferred channel, follow it exactly. The method matters because the goal is not only to inform someone, but to make sure the information reaches the right person quickly.

A phone call works best when:

- your shift starts soon

- the company needs immediate confirmation

- you have never used the text process before

A text message can work when:

- the company officially allows it

- you are calling early in the morning and the office is not open yet

- your workplace uses chat for daily attendance

If you send a text, keep it short and complete. Include your name, work site if needed, the reason in one line, and whether you will update them later. Example: “Good morning, this is Tanaka at Factory A. I have a fever and cannot work today. I will contact you again this afternoon.”

Never assume that a short message with no details is enough. Silence creates extra work for the team and can make you look unreliable.

5. How much explanation is appropriate

When people are sick, they often feel pressure to explain too much. In a Japanese workplace, simple and polite is usually best. Your manager does not need the full story unless your symptoms affect safety, your return date is unclear, or you may need several days off.

Give enough information for scheduling, but not so much that the conversation becomes awkward. A good rule is: state the fact, the expected absence, and whether you will follow up. If you are likely to return the next day, say so. If you are not sure, say that you will update them after you see a doctor or rest.

If your company asks for a doctor’s note, follow the rule. Some places require documentation for longer absences, especially if you miss multiple days or if the factory operates under strict attendance policies. Keep any documents or photos they request ready and send them promptly.

Be careful about saying you are “just tired” if the real issue is illness. That can make managers think you are not serious. At the same time, do not claim a severe illness if you only have a minor issue. Clear and honest communication is the safest approach.

6. Common phrases you can use

Having a few ready-made phrases makes the call easier, especially if you are nervous or speaking Japanese as a second language. Memorize one short script and adjust it to your workplace.

English:

“Hello, this is [name]. I’m not feeling well and cannot come to work today. I’m sorry for the inconvenience.”

“I have a fever, so I need to take sick leave today.”

“Please let me know if you need any documents.”

Japanese:

“おはようございます。○○です。熱があり、今日はお休みさせてください。ご迷惑をおかけして申し訳ありません。”

“体調不良のため、本日は欠勤します。”

“診断書が必要でしたらお知らせください。”

These phrases are useful because they are respectful without sounding too formal or unnatural. If you are speaking to a section leader or hanchō / 班長, the tone should be polite but direct. If the person sounds busy, do not keep talking longer than needed.

Tip Prepare a sick-call script in your phone notes before you need it. When you are feverish or dizzy, having the words ready saves energy and reduces mistakes.

7. Mistakes that create problems

Several small mistakes can turn a simple sick day into a larger issue. One common mistake is waiting until after the shift starts. Another is texting a coworker instead of the proper contact. A third is giving a vague message like “I can’t make it” without saying why or when you will follow up.

Another problem is changing your story. If you tell one person one thing and another person something different, people may think you are hiding the real reason. If you are sick, keep the explanation consistent and honest.

Be especially careful with repeated absences. Even when every absence is genuine, frequent sick days can lead to questions if you do not communicate well. If you expect a longer recovery, say so early and keep updates coming. That shows responsibility and helps your workplace plan ahead.

Warning Failing to follow the company’s absence rule can lead to discipline, reduced trust, or loss of future shifts. Always check the required contact method and report before your scheduled start time.

Also avoid sounding overly casual. Messages like “I’m not coming in lol” may seem harmless to a friend, but they can be taken badly in a factory or dispatch environment. Keep it professional, even if the channel is LINE or chat.

8. Returning to work after being sick

Your responsibility does not end after the sick call. When you are ready to return, send a short follow-up if your workplace expects one. Thank them for understanding, confirm your first day back, and ask whether you need to bring any paperwork.

A simple message works well: “I’m feeling better and can return to work tomorrow. Thank you for your support.” If the illness affected your ability to eat, sleep, or walk safely, make sure you are fully ready before returning, especially in a factory where safety and speed matter.

If you were absent for more than one day, check whether your supervisor wants a medical certificate or an attendance form. Hand it in on time. Returning responsibly can repair any inconvenience caused by the absence and helps show that you take the job seriously.

In the end, calling in sick properly is about respect, clarity, and timing. You do not need perfect Japanese or a long explanation. You need the right contact, a clear message, and a reliable follow-up. That is usually enough to protect your credibility and keep the workplace running smoothly.
