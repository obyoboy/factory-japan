Useful Japanese Phrases for Factory Workers:
The 50 Words You Need on the Floor

Last updated: March 2026  |  10 min read

You don't need to be fluent in Japanese to work in a Japanese factory. But knowing the right words at the right moment can be the difference between a smooth shift and a serious misunderstanding — or even a safety incident.

This guide covers the 50 most important Japanese phrases you'll actually hear and use on the production floor. Each phrase includes the Japanese writing, the romaji pronunciation, and a plain English explanation of when and how to use it.

How to use this guide Don't try to memorize everything at once. Focus on the first two sections — greetings and safety — before your first day. Then learn the rest gradually as situations come up on the floor.

1. Greetings and Basic Courtesy

These phrases are used every single day. Getting them right shows respect and builds goodwill with your Japanese coworkers and supervisors. In Japan's factory culture, a proper greeting is not just polite — it's expected.

Japanese Romaji Meaning / When to Use
おはようございます Ohayō gozaimasu Good morning — say this when you arrive, to everyone you pass
お疲れ様です Otsukaresama desu "You must be tired" — used mid-shift to acknowledge hard work
お疲れ様でした Otsukaresama deshita End of shift — say this to everyone as they finish or leave
よろしくお願いします Yoroshiku onegaishimasu "Please treat me well" — use when starting something new, meeting someone, or asking for help
ありがとうございます Arigatō gozaimasu Thank you (formal) — use freely; Japanese people appreciate it
すみません Sumimasen Excuse me / Sorry — use to get someone's attention or apologize for a small mistake
申し訳ありません Mōshiwake arimasen I'm very sorry — for more serious apologies to a supervisor
行ってきます / 行ってらっしゃい Ittekimasu / Itterasshai "I'm heading out" / "Take care" — said when leaving the break room or going to another section
Tip Always greet your team leader (班長 / hanchō) first thing in the morning, even before greeting your coworkers. It shows respect for the hierarchy and sets a positive tone for the day.

2. Safety Phrases — Learn These First

Safety is the top priority in any Japanese factory. Knowing these words could prevent an accident — and failing to use them when needed can put you and your coworkers at risk.

Japanese Romaji Meaning / When to Use
危ない！ Abunai! Danger! Watch out! — shout immediately if you see a hazard
止まれ！ Tomare! Stop! — used to stop a machine or person urgently
助けて！ Tasukete! Help me! — emergency only
ケガをしました Kega wo shimashita I got injured — report any injury, no matter how small
体調が悪いです Taichō ga warui desu I don't feel well — tell your hanchō if you feel sick
不良品があります Furyōhin ga arimasu There is a defective item — stop the line and report
機械が止まりました Kikai ga tomarimashita The machine has stopped — do not try to fix it yourself
異音がします Ion ga shimasu There's a strange noise — report unusual machine sounds immediately
Warning Never stay silent about a safety issue because you don't know the Japanese words. Use gestures, point, or even use your phone's translation app. But always communicate — a missed safety report can lead to serious consequences.

These are the terms you'll hear constantly during your shift. Understanding them will help you follow instructions, read signs, and know what's happening around you on the line.

4. Asking Questions and Requesting Help

One of the most common mistakes new foreign workers make is staying silent when they don't understand something. In Japanese factory culture, asking for clarification is not a weakness — struggling silently and making errors is. Use these phrases freely.

Japanese Romaji Meaning / When to Use
もう一度お願いします Mō ichido onegaishimasu Please say that again — use whenever you miss an instruction
ゆっくり話してください Yukkuri hanashite kudasai Please speak slowly
わかりません Wakarimasen I don't understand — honest and necessary
わかりました Wakarimashita I understood / Got it — say this after receiving instructions
教えてもらえますか？ Oshiete moraemasu ka? Could you teach / show me? — use when learning a new task
これでいいですか？ Kore de ii desu ka? Is this okay? / Is this correct? — check your work with a supervisor
手伝ってもらえますか？ Tetsudatte moraemasu ka? Could you help me? — use politely when you need assistance
どこに置けばいいですか？ Doko ni okeba ii desu ka? Where should I put this? — important for 5S compliance
Tip After receiving an instruction, always repeat it back with "Wakarimashita" and a small nod. This confirms you understood and shows attentiveness — it's considered professional behavior in Japan.

5. Reporting and Communication

Japanese factory culture runs on ほうれんそう (hōrensō) — a shorthand for 報告 (hōkoku / reporting), 連絡 (renraku / informing), and 相談 (sōdan / consulting). These three communication habits are expected of every worker, every day.

Japanese Romaji Meaning / When to Use
報告があります Hōkoku ga arimasu I have something to report — use to get your supervisor's attention
終わりました Owarimashita I've finished — always report when a task is complete
確認をお願いします Kakunin wo onegaishimasu Please check / verify — ask supervisor to confirm your work
問題があります Mondai ga arimasu There is a problem — report issues immediately, don't hide them
遅刻します Chikoku shimasu I will be late — call before your shift and say this
休みをください Yasumi wo kudasai Please give me a day off — use when formally requesting leave
残業できますか？ Zangyō dekimasu ka? Are you able to do overtime? — your supervisor may ask you this
残業をお願いできますか？ Zangyō wo onegai dekimasu ka? Could you please do overtime? — a polite request from your supervisor

6. Daily Life at the Factory

These phrases cover the practical day-to-day moments outside of production — the break room, the locker room, and conversations with coworkers.

Japanese Romaji Meaning / When to Use
トイレはどこですか？ Toire wa doko desu ka? Where is the bathroom?
ロッカーはどこですか？ Rokkā wa doko desu ka? Where are the lockers?
食堂はどこですか？ Shokudō wa doko desu ka? Where is the cafeteria / canteen?
これは何ですか？ Kore wa nan desu ka? What is this? — useful when you encounter unfamiliar equipment or forms
サインしてください Sain shite kudasai Please sign here — you may hear this for timesheets or documents
何時に終わりますか？ Nanji ni owarimasu ka? What time do we finish? — use to ask about end of shift time

7. Pronunciation Tips for Non-Japanese Speakers

Japanese pronunciation is actually more consistent than English — once you learn the basic sounds, you can read romaji and be understood. Here are the key points:

  • Vowels: A = "ah", I = "ee", U = "oo", E = "eh", O = "oh" — always short and consistent
  • Long vowels: Ō means you hold the "o" sound longer (e.g., Ohayō = "oh-ha-YOH")
  • R: The Japanese "r" sound is between an English R and L — try saying "L" with the tip of your tongue
  • Double consonants: A double letter (like "tt" in "ittekimasu") means a brief pause before that sound
  • No stress accent: Japanese is fairly flat in pitch — don't emphasize one syllable more than others
Tip Write your most-needed phrases on a small card and keep it in your pocket. On your first few days, it's completely normal to refer to notes — Japanese coworkers will respect the effort you're making to communicate in their language.

Summary: Your Starter Phrase Kit

If you remember nothing else from this article, start with these 10 phrases. They will cover the vast majority of situations on your first week:

1 Ohayō gozaimasu Morning greeting to everyone
2 Otsukaresama deshita End of shift / farewell
3 Sumimasen Excuse me / sorry
4 Wakarimashita I understood
5 Wakarimasen I don't understand

Learning even a handful of Japanese phrases shows your colleagues that you respect the culture and are making an effort. In a Japanese factory, that attitude matters — and it will be noticed. Start small, use what you know, and build from there. You'll be surprised how quickly things get easier.
