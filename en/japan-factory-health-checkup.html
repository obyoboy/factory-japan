Annual Health Checkups at Japanese Factories: What Foreign Workers Need to Know

Last updated: April 2026  |  7 min read

One morning your 班長 (hanchō / team leader) hands you a slip of paper saying you have a health check next Tuesday. You're not sure what to expect — will it hurt? Do you need to fast? What happens if something is wrong? And can your employer use the results against you? This guide walks you through Japan's annual factory health checkup from start to finish.

1. What Is the 定期健康診断?

定期健康診断 (teiki kenkō shindan / periodic health examination) is a legally mandated annual health check for all workers in Japan. Under the 労働安全衛生法 (rōdō anzen eisei hō / Industrial Safety and Health Act), every employer must provide this examination to full-time workers and most part-time workers at least once per year — at the employer's expense. You do not pay anything out of pocket.

This applies to all workers regardless of nationality or visa status. If your company is not offering annual health checks, they are breaking the law.

2. What Tests Are Included

The standard health check covers 11 items required by law. Most factory checkups are done at a designated clinic or mobile health van that comes to the factory. The whole process usually takes 30–60 minutes.

3. Is It Mandatory? Can You Refuse?

Employers are legally required to provide the checkup. Workers are strongly expected to attend — and most employment contracts include language obligating participation. Refusing without a good reason can be treated as a violation of your employment contract.

However, you cannot be forced to undergo specific procedures against your will. If there is a specific test you object to for medical or religious reasons, speak with HR or the 産業医 (san'i / occupational physician) before the checkup date.

If you cannot attend on the scheduled date, ask HR to reschedule. The company must provide a reasonable opportunity to take the checkup within the year.

Tip Some tests (blood test, certain metabolic checks) require fasting for 10 hours beforehand. Check your notification slip for fasting instructions. If there are no instructions in a language you understand, ask HR before the checkup day — not the morning of.

4. Understanding Your Results

Results are usually returned within 2–4 weeks, either as a paper report or via the company's health management system. Japanese health check results typically use a letter-grade system:

  • A — Normal. No concerns.
  • B — Slight abnormality. Within acceptable range. Monitor.
  • C — Requires observation or lifestyle improvement. Consider seeing a doctor.
  • D — Requires medical treatment. See a doctor.
  • E — Currently under treatment. Continue as directed.

Most people receive a mix of A and B grades. Getting a C or D in one area doesn't mean you're in danger — it means that item needs attention. The results sheet will specify which tests came back flagged and with what recommendation.

5. What Happens If Results Flag Something Serious

If a significant concern is found, the 産業医 (san'i / occupational physician — the company's workplace health doctor) may call you in for a follow-up consultation. This is confidential. The san'i may recommend:

  • Visiting a specialist at your own expense (your kenkō hoken / health insurance covers this)
  • Temporary reassignment to lighter duties if your condition affects work safety
  • Modified working hours to reduce strain

Your personal health results are private. The company receives aggregate data and knows whether you attended, but your specific results should not be shared with your supervisor without your consent.

6. Special Health Checks for Hazardous Work

Workers exposed to specific hazards at the factory are entitled to additional 特殊健康診断 (tokushu kenkō shindan / special health examinations) — conducted more frequently and covering hazard-specific tests:

  • Noise exposure (騒音 / sōon): extended audiometry
  • Chemical exposure (有機溶剤/yuki yōzai = organic solvents; 特定化学物質/tokutei kagaku busshitsu = specific chemicals): blood and urine analysis for chemical metabolites
  • Vibration tools (振動工具 / shindō kōgu): circulation and nerve tests for hands
  • Radiation: blood count monitoring

If you work in any of these areas, these additional checks are required by law at the company's expense — often every 6 months rather than annually.

Warning Do not hide a health problem to avoid work restrictions. If your condition worsens and it's later discovered you concealed it from the occupational physician, it can complicate rōsai (workers' compensation) claims and may create grounds for disciplinary action. The checkup system is designed to protect you — use it honestly.

7. Summary: The Health Check Is for You

Japan's annual factory health checkup is one of the better worker protections in the system. It costs you nothing, catches problems early, and gives you documentation of your health at each point during your employment. Attend every year, understand your results, and follow up with a doctor if anything is flagged D or E. Your health is the most important tool you bring to work every day — take care of it.
