What a Dispatch Company Actually Does in Japanese Factory Work

Last updated: 2026-04-26  |  6 min read

If you work through a dispatch company in a Japanese factory, the factory is not your direct employer. The dispatch company handles your contract, pay, and many work-related procedures, while the factory supervises your daily tasks on site.

1. The basic structure: who hires you and who supervises you

In Japanese factory work, a dispatch company (haken kaisha / 派遣会社) is the company that employs you on paper. The factory where you actually work is usually the client company, not your legal employer. This split is the most important thing to understand before you start.

The dispatch company prepares your employment contract, pays your wages, and manages your employment records. The factory decides your daily assignments, machine line duties, break timing, and on-site supervision. In practice, you report to the factory floor leader or team leader for work instructions, but your employment conditions are handled by the dispatch company.

This setup is common in Japanese manufacturing because factories need flexible staffing for busy seasons, product changes, and shift coverage. For workers, it can be a practical way to enter factory work quickly, but it also means you need to know exactly which company handles each issue.

Tip Before your first day, ask the dispatch company: “Who is my direct contact for pay, schedule changes, attendance problems, and resigning?” Write down the name, phone number, and office hours.

2. What the dispatch company handles for you

A good dispatch company does more than just send you to a factory. It should explain the work location, shift system, basic rules, and expected tasks before you start. It also helps with documents, insurance enrollment, pay slips, and sometimes housing support or transportation details.

Many workers first meet the dispatch company during registration or interview. From there, the company matches you with a factory that fits your skills, language level, and availability. If the job involves night shifts, heavy lifting, or machine operation, the dispatch company should explain those conditions clearly in advance.

During employment, the dispatch company remains your official point of contact for payroll questions, contract renewals, overtime pay issues, leave requests, and some workplace complaints. If the factory changes your tasks beyond what was agreed, you should tell the dispatch company immediately.

3. What the factory handles on the worksite

The factory is responsible for the actual production environment. That means the work line, machines, tools, product standards, shift allocation, and immediate safety instructions are managed on site. If you need to know how to pack a product, adjust a line process, or respond to a machine alarm, the factory supervisor will usually give that instruction.

Even though the factory supervises your daily work, it does not replace the dispatch company in legal matters. For example, if there is a contract dispute, late pay, or a request to change your employment conditions, the factory cannot usually solve it alone. The dispatch company must handle those issues.

Workers sometimes assume the factory can approve everything, but that can cause problems. If you ask the factory for a schedule change and do not tell the dispatch company, your record may not be updated correctly. Always keep both sides informed when the matter affects your employment.

Warning Do not treat the factory supervisor as your employer for contract changes, resignation procedures, or pay disputes. If you skip the dispatch company, your request may be ignored or handled incorrectly.

4. Why factories use dispatch workers

Factories use dispatch companies because manufacturing demand changes often. Orders can rise and fall quickly, and some production lines need extra hands for only a limited time. Dispatch staffing helps factories respond without hiring everyone as full-time employees.

This arrangement can also help factories cover absences, fill night shifts, and support seasonal peaks such as year-end production or new product launches. For the factory, it is a flexible labor solution. For workers, it can mean faster placement and more job openings, especially if you are looking for work without long hiring delays.

However, flexibility cuts both ways. Because dispatch arrangements are designed for staffing needs, your placement can end when the project or contract ends. That is why it is important to confirm the expected contract length, renewal conditions, and whether the company has other factory placements if the assignment finishes.

5. Common misunderstandings workers should avoid

One common misunderstanding is thinking that the dispatch company can solve every workplace problem immediately. In reality, some issues must go through the factory first, such as a broken machine, a line mistake, or a safety hazard on the floor. Other issues, such as wages or contract terms, must go through the dispatch company.

Another misunderstanding is assuming all dispatch jobs are the same. Some placements are short-term, while others are long-term. Some include overtime and night shifts, while others are daytime only. Some factories provide uniforms, meals, or dormitories; others do not. You need to confirm the exact conditions of your assignment, not just the job title.

Workers also sometimes believe they can be moved to any department without notice. That is not always true. If the change is significant, the dispatch company should explain it and make sure it matches your contract. Do not accept major job changes blindly.

6. What to confirm before you start

Before your first day, ask clear questions. Confirm the factory name, work address, shift schedule, pay date, overtime rules, break time, and whether transportation or housing is included. Also confirm who you should contact if you are sick, late, or injured.

If you are not comfortable reading Japanese documents, ask for an explanation in simple language. A reliable dispatch company should be able to explain the essentials without pressure. You should never sign something you do not understand, especially if it concerns wages, penalties, or contract length.

It is also smart to ask how attendance is reported. Some factories want a direct phone call, while others require a message to both the site and the dispatch company. A missed attendance notice can lead to confusion, so learn the exact procedure on day one.

Tip Keep a small note in your phone with your dispatch company contact, factory address, shift hours, and emergency phone numbers. In factory work, quick communication prevents many problems.

7. Your rights and the limits of the role

The dispatch company is not just a middleman that sends you to work. It has responsibilities under the employment contract, labor insurance rules, and safety obligations. If you are injured, not paid correctly, or treated unfairly, the dispatch company should investigate and take action.

At the same time, the dispatch company has limits. It cannot personally run the factory floor, change production rules, or override site safety instructions. That is why workers need to understand the chain of responsibility. The dispatch company handles employment, while the factory handles production.

If something feels wrong, report it early. Waiting too long can make it harder to fix. This is especially true for attendance problems, harassment, unsafe work, or unexplained schedule changes. Clear communication protects both your job and your record.

8. The practical takeaway for factory workers

A dispatch company in Japanese factory work is your legal employer, your payroll contact, and your support window for contract matters. The factory is your day-to-day workplace, where you receive instructions and do the actual job. Once you understand that split, many confusing situations become easier to handle.

Before starting, confirm the basics in writing, keep your contact list updated, and do not guess when a problem appears. If you know which company is responsible for which issue, you can work more safely and avoid misunderstandings. That is the key to using dispatch work well in Japan.
