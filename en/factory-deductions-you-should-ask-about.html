Salary Deductions Foreign Factory Workers Should Ask About in Japan

Last updated: 2026-04-06  |  7 min read

When you get your first paycheck in a Japanese factory, the total can be lower than you expected. That does not always mean something is wrong, but it does mean you should check every deduction carefully.

1. Why your paycheck is smaller than your gross salary

Many foreign factory workers in Japan see a payslip that looks very different from the monthly salary they were promised. The number at the top is usually your gross pay, but the amount deposited into your bank account is your net pay after deductions. Some deductions are required by law, while others depend on your company, dorm, or contract. If you do not understand them, it is easy to assume the company is taking money unfairly.

The key is to separate legal deductions from work-related charges. Taxes and social insurance are normal. Rent, utilities, food, tools, or cleaning fees may also be normal if they were explained clearly before you started. Problems happen when the company adds items that were never discussed or cannot show you a written rule for them.

Tip Ask for a sample payslip before your first payday. If possible, request a written list of all possible deductions in simple Japanese and English.

2. The deductions that are usually mandatory

In Japan, certain deductions are standard for most employees. These often include income tax, residence tax, health insurance, pension contributions, unemployment insurance, and long-term care insurance if you are eligible by age. These are not factory-specific; they apply across many jobs. The exact amount depends on your salary, age, family status, and whether you are enrolled in company insurance or the national system.

You should still check the numbers. A deduction can be legal and still be incorrect if the company enters the wrong salary amount, uses the wrong insurance category, or continues deducting after your status changes. If you transfer departments, take unpaid leave, or leave the company, the amount may change. Keep copies of every payslip so you can compare month to month.

DeductionWhat to check
Income taxWhether the amount matches your monthly pay and declared dependents
Health insuranceWhether you are enrolled in company insurance or the national system
PensionWhether you are paying correctly and whether exemptions apply
Unemployment insuranceWhether the rate is applied to the right salary base

3. Dorm, rent, and utility charges

Many factory jobs for foreign workers include company housing or a dormitory. This can be helpful because it reduces the stress of finding housing in Japan, but it also creates a common source of confusion. The company may deduct rent, cleaning fees, maintenance fees, water, gas, electricity, or internet charges directly from your salary. Some places also charge a deposit, key money, or move-in fee at the beginning.

Ask whether the dorm fee is fixed or changes with occupancy, season, or utility use. Also ask whether the room is shared or private, because the price should match the arrangement. If the company says utilities are included, confirm what that means. Sometimes only one utility is included, or there is a usage cap. If you use air conditioning heavily in summer or winter, the bill can be much higher than expected.

Warning If housing deductions are not written in your contract or a company rule document, do not accept a vague explanation. Unclear dorm charges can become a dispute when you leave the job or move out.

4. Uniforms, equipment, and training fees

Factories often require uniforms, safety shoes, gloves, masks, or other protective gear. A company may provide these for free, lend them, or deduct part of the cost from your pay. Training time can also be a gray area. If you attend orientation, safety training, or machine training before production starts, ask whether that time is paid.

Uniform deductions are especially important to clarify. If the company says you must pay for a uniform, ask whether the cost is a one-time purchase or a recurring charge. If items are replaced because they wear out during normal work, that is different from replacing lost or damaged items due to your own mistake. You should also ask who pays for laundry, repairs, and special cleaning if the work is dirty or oily.

5. Fines, penalties, and damage charges

Some companies try to deduct money for mistakes, lateness, lost items, or damaged equipment. In Japan, employers cannot freely punish workers by cutting pay without a proper basis. Minor mistakes do not always justify a deduction, and companies usually need a clear rule, written notice, and a lawful method. Even then, automatic penalties can be problematic. This is one area where workers should be extra careful.

If the company claims you caused damage, ask for evidence, photos, and an explanation of how the amount was calculated. A small mistake should not become a large bill without proof. Also ask whether the item was already old, worn, or defective before you used it. If you are asked to sign a document admitting responsibility, do not sign quickly under pressure. Read it first and request time to review it.

Tip If something is deducted as a “penalty,” ask for the rule number, the reason, and the exact calculation in writing before agreeing.

6. How to read your payslip and compare it with your contract

Your payslip should show the breakdown of gross salary, deductions, and net salary. If the document is hard to read, ask someone at the company, a supervisor, or a support person to explain it line by line. Compare the deductions with your contract, work rules, and any housing agreement. The important question is simple: was each deduction explained in advance, and is it being applied exactly as agreed?

It helps to make a checklist. Confirm the salary amount, overtime pay, night shift pay, transport allowance, dorm fee, utilities, and any special charges. Then compare the total to the amount in your bank account. Small errors can happen, but repeated unexplained deductions are a sign that you should ask more questions. If you keep your documents organized, it becomes much easier to prove what was promised.

DocumentWhy it matters
Employment contractShows agreed salary, hours, and major conditions

7. Questions you should ask before signing anything

The best time to ask about deductions is before you sign the contract or move into company housing. Ask what is mandatory, what is optional, and what may change over time. Ask for examples of a normal paycheck, especially during the first month when partial salary, training days, or advance charges may apply. If you are using an interpreter or support staff, make sure the explanation is not rushed.

You should also ask what happens if you resign, are transferred, or miss several workdays. Some deductions are settled at the end of the month, while others appear later. If you owe housing money after leaving, the company may deduct it from your final paycheck. That is exactly why you need written explanations and copies of every agreement from the beginning.

Warning Never sign a payslip correction, debt acknowledgment, or damage statement you do not fully understand. Once you sign, it can be much harder to contest the deduction later.

8. What to do if a deduction looks wrong

If a deduction seems too high or does not match your contract, raise the issue politely but quickly. Start with the payroll office, HR, or your supervisor and ask for a written explanation. Keep your tone calm and specific. For example, ask, “What is this deduction for?” and “Where is this rule written?” rather than saying the company is cheating you immediately. Clear questions often get better answers.

If the company does not respond or the explanation does not make sense, contact your union, technical intern support organization, foreign worker support center, or local labor consultation office. Bring your contract, payslips, and any messages about the deduction. The sooner you act, the easier it is to fix the problem before it becomes a pattern. Knowing your deductions is not just about money; it is also about protecting your rights and staying confident at work.
