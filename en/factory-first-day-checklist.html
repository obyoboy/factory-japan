First-Day Checklist for Foreign Workers Starting at a Japanese Factory

Last updated: 2026-04-04  |  7 min read

Your first day at a Japanese factory can feel overwhelming, especially if you are new to the workplace or new to Japan. A simple checklist helps you arrive prepared, make a good impression, and avoid mistakes that can affect safety or attendance.

1. What to Prepare the Night Before

Your first day starts before you leave home. Lay out everything you need the night before so you are not rushing in the morning. Check your uniform, shoes, gloves, lunch, water bottle, residence card, commuter pass, and any documents your employer asked you to bring. If you need to wear safety equipment, confirm it fits and is clean. Charge your phone, set two alarms, and check the route to the factory. If you are not sure how long the commute takes, add extra time. Being early is better than being exactly on time on your first day.

Tip Put your essentials in one bag the night before: wallet, phone, residence card, pen, notebook, handkerchief, and any required papers. This reduces stress and helps you avoid forgetting something important.

2. Documents and Items to Bring

Factories often ask new workers to bring identification and paperwork on the first day. In many cases, you may need your passport, residence card (zairyū kādo), bank book or account details, My Number information, emergency contact, and documents for taxes or insurance. Some workplaces also ask for a seal (hanko) or a photo for employee records. Keep these items in a clear folder so they do not get damaged. If your supervisor or recruiter gave you a list, follow it exactly. Do not assume the factory will have copies of everything ready for you.

3. What to Wear and How to Show Up

First impressions matter in a Japanese factory. Wear the clothes you were instructed to wear, and make sure they are clean and neat. If you are given a uniform, arrive ready to change quickly. Keep your hair tidy, avoid strong perfume, and remove accessories that could be unsafe around machines. Shoes should meet workplace rules, especially if the factory requires safety shoes. Arrive a little early, then wait calmly until someone tells you what to do. When you enter, greet people clearly with ohayō gozaimasu or the appropriate greeting for the time of day. Even a simple, polite greeting helps people see that you are serious and respectful.

Warning Never wear loose clothing, open sandals, or jewelry that could be caught in equipment. A small fashion choice can become a serious accident in a factory environment.

4. How to Introduce Yourself

On your first day, you will probably meet a supervisor, trainer, or senior coworker. Introduce yourself clearly, speak slowly, and listen carefully. A basic self-introduction in Japanese is enough: say your name, your country, and that you will work hard. If your Japanese is limited, do not panic. A short, polite introduction is better than saying nothing. Smile, make eye contact if comfortable, and bow lightly when appropriate. If you do not understand a name or instruction, ask them to repeat it. It is normal to ask questions on the first day, and it is better to clarify early than make a mistake later.

5. Safety Rules You Must Understand Immediately

Safety comes first in any factory. Before you start work, pay close attention to emergency exits, alarms, restricted areas, and machine guards. Learn where to put your belongings so they do not block walkways. Ask which tasks require gloves, goggles, helmets, ear protection, or masks. If your job involves moving heavy items, learn proper lifting technique. Never turn on a machine unless you are trained and authorized. If you see something strange, stop and report it. Do not assume that a small problem will fix itself. In a factory, fast reporting can prevent injury and avoid production damage.

Tip If you do not understand a safety instruction, repeat it back in your own words or ask for a demonstration. Watching once is not always enough; confirmation helps prevent accidents.

6. Understanding Your Schedule, Breaks, and Attendance

Ask for the work schedule, start time, break times, and end-of-day procedure as soon as possible. In Japanese workplaces, punctuality is taken seriously. If the shift starts at 8:00, you should be ready before 8:00, not walking in at that exact time. Learn how attendance is recorded, how to report lateness, and who to contact if you are sick. Some factories have strict rules about using your phone, leaving your station, or talking during work hours. Make sure you understand the break room rules too. If anything is unclear, ask now. Missing a rule because you were shy is not a good excuse in a factory setting.

7. Mistakes to Avoid on Day One

Many first-day problems come from simple misunderstandings. Do not pretend to understand if you do not. Do not use your phone while someone is giving instructions. Do not leave your station without telling anyone. Do not be afraid to ask where to place your bag, how to use the locker, or when to take off your jacket. Another common mistake is forgetting that Japanese factories may have very specific rules about noise, cleanliness, and behavior. Observe carefully, copy what experienced workers do, and keep your movements organized. The goal on day one is not to work fast. The goal is to learn safely and show that you are reliable.

8. What to Do After Your Shift Ends

When the first day is over, take a few minutes to review what you learned. Check whether you need to return any borrowed items, such as keys, badges, or tools. Confirm the next day’s start time before you leave if it was not already explained. If you received a handbook, read the parts about attendance, safety, and workplace rules as soon as you get home. Send a quick message or note to yourself about names, tasks, and anything you still need to ask. A strong first day does not mean knowing everything. It means ending the day prepared for the next one.

Warning If you feel unwell, injured, confused about a dangerous task, or unable to continue safely, report it immediately. Hiding a problem can lead to injury, discipline, or worse.

Starting work at a Japanese factory is easier when you treat the first day as a learning day. Bring the right items, arrive early, listen carefully, and ask questions when needed. If you stay organized and respectful, you will make a good impression and set yourself up for a smoother first week.
