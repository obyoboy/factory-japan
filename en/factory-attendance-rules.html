Attendance Rules in Japanese Factories

Last updated: 2026-04-04  |  6 min read

Attendance is one of the most important workplace rules in Japanese factories. This guide explains what to do for clock-in, lateness, and absences.

In Japanese factories, attendance is treated as a basic trust rule. Even good work quality can be overshadowed if your time records are inconsistent. This guide explains the practical rules most factories use and what foreign workers should do from day one.

1. Clock In Before Shift Start, Not At Shift Start

Many workers think arriving exactly at start time is enough. In most Japanese factories, you are expected to be changed, prepared, and ready at your station before the shift begins. That means you should clock in early enough to store your belongings and wear required safety gear.

If your commute is long, add a buffer to avoid repeated borderline lateness.

2. Report Lateness and Absence Immediately

If you will be late or absent, contact your supervisor or staffing agency before shift start. Do not wait until after the scheduled time. A short, clear call is usually better than a late text message.

When reporting, include your name, section, expected arrival time (or absence reason), and whether you can work the next shift.

Tip Save emergency contact numbers in your phone and prepare a short Japanese script for absence calls. This reduces stress when problems happen early in the morning.

3. Understand the Difference Between Break Time and Working Time

Clock records are checked against line schedules. Returning late from break can also be treated as attendance trouble, especially if it happens repeatedly. Follow break bells and return to your station before the end signal.

Do not assume small delays are ignored. In many workplaces, every minute is visible in system logs.

4. Overtime Must Match Approval and Records

Overtime is common in many factories, but it must be approved and recorded properly. Do not continue working unofficially after your shift without instruction. Unapproved overtime can create payroll and safety issues.

If your leader asks for overtime, confirm end time and where that time is recorded.

Warning Never ask a coworker to clock in for you. Proxy clock-in is treated as serious misconduct and can lead to immediate contract termination.

5. Correct Time Record Errors the Same Day

Sometimes IC cards, time clocks, or apps fail. If you forgot to clock in or out, report it the same day and request a correction through the official process. Keep your own memo of actual arrival and leaving times for safety.

Fast reporting protects both your salary and your reliability with supervisors.

6. Quick Attendance Checklist

SituationWhat to DoWhen
Normal workdayClock in early and be ready at stationBefore shift starts
Running lateCall supervisor/agency with ETAImmediately
Absent due to illnessReport reason and next-day availabilityBefore shift starts
Forgot clock-outRequest formal correctionSame day
Overtime requestConfirm approval and recorded end timeBefore overtime begins

7. Summary

Good attendance is not only about being present. It is about clear reporting, correct records, and consistent behavior. If you follow these rules, you build trust quickly and avoid avoidable salary or contract problems.
