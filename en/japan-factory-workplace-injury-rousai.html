What to Do If You Get Injured at a Japanese Factory (Rōsai Guide)

Last updated: April 2026  |  8 min read

You're on the line, something goes wrong, and suddenly you're hurt. Maybe it's a cut from a machine edge, a burn from hot material, a fall from a platform, or a strain from lifting the wrong way. In that moment, most foreign workers freeze — not just from pain, but because they have no idea what to do next in a Japanese workplace. This guide walks you through every step, from the factory floor to filing your compensation claim.

1. Immediate Steps Right After the Injury

Your first priority is your own safety and health. Do not try to hide an injury or "push through" to avoid slowing down the line. Here is what to do in the first few minutes:

  • Stop working immediately and move away from the hazard if you can do so safely.
  • Tell your 班長 (hanchō / team leader) or supervisor right away. Even if the injury feels minor, report it verbally on the spot.
  • If you need emergency first aid, the factory's 救護室 (kyūgoshitsu / first aid room) is typically near the main entrance or production floor entrance. Ask: "Kyūgoshitsu wa doko desu ka?" (Where is the first aid room?)
  • For serious injuries — heavy bleeding, broken bones, head trauma, breathing difficulty — have someone call 119 (Japan's ambulance number) immediately.
Tip Even if the pain feels small at the time — say, a wrist strain — always report it the same day. Some injuries (repetitive strain, back injuries) get worse over weeks, and if you did not report it on Day 1, the company may later claim it happened outside work. Reporting on the spot protects you legally.

2. Understanding Rōsai Hoken (労災保険): Japan's Workers' Compensation

Japan has a government-run workers' compensation system called 労災保険 (rōsai hoken / labor accident insurance). It is not optional — by law, every employer in Japan must enroll all workers, including foreigners, in this system. You do not pay any premiums yourself; the employer pays 100% of the cost.

Rōsai covers two types of accidents:

  • 業務災害 (gyōmu saigai) — injuries or illness that happen during work, on company premises, or while doing work-related tasks.
  • 通勤災害 (tsūkin saigai) — injuries that happen during your commute between home and the workplace (using a normal, reasonable route).

This means if you get hurt walking from the company dormitory to the factory gate, or on the bus the company provides, that is also covered.

Warning Some employers — especially smaller haken (dispatch) agencies — may pressure you to use your regular 健康保険 (kenkō hoken / health insurance) instead of filing a rōsai claim. Do not accept this. Using health insurance for a work injury is technically illegal and cheats you out of full compensation. Rōsai pays 100% of medical costs; regular health insurance only covers 70%.

3. The Official Reporting Process

Here is how the formal process works after reporting your injury to your supervisor:

  1. Your supervisor files a 労働者死傷病報告 (rōdōsha shishōbyō hōkoku) — an official accident report — with the Labour Standards Inspection Office (労働基準監督署 / rōdō kijun kantokusho). For injuries requiring 4 or more days off work, this must be submitted. You should receive a copy.
  2. You go to the hospital. When you check in, tell the reception desk you were injured at work and want to use rōsai hoken. Say: "Rōsai wo tsukaitai desu." The hospital will give you a special rōsai form instead of billing your regular insurance.
  3. You fill in a rōsai claim form (様式第5号 / yoshiki daigo-gō for workplace accidents). Your company needs to fill in a section confirming the accident happened at work. If your company is slow or uncooperative, you can submit directly to the Labour Standards Inspection Office — they will investigate and contact the employer.
  4. The inspection office approves the claim and your medical costs, lost wages, and other benefits are paid.

5. Common Mistakes Foreign Workers Make

These mistakes can cost you your legal right to compensation:

  • Not reporting immediately. As mentioned above, always report the same day. A delay creates doubt about whether the injury really happened at work.
  • Using personal health insurance. If the hospital uses your kenkō hoken card for a work injury, you'll need to go through a complicated process to switch it to rōsai later. Avoid this from the start.
  • Accepting informal "sympathy money" from the company. Some supervisors offer cash on the spot to settle things quietly. This is not a substitute for rōsai — once you accept an informal payment and sign anything, you may lose your right to file a proper claim.
  • Missing the statute of limitations. Rōsai claims must be filed within 2 years of the injury (5 years for disability or survivor benefits). Don't wait too long.
  • Not keeping records. Take photos of the injury, the location where it happened, and any equipment involved. Keep copies of all forms you sign.

6. If Your Company Is Uncooperative

In theory, rōsai is straightforward. In practice, some employers — especially dispatch companies — drag their feet on paperwork, pressure workers to stay quiet, or deny that the injury happened at work. You have options:

  • Go directly to the Labour Standards Inspection Office (労基署 / rōkisho). You can file a rōsai claim yourself without your employer's cooperation. The office can compel the employer to respond. To find your nearest office, search for "労働基準監督署 + [your city name]."
  • Contact a labor union (労働組合 / rōdō kumiai). Japan has unions specifically for foreign workers and part-time workers. They can advise you and sometimes negotiate with your employer on your behalf — often for free.
  • Call the Ministry of Health, Labour and Welfare hotline. The Comprehensive Labor Consultation Corner (総合労働相談コーナー) operates at Hello Work offices nationwide and handles foreign workers' questions, often in multiple languages.
Tip Keep a small notebook or use your phone's notes app to record what happened: date, time, location, what you were doing, who was present, and what your supervisor said. This written record is your best protection if a dispute arises later.

7. Injuries vs. Occupational Disease

Rōsai does not only cover sudden accidents. It also covers 職業病 (shokugyōbyō / occupational disease) — health problems that develop gradually because of your work conditions. Examples include:

  • Hearing loss from constant exposure to loud machinery
  • Back or joint damage from repeated heavy lifting
  • Respiratory problems from exposure to chemical fumes or dust
  • Skin conditions from contact with factory chemicals
  • Mental health conditions caused by extreme workplace stress (though these claims are harder to prove)

If you believe your health condition is caused or worsened by your work environment, you can file a rōsai claim even without a single dramatic accident event. Keep records of your symptoms and when they started.

8. Summary: Know Your Rights, Protect Yourself

Getting injured at work is scary enough without having to fight for your legal rights at the same time. The key points to remember: report every injury the same day, use rōsai hoken (not your regular health insurance), get copies of every document you sign, and know that you can file a claim yourself if your employer refuses to help. Japan's workers' compensation system exists to protect everyone who works here — including you. Don't let anyone convince you that the paperwork is too complicated or that it's better to just let it go. Your body and your livelihood are worth protecting.
