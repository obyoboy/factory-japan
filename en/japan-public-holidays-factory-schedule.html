Japan's Public Holidays and How They Affect Your Factory Work Schedule

Last updated: April 2026  |  8 min read

You're trying to plan a trip home to visit your family — or a coworker just told you the factory will be closed for five days straight in May. Why so long? And are you getting paid for those days? Japan has 16 national public holidays and three major shutdown periods that every factory worker needs to understand. Once you know the system, Japan's holiday calendar becomes very predictable, and you can plan your time, travel, and paperwork around it with confidence.

1. Japan's 16 National Public Holidays

Japan's national holidays are set by law under the 国民の祝日に関する法律 (Kokumin no Shukujitsu ni kan suru Hōritsu — Act on National Holidays). There are currently 16 of them spread throughout the year. Factories are not automatically required to be closed on these days — that depends on your company's annual calendar — but most factories do observe the majority of them as days off.

Note that several holidays fall on "Happy Monday" dates — meaning they're always on a Monday to create a three-day weekend. Coming of Age Day, Marine Day, Respect for the Aged Day, and Sports Day all follow this rule. The equinox holidays (春分の日 and 秋分の日) shift slightly each year based on the astronomical calendar.

When a holiday falls on a Sunday, the following Monday becomes a 振替休日 (furikae kyūjitsu — substitute holiday). This is a legal right, not just a convention. If your factory schedules you to work on a substitute holiday without the proper premium pay, that is a labor violation.

2. The Three Major Shutdown Seasons

Beyond individual public holidays, Japanese factories typically observe three extended shutdown periods each year. These are the times when the factory may close for a full week or longer. Understanding each one helps you plan ahead.

お正月 (Osōgatsu — New Year Shutdown): Japan's most culturally important holiday period. Most factories close from around December 29 or 30 through January 3 or 4, giving workers roughly five to seven days off. Some companies extend this to eight or nine days if the calendar falls favorably. New Year is when most Japanese families gather, and public transportation becomes extremely crowded. If you're planning to travel during this period, buy your bus or train tickets as early as possible — often two months in advance.

ゴールデンウィーク (Gōruden Wīku — Golden Week): Japan's biggest holiday cluster, running from April 29 through May 5. Three national holidays fall in quick succession: Shōwa Day (Apr 29), Constitution Memorial Day (May 3), Greenery Day (May 4), and Children's Day (May 5). Many factories tack on the weekend days and bridge days to create a full week of shutdown, typically seven to ten days total. Golden Week is one of the busiest travel periods of the year in Japan — hotels, trains, and highways are packed.

お盆 (Obon — Mid-Summer Shutdown): Obon is a traditional Buddhist observance for ancestors, and while it is not an official national holiday, virtually every factory observes it. The core dates are around August 13–16, but most factories add surrounding days to create a five to ten day break, usually centering on the second or third week of August. This is the most popular time for foreign workers to fly home, so flights book up quickly and prices rise sharply in late July and early August.

3. Holiday Pay Rules

Whether you get paid for a factory shutdown day — and how much — depends on two things: whether the day is a legal holiday or a company-designated holiday, and what your employment contract says.

Japanese labor law distinguishes between two types of rest days:

法定休日 (hōtei kyūjitsu — statutory rest day): A mandatory day of rest required by the Labor Standards Act. Employers must give workers at least one of these per week. If your factory asks you to work on this day, they must pay at least a 35% premium on top of your regular hourly wage.

所定休日 (shotei kyūjitsu — company-designated rest day): Any additional day off that your company has scheduled beyond the legal minimum — such as a second day off per week, or a day off over Golden Week. Working on this day requires a premium of at least 25%.

As for public holidays themselves: Japan's labor law does not automatically require that national holidays be paid days off. Whether you receive holiday pay depends on your individual contract or company rules (就業規則 / shūgyō kisoku). Many full-time employees receive paid public holidays; many dispatch (派遣 / haken) workers do not, unless it is written into their dispatch contract. Read your contract carefully and ask your派遣会社 (haken gaisha — dispatch company) if you are unsure.

4. Understanding Your Company's Annual Calendar

At most Japanese factories, your specific days off for the year — including national holidays, factory-designated shutdowns, and any extra bridge days — are printed in the 年間カレンダー (nenkan karendā — annual calendar). This is usually handed out in late December or early January for the coming year, or posted on a noticeboard in the break room (休憩室 / kyūkei-shitsu).

On the calendar, days printed in red are typically holidays or days off. Days printed in black are working days. Some calendars use a different color or symbol system, so ask your supervisor to explain it if you're not sure.

The nenkan karendā is important because it shows factory-specific days off that don't correspond to national holidays. For example, your factory might close for an extra day in August for equipment maintenance, or add a bridge day in September to extend a three-day weekend to four days. You won't find these on any national holiday list — only on your factory's own calendar.

Tip Ask HR for the 年間カレンダー (nenkan karendā) during your first week. It shows every scheduled day off for the year, including factory-specific days that aren't national holidays. Photograph it and keep it on your phone so you can plan trips, remittances, and appointments well in advance.

5. What Happens During Extended Shutdowns

When the factory closes for Golden Week or New Year, a few practical questions come up for every worker:

Will I still be paid? For regular full-time (正社員 / seishain) employees, shutdown days are typically paid as part of annual salary. For haken (dispatch) and short-term contract workers, the situation varies significantly. Some haken contracts specify that shutdown days are unpaid. If you're on a daily or monthly flat-rate contract, check whether the shutdown days are counted as workdays for pay purposes. Don't assume — ask your haken gaisha directly, in writing if possible.

How much advance notice will I get? Reputable factories announce their annual shutdown dates months in advance through the nenkan karendā. If your factory posts the calendar in January, you'll know the Golden Week, Obon, and New Year dates well ahead of time. Use this information to plan flights, family visits, and administrative tasks.

Can I work during the shutdown? Some factories ask volunteers or specific workers to come in for maintenance, production catch-up, or security during shutdowns. If you're asked to work during an official factory holiday period, make sure you understand what premium pay applies and get the terms confirmed in writing or by email before agreeing.

6. If You're Asked to Work on a Holiday

Legally, your employer generally needs your agreement to schedule you on a holiday — especially on a 法定休日 (hōtei kyūjitsu). If a 36協定 (San-roku kyōtei — the labor-management agreement that allows overtime and holiday work) is in place at your workplace, holiday work can be required, but it must still be compensated at the correct premium rate.

You may also be offered 代休 (daikyū — compensatory time off) instead of, or in addition to, holiday pay. For example, instead of the 35% premium in cash, you might be given a day off later. The rules on how compensatory time off is calculated and when it must be taken vary by company, so ask HR to explain clearly before you agree.

If you feel pressured to work on a holiday without proper pay or agreement, that is a matter for your labor union (労働組合 / rōdō kumiai), your haken gaisha, or the nearest 労働基準監督署 (Rōdō Kijun Kantoku-sho — Labor Standards Inspection Office).

7. Planning Around Holidays: Practical Tips

Japan's holiday calendar affects more than just your work schedule. Here are the practical things to watch for:

Travel during Golden Week and New Year: Bullet trains (新幹線 / Shinkansen), long-distance buses, and domestic flights fill up weeks or even months in advance during these periods. If you need to travel — including to catch an international flight — book your domestic transportation as early as possible. Prices also spike, so planning ahead saves money.

Bank and government office closures: Japanese banks (銀行 / ginkō) and government offices (役所 / yakusho) are closed on all national holidays. If you need to open a bank account, renew your residence card (在留カード / zairyū kādo), or handle any official paperwork, do it well before a long holiday period — not after. The queues when offices reopen after Golden Week or New Year are long.

Remittance services: International money transfer services may be slower during holidays or may have reduced staff. Some bank-to-bank transfers may take an extra day to process around major holiday periods. If your family back home is expecting money on a specific date, send it a few days early during these periods.

Warning During Golden Week and New Year, many government offices and banks are closed. If you need to renew your residence card or handle visa paperwork, do it BEFORE the holiday period — not after. Immigration offices are closed on national holidays, and the backlog when they reopen can add additional days to processing times.

8. Summary

Japan's holiday calendar follows a consistent and predictable pattern once you know it. Sixteen national holidays, three major shutdown periods, and your factory's own annual calendar together tell you almost everything you need to know about your time off for the year. The key steps are simple: get your factory's 年間カレンダー early, understand how your contract handles shutdown pay, book travel well in advance for Golden Week and New Year, and handle any government paperwork before — never right before or after — a long holiday period. Japan gives you real, structured breaks from work. Plan for them well, and they become some of the best parts of the year.
