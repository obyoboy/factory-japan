What to Do if Your Factory Time Card Is Wrong in Japan

Last updated: 2026-04-04  |  6 min read

If your factory time card is wrong, don’t ignore it. Even a small mistake can affect your pay, overtime, and attendance record. The important thing is to check it early, report it clearly, and keep proof of what really happened.

1. Why time card errors matter

In Japan, the time card is more than a simple clock-in record. It is often used to calculate wages, overtime, late arrival deductions, and attendance history. If the record is wrong, you may be underpaid or marked as absent even when you worked normally.

Some factories use electronic systems, while others still use paper cards or shared terminals. No matter the system, you should treat every entry as important. A few minutes missing here and there can add up over a month.

Tip Check your time card regularly, not only on payday. It is much easier to correct an error the same day or the same week.

2. Common kinds of mistakes

Time card problems can happen for many reasons. You may forget to tap in after arriving, or the machine may fail to read your card. A supervisor may also enter the wrong time by mistake, or a shift change may not be updated correctly.

Common errorTypical result
Forgot to clock inLate arrival or missing attendance
Machine errorIncorrect start or end time
Supervisor input mistakeWrong shift record
Overtime not recordedUnderpaid wages

Some workers also have problems when they leave early for medical reasons, take a break outside the usual pattern, or work past midnight in a night shift. If the factory system is old or shared by many people, errors can happen more often than you expect.

3. Check the record as soon as you notice a problem

When you see an error, compare the record with your own memory and any proof you have. Look at your phone photo, transportation receipt, shift schedule, message from the supervisor, or any other note that supports your actual working time.

If the problem is on the same day, report it quickly. Waiting too long makes it harder for the company to verify the facts, especially when several workers use the same machine or when the memory of the shift is already unclear.

Warning Do not assume the company will fix it automatically. If you stay silent, the wrong record may be used for payroll and attendance, and that can create bigger problems later.

4. How to report the error politely

In Japan, a calm and clear explanation works best. Tell your supervisor, team leader, or the person in charge of attendance that the time card is incorrect. Explain the date, shift, and what the correct time should be.

You do not need a long story. Keep it simple: mention the mistake, the correct time, and any proof you have. If possible, ask for the correction in writing or send a message after speaking in person. That gives you a record of the report.

Example: “My time card shows 7:10, but I arrived at 6:55 because the train was on time and I entered the factory immediately. Could you please check the record?”

5. What proof can help

Useful proof can be as simple as a photo, a message, or a schedule. The best evidence depends on the situation. If you had to come in early or stay late, a message from the supervisor, a factory log, or a security gate record may help. For attendance disputes, even a screenshot of the shift group chat can be useful.

Keep your own notes if you often work variable shifts. Write down the date, clock-in time, clock-out time, and any unusual event, such as machine trouble or being asked to stay overtime. This habit makes it easier to explain things later.

Proof typeWhen it helps
Shift scheduleConfirms planned work hours
Message from supervisorShows approved changes
Photo or screenshotSupports time and location
Security or gate recordHelps verify entry and exit

6. If the company does not fix it right away

Sometimes the first person you speak to cannot change the record immediately. In that case, ask when the correction will be reviewed and who is responsible. Keep following up politely. If the wrong record affects your pay, you should check the payroll slip after wages are calculated.

If the error is repeated, document each case. A pattern of mistakes can show that the factory system needs improvement. It may also help if you later need to explain the issue to HR or a labor consultation office.

Tip Save copies of messages, photos, and corrected records. A simple folder on your phone can protect you if the issue happens again.

7. Preventing future problems

Good habits can reduce time card trouble. Arrive a little early, clock in immediately, and check that the machine records your entry. When changing shifts, confirm the new schedule in advance. If you work nights, make sure the date and end time are recorded correctly, since midnight crossings are easy to mix up.

If your factory uses a shared attendance system, learn who handles corrections and how the request should be made. Some workplaces require a form, while others accept a direct message. Knowing the process saves time and avoids misunderstanding.

8. When the issue becomes serious

If the wrong time card causes unpaid wages, repeated attendance penalties, or pressure to accept an incorrect record, the matter is more serious. You may need to speak with HR, a union representative, or a labor consultation service. Bring your proof and stay factual. Focus on the dates, times, and the exact difference in pay or attendance record.

Never sign something that says the record is correct if you know it is not. Once you confirm a false record, it becomes harder to challenge later. Protecting your attendance record is also protecting your rights as a worker.

Warning Do not sign or approve a wrong time card just because a supervisor asks you to “make it easy.” A false record can lead to wage loss or make you look dishonest later.

In a factory job, small attendance mistakes can become payroll problems fast. The safest approach is simple: check early, report clearly, keep proof, and follow up until the record is corrected.
