Pension Deductions on Your Japanese Factory Payslip: What They Mean

Last updated: 2026-04-21  |  6 min read

Every month, a chunk of your salary disappears as a pension deduction — and many factory workers have no idea what it is or whether they will ever see that money again. Here is exactly what it means and what you can do about it.

1. What Are Pension Deductions?

Look at your Japanese payslip (給与明細 / kyūyo meisai) and you will likely see a line that says 厚生年金 (kōsei nenkin). This is Japan's public pension contribution — money deducted from your salary every month and held by the government until you reach retirement age or qualify for a payout.

In simple terms: Japan requires almost everyone who works here to contribute to a pension fund. This is not optional — it is written into law, and your employer is legally required to deduct it and pay their share on top. Even if you plan to stay in Japan for only a few years, you are still enrolled automatically the moment you start working full-time at a factory.

Many foreign workers feel frustrated seeing this deduction because they do not plan to retire in Japan. The good news is that Japan has a system specifically for this situation — but you need to understand how it works before you leave.

2. The Two Main Pension Systems

Japan has two pension systems, and which one applies to you depends on how you are employed.

SystemJapanese NameWho It Covers
Employees' Pension Insurance厚生年金 (Kōsei Nenkin)Full-time employees and most dispatched / contract workers at companies
National Pension国民年金 (Kokumin Nenkin)Self-employed, freelancers, part-time workers not covered by employer pension

If you work at a factory through a staffing agency (haken) or directly as a full-time employee, you are almost certainly enrolled in Kōsei Nenkin. This is the more advantageous system because your employer pays half of the total contribution — meaning the deduction you see on your payslip is only half of what is actually being put into your pension account each month.

3. How Much Gets Deducted?

The Kōsei Nenkin contribution rate is currently set at 18.3% of your standard monthly remuneration (標準報酬月額 / hyōjun hōshū getsugaku). This total is split equally between you and your employer, so you each pay 9.15%.

4. What Benefits Do You Actually Get?

Contributing to Kōsei Nenkin builds up a pension balance that can pay out in several situations:

Old-age pension (老齢年金 / rōrei nenkin): If you have contributed for at least 10 years total and reach age 65, you can receive a monthly pension for the rest of your life. For many foreign workers who stay long-term, this is a real retirement benefit.

Disability pension (障害年金 / shōgai nenkin): If you become seriously ill or injured and cannot work, you may qualify for a disability pension regardless of your age. This protection is active from the moment you are enrolled — you do not have to wait years for it.

Survivor pension (遺族年金 / izoku nenkin): If you die while enrolled, your spouse or dependents may receive a pension. This is meaningful protection for workers who support families back home.

Lump-sum withdrawal (脱退一時金 / dattai ichiji kin): This is the most commonly used benefit for foreign factory workers who plan to return home. If you have contributed for at least 6 months and leave Japan permanently, you can apply for a one-time payout within 2 years of departure. More on this below.

5. How to Check Your Pension Record

Every person enrolled in Japan's pension system has a Nenkin Techō (年金手帳) — a pension booklet — or is registered with a basic pension number (基礎年金番号 / kiso nenkin bangō). You can check your full contribution history and estimated future benefits through the Nenkin Net (ねんきんネット) online portal run by the Japan Pension Service (日本年金機構 / Nihon Nenkin Kikō).

To register for Nenkin Net, you need your My Number (マイナンバー) or your basic pension number. The site has limited English support, but the Japan Pension Service has a toll-free multilingual consultation line that can assist you.

Tip Keep your pension booklet and any pension-related documents in a safe place. When you eventually apply for a lump-sum withdrawal or a full pension, you will need your basic pension number and records of where you worked. Losing these documents can make the process significantly harder.

6. Lump-Sum Withdrawal When Leaving Japan

If you are a foreign national who contributed to Japanese pension but will not retire in Japan, the dattai ichiji kin (脱退一時金) lets you claim back a portion of what you paid. Here is how it works:

You must apply within 2 years after leaving Japan. The application is submitted to the Japan Pension Service from outside Japan by mail. The amount you receive is based on how many months you contributed, with a calculation cap of 60 months (5 years) — even if you contributed longer, contributions beyond 5 years do not increase the lump-sum amount. However, those extra months still count toward a full pension if you ever return and continue contributing.

Warning If you miss the 2-year window after leaving Japan, you permanently lose the right to claim the lump-sum withdrawal. Set a reminder before you leave. Also note: once you receive the lump-sum payout, those contribution months are erased from your pension record. If you later return to Japan to work, you start building your pension history from zero.

Some countries have social security agreements (社会保障協定) with Japan that allow contribution periods to be combined or prevent double-enrollment. Check whether your home country has an agreement with Japan — this can significantly affect how much you ultimately receive.

7. Common Questions from Factory Workers

「Can I opt out of pension contributions?」 No. If you are a full-time or dispatched employee, enrollment is mandatory by law. Any employer who says they can skip it is breaking the law — and you would also lose disability and survivor pension protection.

「My employer said they pay it all, so I don't see a deduction — is that normal?」 No. Under Kōsei Nenkin, you and your employer each pay exactly half. If your payslip shows zero pension deduction, ask for a copy of your shakai hoken (社会保険) enrollment certificate and confirm with the Japan Pension Service. Some unscrupulous employers pocket the employee share.

「What is the difference between 厚生年金 and 健康保険 on my payslip?」 健康保険 (kenkō hoken) is health insurance — it covers your medical bills. 厚生年金 (kōsei nenkin) is the pension — it covers retirement and disability. Both are part of the 社会保険 (shakai hoken / social insurance) package, and both are deducted from your salary every month.

「Does overtime affect my pension deduction?」 Not immediately. Overtime is included when calculating your standard monthly remuneration, which is reviewed once a year. A sustained increase in overtime hours can raise your deduction slightly from the following September.
