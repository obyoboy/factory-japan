Hōrenso: The Japanese Workplace Rule of Report, Contact, and Consult

Last updated: 2026-04-06  |  6 min read

In Japanese workplaces, there is a golden rule called hōrenso. It stands for three communication habits every worker is expected to follow—and missing any one of them can seriously damage your reputation at work.

1. What Is Hōrenso?

Hōrenso (報連相 / hōrenso) is a shorthand for three essential workplace communication habits in Japan:

  • 報告 (hōkoku) — Report
  • 連絡 (renraku) — Contact / Notify
  • 相談 (sōdan) — Consult / Ask for advice

The word hōrenso is also the Japanese word for spinach—a fun way to remember that these habits keep you "healthy" at work. In almost every Japanese factory, these three actions are not optional. They are expected daily behavior.

Tip When you first start at a new factory, make hōrenso a habit from day one. Supervisors notice workers who communicate well—it builds trust quickly.

2. Hōkoku — How and When to Report

Hōkoku means reporting the result or progress of a task back to your supervisor. In a Japanese factory, you should report in these situations:

  • When you finish a task you were assigned
  • When you make a mistake or find a defect
  • When something is taking longer than expected
  • At the end of your shift if you did not finish your assigned work

Reports should be short and clear. Supervisors do not want long stories—they want the key facts. A common structure is: what you were doing → what happened → what is the current status.

Example in English: "I finished the inspection of Line 3. I found two defective parts and set them aside in the red bin."

Warning Never hide a mistake or skip a report because you are embarrassed. In Japanese factories, hiding problems (隠す / kakusu) is considered far worse than the mistake itself. Always report early.

3. Renraku — How and When to Notify

Renraku means sharing information that others need to know, even if no action is required right away. It is about keeping the team and your supervisor informed so that no one is surprised.

Use renraku in situations like:

  • You will be late to work
  • A machine is making an unusual sound but is still running
  • You need to leave your station for a few minutes
  • There is a change in schedule or materials that affects your line

Renraku is not just for big problems. Even small updates matter. Japanese workplaces run on shared awareness (minna de wakaru / みんなでわかる), and renraku is how that awareness is built.

4. Sōdan — When to Ask for Advice

Sōdan means consulting your supervisor or a senior coworker before making a decision you are unsure about. This is especially important for foreign workers who may not yet understand all the rules of the factory.

Use sōdan when:

  • You are not sure which task to prioritize
  • You do not understand an instruction
  • You think there might be a safer or better way to do something
  • You are asked to do something that seems different from your training

In Japan, asking for advice is not a sign of weakness—it is a sign of responsibility. Supervisors generally prefer a worker who asks questions over one who guesses and causes a bigger problem.

Tip If you do not speak much Japanese yet, you can say "Chotto ii desu ka?" (ちょっといいですか?) to get your supervisor's attention politely, then use gestures or your phone to communicate. Most supervisors appreciate the effort.

5. The Difference Between the Three

It can be confusing to know which one to use. Here is a simple way to think about it:

  • Hōkoku = After the fact. You did something, and you are telling your supervisor what happened.
  • Renraku = Right now or soon. Something is happening and you want to make sure the right people know.
  • Sōdan = Before you act. You are not sure what to do and you want guidance first.

All three overlap sometimes, and that is okay. The key is to keep communicating rather than staying silent.

6. Why Hōrenso Matters in Factories

Japanese factory culture is built on teamwork (chīmuwāku / チームワーク) and continuous improvement (kaizen / 改善). Both of these only work if information flows freely between workers and supervisors.

When you practice hōrenso well:

  • Your supervisor trusts you with more responsibility
  • Problems get caught early before they become big issues
  • Your coworkers can do their jobs better because they have the right information
  • You become known as a reliable worker (shinraidekiru hito / 信頼できる人)
Warning If you often skip hōrenso—especially hōkoku after finishing tasks or renraku when you are running late—your supervisor may start to see you as unreliable. This can affect your contract renewal and your relationship with the whole team.

7. Practical Phrases to Use Every Day

You do not need perfect Japanese to practice hōrenso. These short phrases will help you get started:

  • Hōkoku: "Owarimashita." (終わりました) — "I finished."
  • Hōkoku (problem): "Mondai ga arimasu." (問題があります) — "There is a problem."
  • Renraku (late): "Chotto okuremasu." (ちょっと遅れます) — "I will be a little late."
  • Sōdan: "Dō sureba ii desu ka?" (どうすればいいですか?) — "What should I do?"
  • Sōdan (unsure): "Wakaranai node, oshiete kudasai." (わからないので、教えてください) — "I don't understand, please teach me."

Even if your pronunciation is not perfect, making the effort to communicate in Japanese is always appreciated by Japanese supervisors and coworkers.
