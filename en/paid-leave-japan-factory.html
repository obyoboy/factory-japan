Paid Leave (有給休暇) in Japanese Factories: How It Works for Foreign Workers

Last updated: April 2026  |  8 min read

You've been at the factory for six months and you need a day off — not a sick day, not a holiday. You just need a break. Can you actually take paid leave? Do you have any? Is it something only full-time Japanese workers get? The answer is: you have paid leave, it's a legal right, and understanding how it works will help you use it without confusion or friction.

1. What Is 有給休暇?

有給休暇 (yūkyū kyūka / paid annual leave) — often shortened to 有休 (yūkyū) — is legally guaranteed paid time off under Japan's 労働基準法 (rōdō kijun hō / Labor Standards Act). It applies to all workers regardless of nationality, visa status, or whether you are direct-hire or a haken (dispatch) worker, as long as you meet the eligibility conditions.

Paid leave means you take a day off work and still receive your full day's pay for that day. Your employer cannot deduct wages for days you take as 有給.

2. How Many Days Do You Get?

The number of paid leave days increases with your length of service. Here is the standard table under the Labor Standards Act:

Months of ServiceDays GrantedNotes
6 months10 daysFirst grant — eligibility starts here
1 year 6 months11 days
2 years 6 months12 days
3 years 6 months14 days
4 years 6 months16 days
5 years 6 months18 days
6 years 6 months+20 daysMaximum under law

Eligibility conditions: You must have worked at least 80% of the scheduled workdays in the period. If you miss many days due to absence, your grant may be reduced proportionally.

Part-time workers: Part-time and short-hours workers also receive paid leave, but on a proportional scale based on the number of days per week they work.

3. The Mandatory 5-Day Rule

Since April 2019, Japanese law requires employers to ensure that any worker with 10 or more days of annual leave must take at least 5 of those days per year. This is not optional — it is the employer's legal obligation. If your company hasn't scheduled your 5 days, HR will likely approach you to designate specific dates.

This rule works in your favor: if your supervisor makes it difficult for you to take leave, the employer is actually at legal risk for not ensuring compliance with the 5-day minimum.

4. When Do Your Days Expire?

Unused paid leave days carry over for 2 years. After that, they expire. So if you receive 10 days at the 6-month mark but only use 3 of them, the remaining 7 days carry over to next year. Any days still unused after 2 years are forfeited.

Practically: try to use your days before they expire. Check with HR how many days you currently have — many companies show this on your payslip (給与明細 / kyūyo meisai) or on a notice board.

Tip Ask HR or your 班長 (hanchō): 「有給は何日残っていますか？」(yūkyū wa nan-nichi nokotte imasu ka / how many paid leave days do I have left?). You can also ask to see your 有給残日数 (yūkyū nokori nissū / remaining paid leave days). Knowing your balance helps you plan and ensures you don't lose days to expiry.

5. How to Request Paid Leave

The process varies by company, but generally follows these steps:

  1. Check your remaining days and decide which day(s) you want off.
  2. Submit a 有給休暇申請書 (yūkyū kyūka shinseisho / paid leave request form) — your company will have a form, often on paper or a kiosk terminal.
  3. Submit in advance — most factories require at least 3 business days' notice for non-urgent leave. The earlier the better.
  4. Your supervisor will approve or, in rare cases, request you change the date if the timing causes operational difficulties (this is called 時季変更権 / jiki henkou-ken / time-change right). They cannot simply deny your leave without rescheduling it.

For urgent personal reasons (medical emergency, family matter), explain briefly and most supervisors will accommodate same-day or next-day requests. You are not required to disclose detailed personal reasons.

6. Taking Leave When You Resign

If you resign, you are entitled to use your remaining paid leave days during your notice period, or have them paid out — depending on company policy. Many workers choose to take all remaining leave during the final weeks before their last day. Confirm this in writing with HR when you submit your resignation.

Warning Do not confuse paid leave (有給休暇) with unpaid leave (欠勤 / kekkin / unexcused absence). If you simply don't come to work without submitting a paid leave request, it will be recorded as 欠勤, which reduces your pay and can affect your attendance record for bonus and contract renewal purposes. Always submit a request — even if it's the same day for a sudden illness.

7. Summary: Your Leave Days Are Yours — Use Them

Paid leave in Japan is a legal right, not a favor. You earn days based on time served, they're yours to use, and since 2019 your employer is legally required to make sure you take at least 5 per year. Track your balance, submit requests properly in writing, and don't let days expire. A factory job is physically demanding — taking your leave is not a sign of weakness, it's a right you've earned.
