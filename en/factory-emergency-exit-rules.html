Emergency Exit Rules Every Factory Worker in Japan Should Know

Last updated: 2026-04-07  |  6 min read

In a Japanese factory, knowing where the emergency exits are — and how to use them correctly — could save your life. These rules are not just guidelines; they are required by law and enforced seriously by your employer.

1. Why Emergency Exit Rules Matter in Japanese Factories

Japanese factories handle heavy machinery, chemicals, heat, and high-voltage equipment every day. In an emergency — whether it's a fire, gas leak, earthquake, or industrial accident — the difference between life and death often comes down to whether workers know exactly what to do and where to go.

Under Japan's Fire Services Act (消防法 / shōbōhō) and the Industrial Safety and Health Act (労働安全衛生法 / rōdō anzen eisei hō), employers are legally required to post evacuation routes, conduct drills, and ensure emergency exits are always accessible. You, as a worker, are required to follow these procedures.

Tip On your very first day — or during your initial safety orientation — walk every evacuation route in your section. Don't just read the posted map. Walk it. Memorize it. Time yourself.

2. How to Identify Emergency Exits

In Japan, emergency exits are marked with a standardized green sign showing a running figure and an arrow. This is called the 非常口 (hijōguchi) sign. You will see it above doors, at the ends of corridors, and on stairwells.

Key things to look for:

  • Green illuminated signs — these glow even when the power goes out, thanks to photoluminescent or battery-backed lighting
  • Green push-bar doors — emergency exit doors in factories typically open outward with a single push bar; no key required from the inside
  • Arrow markings on the floor — some factories use green floor arrows to guide workers in low-visibility conditions (smoke, darkness)

3. What to Do When the Alarm Sounds

When the fire alarm or emergency siren goes off, follow these steps immediately:

  1. Stop all machinery — press the emergency stop (非常停止 / hijō teishi) on your workstation if it is safe to do so
  2. Do not grab your belongings — leave your bag, phone, and lunch box. Your life is not worth any of it
  3. Move quickly but do not run — running causes panic and injuries; walk fast and stay calm
  4. Follow the evacuation route signs — do not use elevators (エレベーター) under any circumstances
  5. Close doors behind you — this slows the spread of fire and smoke; do not lock them
  6. Go to the assembly point — your 集合場所 (shūgō basho) will be clearly marked outside the building, usually in a parking area or designated open space
  7. Report to your 班長 (hanchō) — your team leader or section leader will take attendance to confirm everyone is out
Warning Never re-enter the building after evacuating unless you are explicitly told it is safe by emergency personnel (消防士 / shōbōshi) or your supervisor. Re-entering without authorization can result in disciplinary action — and more importantly, serious injury or death.

4. Fire Doors and What You Must Never Block

Fire doors (防火扉 / bōka tobira) are heavy doors located at intervals along factory corridors and stairwells. They are designed to seal off sections of the building during a fire, preventing flames and smoke from spreading.

These doors must always remain unobstructed. In Japanese factories, blocking a fire door — even temporarily with a cart, pallet, or box — is a serious safety violation. Inspectors from the fire department (消防署 / shōbōsho) conduct unannounced checks, and violations can result in fines for your employer and formal warnings for your worksite.

Rules around fire doors:

  • Never prop them open with equipment or materials
  • Never stack goods within one meter of a fire door
  • Report any fire door that does not close properly to your supervisor immediately

5. Evacuation Drills (避難訓練 / hinan kunren)

Japanese factories are required by law to conduct evacuation drills at least once or twice per year. These drills are taken seriously — they are not just a formality. Participation is mandatory.

During a drill, you will:

  • Evacuate your section using the assigned route
  • Gather at the assembly point
  • Be counted by your 班長 (hanchō)
  • Receive a debrief on the time taken and any issues observed
Tip If the drill instruction is given in Japanese and you don't fully understand, ask your hanchō or a coworker to walk through the steps with you beforehand. Many factories provide evacuation maps in multiple languages — ask HR if you haven't received one.

6. Special Situations: Earthquakes and Chemical Leaks

Not all factory emergencies involve fire. Japan is earthquake-prone, and many factories work with hazardous chemicals. The procedures are slightly different:

Earthquake (地震 / jishin):

  • Drop, cover, and hold during shaking — get under a sturdy table or crouch near an interior wall away from shelves and machinery
  • Do not run outside during shaking — falling debris is dangerous
  • After shaking stops, follow evacuation instructions from your supervisor

Chemical leak (化学物質漏洩 / kagaku busshitsu rōei):

  • Do not try to identify or stop the leak yourself unless trained
  • Cover your mouth and nose, evacuate upwind of the source
  • Alert your supervisor and activate the alarm if not already sounding
  • Specific assembly points may differ from fire evacuations — confirm with your supervisor

7. Your Responsibilities as a Factory Worker

Safety in a Japanese factory is a shared responsibility. Beyond simply following instructions, you are expected to take an active role:

Warning Ignoring safety rules in a Japanese factory — including those related to emergency exits — is grounds for disciplinary action, and in serious cases, termination of your contract. Safety compliance is non-negotiable in Japanese manufacturing culture.
