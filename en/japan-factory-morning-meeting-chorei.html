Japan Factory Morning Meetings (Chōrei):
What to Expect Every Day

Last updated: April 2026  |  7 min read

Every morning at a Japanese factory, before the machines start and the shift officially begins, there is a ritual that nearly every worker takes part in: chōrei (朝礼). If you are new to factory work in Japan, this might catch you off guard. Nobody hands you a schedule. You just notice that everyone stops, lines up, and faces the supervisor. This guide explains exactly what chōrei is, what happens during it, and how to act so you start every shift on the right foot.

1. What Is Chōrei (朝礼)?

Chōrei literally means "morning assembly" — 朝 (chō) means morning, and 礼 (rei) means ceremony or greeting. It is a short daily gathering at the start of the shift where the supervisor shares information, reviews safety points, and prepares the team mentally for the day ahead.

Almost every Japanese factory holds chōrei, whether the shift starts at 6 AM, 8 AM, or even midnight. If you work a night shift (夜勤 / yakin), your "morning meeting" simply happens at the start of your night shift — the format is the same.

2. What Typically Happens

Chōrei usually follows the same pattern every day. Here is what to expect:

  • Line-up (整列 / seiretsu): Workers stand in a line or semi-circle facing the supervisor or team leader (班長 / hanchō).
  • Attendance check: The supervisor confirms everyone is present. If you are absent without notice, it is noticed immediately.
  • Safety topic (安全確認 / anzen kakunin): A brief safety reminder — recent incidents, hazards to watch for that day, or a rotating safety tip.
  • Production announcements: Targets for the day, schedule changes, equipment issues, or information about visitors.
  • Stretching (ラジオ体操 / rajio taisō): Many factories do a short group stretching routine before work. This is considered injury prevention, not optional recreation.
  • Closing words: The supervisor ends with a phrase like "yoroshiku onegaishimasu" (よろしくお願いします), and workers respond together.

3. How Long Does It Last?

Typically 5 to 15 minutes. If stretching is included, it can run up to 20 minutes. Chōrei is considered part of your work time at most factories, but confirm this with your dispatcher or HR — some companies start the clock after chōrei ends.

4. What You Should Do as a Foreign Worker

You do not need to understand every word. What matters most is your body language and participation.

  • Arrive early. Show up 3–5 minutes before the shift starts so you are already in position when chōrei begins.
  • Stand straight and pay attention. Looking at your phone or chatting during chōrei is disrespectful.
  • Follow along visually. Watch your teammates and mirror what they do — bow when they bow, stretch when they stretch.
  • Say the group response. Even if you are unsure of the words, say something. "Yoroshiku onegaishimasu" (よろしくお願いします) covers most situations.
  • Do not leave early. Stay until the supervisor signals the meeting is over.
Tip Even if you understand almost no Japanese, watch your hanchō (班長 / team leader) for visual cues. When they bow, bow. When they say something and pause, nod and say "hai" (はい). This shows respect and awareness even without fluency.

5. What Happens if You Skip Chōrei?

Skipping chōrei without permission is treated the same as arriving late. It can result in a formal warning (警告 / keikoku) on your record. Repeated absences from chōrei can affect your evaluation and, if you are a haken (派遣 / dispatched) worker, may lead to your contract not being renewed.

Warning Never skip chōrei, even if you feel it is just a formality. In Japanese factory culture, group participation is a sign of commitment. Missing it once is noticed. Missing it regularly marks you as a problem worker.

6. Chōrei Phrases Worth Learning

You do not need to be fluent, but knowing these phrases will help you participate and make a good impression:

  • おはようございます (ohayō gozaimasu): Good morning — say this when you arrive
  • よろしくお願いします (yoroshiku onegaishimasu): "I'm counting on you" / group closing phrase
  • はい (hai): Yes — respond when your name is called
  • わかりました (wakarimashita): I understand — when the supervisor explains something
  • 気をつけます (ki wo tsukemasu): I will be careful — after a safety reminder

7. Summary

Chōrei is one of the first things that will feel different about working in a Japanese factory. It is short, structured, and repeated every single day. Once you get used to it, chōrei becomes a natural part of your rhythm — a signal that the shift is starting, the team is together, and everyone is ready. Show up on time, pay attention, participate with your body even if not your words, and you will fit in faster than you expect.
