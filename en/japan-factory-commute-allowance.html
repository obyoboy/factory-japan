Commute Allowance at Japanese Factories: What You're Owed and How It Works

Last updated: April 2026  |  8 min read

Every month, a line on your payslip says 交通費 (kōtsūhi / commute allowance) or 通勤手当 (tsūkin teate / commutation allowance). Is that extra spending money? Is it taxed? What happens if you move closer to the factory and your commute costs drop? These are common questions that many foreign factory workers never bother to ask — and that can cost them money or, worse, get them into serious trouble. This guide explains exactly how commute allowance works in Japan, from the basics to the edge cases.

1. What Is Commute Allowance?

Commute allowance is a payment from your employer designed to reimburse — or partially cover — the cost of getting to and from work. It is not a bonus, not a reward, and not extra income. It is cost reimbursement. The key distinction matters because it affects how it is taxed and how it must be reported.

In Japanese, the terms you will see on your payslip are 交通費 (kōtsūhi) or 通勤手当 (tsūkin teate). Both refer to the same concept: the employer paying back what it costs you to commute. Most Japanese factories and companies include this as a standard component of the compensation package, though the exact rules depend on each employer.

2. Is Commute Allowance Required by Law?

Here is a fact that surprises many workers: Japan has no legal requirement to pay commute allowance. The 労働基準法 (rōdō kijunhō / Labour Standards Act) does not mandate it. Employers are free, in theory, to offer zero commute support.

In practice, however, virtually all factories and companies pay it. The obligation comes from your employment rules document, the 就業規則 (shūgyō kisoku / work rules / company regulations). This internal document — which your employer is legally required to have if they employ 10 or more workers — specifies how commute allowance is paid, at what rate, and under what conditions. If your company's shūgyō kisoku says they pay commute allowance, that is a binding commitment.

If you are a haken (派遣 / dispatch) worker, the rules come from your dispatch agency, not the factory where you work. Read your dispatch agency's employment terms carefully — they may pay a flat daily amount, a distance-based rate, or the actual cost of a commuter pass, depending on their own policies.

3. How Commute Allowance Is Calculated

There are two main methods, depending on how you commute:

Public transport commuters: If you use trains or buses, you submit the cost of your 定期券 (teikiken / commuter pass) to HR. The company reimburses that amount — either monthly, quarterly, or as a lump sum if you buy a 6-month pass. You need to show proof: the receipt from buying the pass, or the pass itself.

Car or bicycle commuters: If you drive or cycle to work, allowance is usually calculated by distance using a fixed rate per kilometer per month set by the company. Rates vary but typically range from a few hundred yen to a few thousand yen per month depending on the distance. Some companies use Japan's National Tax Agency distance tier table as a reference.

There is a critical cap to know: the tax-free limit for commute allowance is ¥150,000 per month. Up to that amount, commute allowance is not counted as taxable income. Any amount above ¥150,000 per month becomes taxable income and must be declared. For the vast majority of factory workers, this cap is never reached — but it is important to understand.

4. Getting and Using a Commuter Pass

A 定期券 (teikiken / commuter pass) is a prepaid train or bus pass valid for a specific route for a set period — one month, three months, or six months. It is significantly cheaper per trip than buying individual tickets each day. Major train companies in Japan sell these at ticket machines or staffed windows at stations.

The process for claiming commute allowance at most factories works like this:

  1. When you start work, complete a 通勤申請 (tsūkin shinsei / commute application form) and submit it to HR. This form records your home address, your nearest station, your commute route, and the cost.
  2. HR reviews and approves the application.
  3. You receive the allowance either as a monthly addition to your salary or as a lump sum to cover a longer-term pass.
  4. Keep all receipts for passes you purchase. If HR asks for proof, you need to provide it.

If your commute route changes — even temporarily — notify HR. Claiming reimbursement for a route you are not actually using is a form of fraud.

5. The Company Bus: 送迎バス

Many factories — especially those in industrial areas far from train stations — operate a 送迎バス (sōgei basu / company shuttle bus) that picks workers up from designated stops near residential areas or train stations and drops them at the factory gate.

If a company bus is available to you and you use it, you typically do not receive a separate commute allowance — because the company is already covering your transport cost by running the bus. Some factories still offer a small allowance to cover the portion of your commute before the bus stop, but this varies by company.

Check your shūgyō kisoku or ask HR directly: "Does using the company bus affect my commute allowance?" The answer will be specific to your employer.

6. What If You Move Closer to the Factory?

This is an area where many foreign workers make expensive mistakes. If you move to a new address — whether closer to the factory or further away — you are legally and contractually required to report this to HR promptly. Most companies require you to update your address and commute application within two weeks of moving.

The reason this matters: if you move closer to the factory and your actual commute cost drops, but you continue claiming the old (higher) commute allowance, you are claiming money you are not entitled to. In Japan, this is treated seriously — it can be classified as fraudulent expense claiming, which is a disciplinary offense. In severe cases it has led to termination and demands to repay the difference.

Conversely, if you move further away and your costs increase, you can submit an updated commute application to have your allowance increased accordingly. This is a legitimate claim — use it.

Warning Claiming commute allowance for a route you are not actually using — or continuing to claim old costs after moving to a new address — can be treated as fraud and may result in disciplinary action including termination. Always update your address and commute details with HR promptly when your situation changes. Two weeks is the standard window most companies require.

7. What About Working from Home or Missing a Day?

For office workers, some Japanese companies now deduct commute allowance for days when employees work from home. Since factory workers physically must be on the production floor to do their job, remote work almost never applies. Your commute allowance is not typically adjusted day by day based on attendance.

However, if you take an extended leave of absence — for illness, for example — it is worth checking your company's policy. Some companies continue paying commute allowance during leave; others pause it. Read your shūgyō kisoku or ask HR.

8. Commute Allowance When You Resign

If you resign or your contract ends, commute allowance is typically prorated to your last working day. The complication arises if you have already received a lump-sum payment covering a longer period — for example, a 6-month commuter pass reimbursement — and you leave before that period is up.

In that case, the company may ask you to return the unused portion. For example, if you were reimbursed for a 6-month pass but leave after month 3, the company may expect you to return approximately half of the amount. This is standard practice. Some companies handle this by deducting it from your final paycheck; others ask for direct repayment. Check your contract or employment rules for the specific terms.

Tip If you know you will be staying at the same factory for at least 6 months, ask HR whether you can claim reimbursement for a 6-month commuter pass upfront. A 6-month pass is significantly cheaper per day than buying monthly passes one at a time — the discount can be 10–15% or more. Some companies reimburse the full lump sum when you buy it; others pay monthly regardless. Either way, buying the longer pass saves real money.

10. Summary

Commute allowance is a standard and expected benefit at Japanese factories. It is not taxable income within the allowed limits, it is not a bonus, and it is not yours to keep if your commute situation changes. The rules are straightforward: submit an accurate commute application when you start, keep your receipts, update HR when you move or your commute changes, and follow up if your payslip looks wrong. Treat this benefit honestly and it will work in your favor every month. Abuse it and the consequences can be serious. Know what you are entitled to, document everything, and keep it honest.
