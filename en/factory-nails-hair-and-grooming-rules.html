Nail, Hair, and Grooming Rules in Japanese Factories

Last updated: 2026-04-11  |  6 min read

Japanese factories have strict grooming rules that go beyond personal preference — they exist for safety, hygiene, and product quality. Knowing these rules on your first day will help you avoid warnings and fit in quickly.

1. Why Grooming Rules Exist in Japanese Factories

In a Japanese factory, your appearance is not just a personal matter — it is part of the job. Grooming rules exist for three core reasons: safety, hygiene, and product quality. Long nails can tear gloves and contaminate products. Loose hair can get caught in machinery or fall into food and electronics. Jewelry can conduct electricity or snag on equipment.

These rules apply to everyone equally — Japanese workers, foreign workers, temporary staff, and full-time employees alike. The rules are usually explained during your first-day orientation (nyūsha kenshū / 入社研修), but they may be posted on notice boards in the locker room or changing area as well.

2. Hair Rules: Tying, Tucking, and Covering

Hair management is one of the most strictly enforced grooming rules in Japanese factories. The basic standard across most facilities is that all hair must be fully contained — nothing can hang loose while you are on the factory floor.

At most factories, you will be required to wear a hair net (hēanetto / ヘアネット) over your entire head. In food, pharmaceutical, and electronics factories, this is non-negotiable. In some automotive or parts factories, a helmet or hard hat may replace the hair net, but you still need to tie your hair back tightly before putting it on.

  • Short hair: must be tucked into a hair net or under a hat completely
  • Medium hair: tie into a bun or ponytail first, then cover with a net
  • Long hair: braid or bun tightly, secure with a hair tie, then cover fully
  • Dreadlocks or thick braids: must fit fully inside the net — bring extra bobby pins if needed
Tip Buy a pack of hair nets before your first day and practice putting them on at home. Some workers keep a spare net in their locker in case one breaks during a shift. Hair nets are usually sold at 100-yen stores (hyakkin / 百均) or convenience stores near industrial areas.

Regarding hair color: most factories allow dyed hair as long as it is fully covered on the floor. However, some conservative workplaces — especially those with traditional Japanese corporate culture — may ask you to return to a natural color. Check your employment contract or ask your haken (派遣 / staffing agency) coordinator before dyeing.

3. Nail Rules: Short, Clean, and Natural

Nail rules in Japanese factories are almost universal: nails must be kept short, clean, and free of polish or extensions. This applies to both fingernails and, in some food factories, toenails (if sandals are worn in break areas).

The standard maximum length is that nails should not extend beyond the fingertip — roughly 1–2 mm past the skin. You will likely be checked during the daily morning inspection (chōrei / 朝礼) or before entering a clean room.

Warning Nail polish, gel nails, and acrylic extensions are banned in almost all Japanese factory environments. Chips of polish or fragments of acrylic can fall into products and cause contamination or product recalls — which is a serious incident in Japanese manufacturing. Coming to work with gel nails after being warned once is one of the fastest ways to get your contract terminated early.

4. Facial Hair and Makeup Guidelines

Rules for facial hair and makeup vary more by factory type than by company. Here is the general breakdown:

Facial hair: Beards and mustaches are permitted in most Japanese factories as long as they are neatly trimmed and do not pose a hygiene risk. In food processing or pharmaceutical factories, you may be required to wear a face mask (masuku / マスク) at all times, which effectively covers most facial hair anyway. Some factories may ask you to shave completely — this will be stated in your rules booklet (kiteisho / 規程書).

Makeup: Light makeup is generally accepted in office-facing roles, but on the factory floor, the rules are stricter. Heavy foundation, powders, and glitter-based products are often prohibited because particles can contaminate products. In clean rooms or food lines, you may be asked to wear no makeup at all. A simple tinted moisturizer with SPF is usually fine, but ask your line leader (rainī / ラインリーダー) if you are unsure.

5. Jewelry and Accessories: Remove Before You Enter

All jewelry must be removed before entering the production area. This is a firm rule in virtually every Japanese factory. The risks are real: rings can conduct electricity, necklaces can catch on conveyor belts, and earring backs can fall into products undetected.

Most factories provide a small tray or locker for storing accessories in the changing room (kōi shitsu / 更衣室). Leave everything there before you go to the floor. Items typically banned include:

  • Rings (including wedding rings — yes, even those)
  • Necklaces and pendants
  • Bracelets and bangles
  • Dangling or hoop earrings (stud earrings may be allowed in some factories)
  • Watches and fitness trackers
  • Piercings that cannot be removed — ask your supervisor, as some factories allow medical-grade retainers
Tip If your ring is difficult to remove (due to swelling, for example), tell your supervisor proactively. Do not try to hide it with tape — that is usually not accepted and looks like you are trying to deceive the inspection process.

6. Department-Specific and Seasonal Rules

Grooming rules can change depending on which line or department you work in, and sometimes by season. Workers in clean rooms (kuriin rūmu / クリーンルーム) have the strictest standards — full hair nets, face masks, gloves, and sometimes full-body coveralls. Workers on outdoor loading docks or in warehouses may have more relaxed rules.

In summer, some factories allow workers to tie hair loosely under a helmet without a net — but confirm this with your hanchō before assuming. In winter, you may be allowed to wear a thin beanie under your hard hat, as long as all hair is still contained.

If you move between lines or departments, do not assume the rules carry over. Always re-check the posted rules (keijiban / 掲示板) in the new area or ask your new team leader directly.

7. The Morning Inspection: What to Expect

Many Japanese factories run a brief morning inspection before the shift begins. This is often part of the chōrei (朝礼 / morning meeting) and takes only 1–2 minutes. Your team leader or safety officer (anzen kanrisha / 安全管理者) may do a quick visual check of the line.

They are looking for: visible hair outside the net, nail polish or long nails, jewelry, and whether your uniform is clean and properly fastened. If something is flagged, you will usually be asked to fix it immediately — go back to the locker room, sort it out, and return. Repeated violations can result in a formal warning (keikoku / 警告) and may affect your contract renewal.

8. Your Daily Grooming Checklist

Use this quick checklist every morning before entering the factory floor:

  • Hair fully tucked into hair net or hat — no stray strands visible
  • Nails short, clean, no polish or extensions
  • All jewelry removed and stored in locker
  • Face mask worn if required in your area
  • No excessive makeup or strong fragrance
  • Uniform clean, buttons fastened, no loose threads

Getting into this routine quickly will earn you quiet respect from your Japanese coworkers and supervisors. In Japanese factory culture, attention to detail (monozukuri no kokoro / ものづくりの心) starts before the first part is touched — it starts with how you show up.
