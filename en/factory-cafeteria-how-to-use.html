How to Use the Factory Cafeteria in Japan: A Complete Guide

Last updated: 2026-04-15  |  5 min read

The factory cafeteria (食堂 / shokudō) is one of the most useful facilities at your workplace in Japan. Understanding how it works will save you time, money, and awkward moments during your break.

1. What Is a Factory Cafeteria (食堂 / Shokudō)?

Most medium and large factories in Japan have an on-site cafeteria called a 食堂 (shokudō). It is not just a convenience — for many workers, it is the main place to eat during the workday. The cafeteria typically serves breakfast, lunch, and sometimes dinner depending on shift schedules. Prices are heavily subsidized by the company, so eating there is almost always cheaper than buying food outside or from vending machines.

If you live in a company dormitory, your dormitory meals may be separate from the factory cafeteria, so check with your supervisor (班長 / hanchō) to understand which facility covers which meals.

2. How the Meal Ticket System Works

Many factory cafeterias in Japan use a meal ticket machine called a 食券機 (shokkenki). Here is the typical process:

StepWhat to Do
1. Insert money or use your cardPut cash into the machine or tap an IC card if the machine supports it
2. Select your mealPress the button for the dish you want — buttons usually have photos or icons
3. Receive your ticket (食券 / shokken)Take the printed ticket that comes out
4. Hand the ticket to staffGive it to the cafeteria staff at the counter when ordering
5. Wait for your trayYour food will be prepared and called or handed to you

Some newer factories deduct cafeteria charges directly from your paycheck (給与天引き / kyūyo tenbiki). In that case, you may receive a meal card or swipe a card linked to your employee ID. Ask your HR representative how your specific factory handles this.

Tip When you first start, watch what your coworkers do at the cafeteria machine before your first turn. Most systems are simple once you see the flow once. You can also look for picture menus posted on the wall nearby.

3. Reading the Menu

Cafeteria menus in Japan typically feature set meals called 定食 (teishoku). A standard teishoku includes a main dish, rice (ご飯 / gohan), miso soup (味噌汁 / misoshiru), and a small side dish. Common main dishes include:

Japanese TermWhat It Is
焼き魚 (yakizakana)Grilled fish
唐揚げ (karaage)Japanese fried chicken
豚カツ (tonkatsu)Breaded pork cutlet
カレーライス (karē raisu)Japanese curry with rice

Daily specials (日替わり / higawari) rotate each day and are usually the cheapest option. Look for a whiteboard or posted sheet near the entrance that lists the day's special.

4. Cafeteria Break Times and Schedules

Factory cafeterias operate on strict schedules tied to shift break times. Unlike a regular restaurant, you cannot walk in whenever you want. The lunch break (昼休み / hiruyasumi) is usually 45 minutes to 1 hour long. Cafeterias can get very crowded in the first 10–15 minutes, so some factories stagger break times by section or line.

Warning Never leave the cafeteria with your tray before finishing or before your break officially ends. Leaving the designated break area without permission can be considered a rule violation and may be reported to your supervisor.

If your factory uses staggered breaks (交代休憩 / kōtai kyūkei), your hanchō will tell you which time slot your team uses. Never use a different break slot without approval — this disrupts the production schedule and causes problems for other shifts.

5. Seating Rules and Unwritten Etiquette

Japanese factory cafeterias often have informal but important unwritten rules about seating. While there are usually no assigned seats, over time certain groups or teams tend to sit in the same areas. As a new worker, it is wise to follow your coworkers to find where your team sits.

General cafeteria etiquette in Japanese factories:

  • Do not jump the queue at the meal ticket machine or counter
  • Return your tray to the designated tray return area (返却口 / henkyakuguchi) after eating
  • Separate trash into the correct bins (food waste, plastic, burnable, etc.)
  • Do not save seats for large groups during peak hours
  • Keep your voice at a reasonable level — cafeterias are shared spaces
  • Wipe up any spills immediately using the paper towels provided
Tip If you have dietary restrictions (halal, vegetarian, allergies), ask your HR representative or the cafeteria manager early — preferably before you start working. Some factories can accommodate requests, but they need advance notice. Do not assume the cafeteria will have suitable options on your first day.

6. Vending Machines and Additional Food Options

Alongside the cafeteria, most factories have vending machines (自動販売機 / jidōhanbaiki) in the break room or near the cafeteria. These sell drinks, snacks, cup noodles, and sometimes sandwiches. Prices are slightly higher than the cafeteria but the machines are available outside of cafeteria hours.

Some factories also have a small convenience-style counter or kiosk near the entrance where workers can buy onigiri (rice balls), bread, and bottled drinks. Check if your factory has this option for days when the cafeteria menu does not suit you.

7. Payment Methods Accepted

Payment methods vary by factory. Common options include:

Ask your HR or onboarding coordinator which method your factory uses. If it is paycheck deduction, understand how much is taken per meal so it does not surprise you on payday.

8. Common Mistakes to Avoid

Foreign workers new to Japanese factory cafeterias often make a few avoidable mistakes. Being aware of these will help you fit in smoothly from day one:

  • Not returning the tray: In Japan, you are expected to clean up after yourself. Leaving a tray at the table is considered rude.
  • Using the wrong break slot: Always follow your team's scheduled break time. Going early or late can create problems.
  • Talking loudly on the phone: Phone calls at the table are generally frowned upon. Step away if you need to take a call.
  • Skipping the queue: Even if a machine looks free, check if someone is already waiting nearby.
  • Ignoring trash separation: Throwing everything into one bin will get noticed and is considered disrespectful.
Warning Do not bring strong-smelling foods from home into the cafeteria. Foods with very pungent odors (certain fermented foods, strong spices) can cause complaints in enclosed cafeteria spaces. If you bring a bento from home, be mindful of how it smells when opened near others.
