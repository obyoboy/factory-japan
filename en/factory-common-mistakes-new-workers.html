Common Mistakes New Foreign Workers Make in Japanese Factories

Last updated: 2026-04-04  |  6 min read

Starting work in a Japanese factory can feel overwhelming. Small mistakes happen easily when you are learning the rules, the pace, and the expected way of speaking. This guide shows the most common problems new foreign workers run into and how to avoid them.

1. Why New Workers Make Mistakes

Starting a job in a Japanese factory means learning a lot at once: the work itself, the schedule, the safety rules, and the workplace culture. Many new workers are eager to do well, but they may be afraid to ask questions or may assume that the way things work in their home country is the same in Japan. That is where small mistakes often begin.

Factories are usually very structured. Time, order, and consistency matter. Even if your technical skill is strong, missing a small rule can create problems for the team. The good news is that most beginner mistakes are preventable once you know what to watch for.

Tip In your first weeks, focus on accuracy and safety before speed. A careful worker who asks questions is often trusted faster than a fast worker who makes avoidable mistakes.

One useful mindset is to treat every shift as a learning session. Notice how experienced workers prepare, move, speak to supervisors, and handle breaks. Copying good habits early can save you from trouble later.

2. Mistake: Not Confirming Instructions

A very common problem is nodding along without fully understanding the instruction. New workers may feel embarrassed to say, “I do not understand,” so they guess instead. In a factory, that can lead to the wrong part being used, the wrong machine setting, or a missed step in the process.

Japanese workplaces often value confirmation. If a supervisor or senpai (senpai) gives you directions, repeat the key points back in your own words. This is called kakunin (confirming). It shows that you are taking the instruction seriously, not that you are questioning authority.

SituationBetter response
You do not catch the instructionAsk again slowly: “Could you say that once more?”
You understand only part of itRepeat the part you heard and ask for the missing detail
The task seems different from yesterdayConfirm before starting, even if it feels small

It is better to ask twice than to fix a mistake later. If your workplace uses written instructions, keep them nearby and check them before acting.

3. Mistake: Ignoring Safety Rules

Safety mistakes are the most serious errors in a factory. New workers sometimes remove gloves because they feel uncomfortable, skip protective gear for a moment, or reach into an area before the machine fully stops. These actions may seem minor, but they can cause injury or damage quickly.

Never assume a shortcut is acceptable just because “it only takes a second.” In factories, that second can be enough for an accident. Learn where the emergency stop is, when you must wear goggles or a mask, and which areas are off-limits. If a rule seems unnecessary, still follow it and ask why later.

Warning Never bypass guards, disable alarms, or use a machine in a way that was not explained to you. Unsafe behavior can lead to serious injury, disciplinary action, or dismissal.

If you feel unsure about a machine, stop and ask. Good factories prefer a worker who pauses for safety over one who takes a risky guess.

4. Mistake: Being Late or Unprepared

In Japan, being on time means arriving early enough to change clothes, store your belongings, and be ready before the shift starts. New workers sometimes think “on time” means walking in at the exact start minute. In many factories, that is already late.

Being unprepared also includes forgetting a lunch box, not charging your train card, or arriving without the items your workplace requires. These small issues can make your morning stressful and may create a bad impression on your supervisor.

To avoid this, set your alarm earlier than you think you need, prepare your uniform and supplies the night before, and check the route to work in advance. If transportation is delayed, contact the workplace as soon as possible.

5. Mistake: Not Speaking Up About Problems

Some new foreign workers stay silent when they are tired, confused, or hurt. They may think speaking up will cause trouble, show weakness, or make them look difficult. In reality, staying silent can create bigger problems for everyone.

If you feel pain, notice a defect, do not understand a tool, or cannot keep up with the pace, tell someone early. Japanese factories often expect workers to report issues before they grow. Even a simple phrase like “I have a problem” or “Please check this” can prevent a bigger loss later.

Tip If your Japanese is limited, prepare a few emergency phrases you can use quickly. Write them in your phone or on a card so you can point and speak clearly when needed.

Reporting a problem is not the same as complaining. It is part of being reliable.

6. Mistake: Poor Communication With Senpai and Supervisors

Factory communication is often short and practical. New workers sometimes answer too softly, forget to say yes or no clearly, or avoid eye contact and body language that signals attention. Others may use overly casual speech with senior staff, which can sound rude even if they do not mean it that way.

Learn the basic communication style used in your workplace. Usually, a clear “Hai” (yes), a respectful tone, and simple updates are enough. If you finish a task, say so. If you need help, say so. If something is missing, say so before the situation becomes urgent.

Also pay attention to nonverbal habits. Standing where you can be seen, turning toward the speaker, and responding quickly all help build trust. You do not need perfect Japanese to communicate well, but you do need to be consistent and respectful.

7. Mistake: Not Respecting Workplace Etiquette

Workplace etiquette in Japan includes many small habits: greeting coworkers, keeping your area clean, following break times, and not using your phone at the wrong moment. New workers may overlook these details because they seem less important than the job itself, but they strongly affect how others view you.

For example, leaving tools out of place can slow down the next person. Talking loudly across the floor can disturb concentration. Taking a break longer than allowed can create resentment. These are not dramatic mistakes, but they add up.

Try to observe the habits of experienced workers during the first month. Notice how they store items, clean their stations, and speak during busy periods. Matching the rhythm of the workplace is often just as important as learning the task.

8. How to Build Better Habits Fast

The fastest way to improve is to create a simple routine. Check your schedule the night before. Review safety rules before each shift. Ask one clear question when something is unclear. Keep a small notebook for new words, machine names, and instructions. These habits reduce stress and help you learn faster.

It also helps to find one trustworthy person, such as a senpai (senpai) or supervisor, and ask for regular feedback. A short check-in after the shift can show you what to improve before mistakes repeat. If your company offers training, attend carefully and take notes even if the lesson seems basic.

Good habitWhat it prevents
Confirming instructionsWrong process or wrong materials
Arriving earlyLate starts and rushed work
Reporting problems earlyInjuries and production delays

Progress in a factory is usually steady, not dramatic. The workers who improve fastest are often the ones who stay observant, humble, and consistent.
