Common Mistakes New Foreign Workers Make in Japanese Factories

Last updated: 2026-04-04  |  7 min read

Starting strong at a Japanese factory means knowing what not to do. This guide covers the top mistakes new foreign workers make—and how to avoid them.

Starting a new job at a Japanese factory is exciting—but it can also feel overwhelming. The rules are different from what many foreign workers are used to, and even small mistakes can affect your relationships with coworkers and supervisors. This guide covers the most common mistakes new foreign workers make, so you can avoid them from day one.

1. Being Late or Not Reporting Absence Properly

In Japan, punctuality is not just polite—it is expected. Being late, even by a few minutes, is seen as disrespectful and unprofessional. Many factories start their shifts with a morning meeting called chōrei (朝礼), and if you are not there on time, you disrupt the whole team.

If you need to be late or are sick, you must call your supervisor or your 派遣会社 (haken-gaisha / staffing agency) before your shift starts. Sending a message after you are already late is not acceptable in most factories.

Tip Save your supervisor's phone number and your haken-gaisha's number in your phone before your first day. When you call to report an absence, speak clearly and state your name, your section, and the reason.

2. Skipping or Not Paying Attention During Chōrei

The morning meeting, or chōrei (朝礼), happens at the start of every shift. During this time, the hanchō (班長 / team leader) gives important announcements: safety reminders, production targets, and changes to procedures. Many new workers do not understand Japanese well, so they zone out or look at their phones.

This is a big mistake. Even if you do not understand everything, you need to stand straight, look attentive, and listen. Your coworkers and supervisors notice if you look bored or distracted. After the meeting, ask a bilingual coworker to explain anything you missed.

3. Ignoring Safety Rules and PPE Requirements

Japanese factories take safety very seriously. Every worker is required to wear the correct personal protective equipment (PPE)—including helmets, safety shoes, gloves, and protective eyewear—in the areas where it is required. Ignoring these rules, even once, can result in a warning or removal from the factory floor.

New workers sometimes think safety rules are suggestions, or that they can skip them when no supervisor is watching. This attitude will get you in trouble fast. In Japanese factory culture, safety is a shared responsibility, and your coworkers will also report violations.

Warning Never remove your safety equipment while working on the floor, even during slow periods. If a factory inspector or internal auditor sees you without your PPE, your haken-gaisha may be notified and you could lose your assignment.

4. Not Following 5S and Sorting Rules

5S is a Japanese workplace organization system: Seiri (整理 / Sort), Seiton (整頓 / Set in order), Seisō (清掃 / Shine), Seiketsu (清潔 / Standardize), and Shitsuke (躾 / Sustain). Almost every Japanese factory follows 5S principles, and workers are expected to maintain their workstation and common areas accordingly.

Common 5S mistakes include: leaving tools in the wrong place, not cleaning up your workstation at the end of a shift, putting trash in the wrong bin, and not returning items to their labeled storage spots. These may seem minor, but they are taken very seriously by supervisors.

5. Using Your Phone or Talking Loudly on the Production Floor

Using a smartphone on the production floor is prohibited in almost all Japanese factories. This includes texting, checking social media, taking photos, and making calls. The reasons are clear: phones are a distraction, a safety risk, and in some factories, a security concern because of trade secrets.

Many new workers are caught using their phones quietly in corners or bathrooms during work time. This is still a violation. Keep your phone in your locker or designated storage area during your shift. Some factories allow phones to be used only during officially designated break times in the break room.

6. Not Asking for Help When You Are Confused

Many new foreign workers stay quiet when they do not understand instructions. They nod and say "hai" (はい / yes), even when they are completely lost. This leads to mistakes on the production line, which can affect product quality, slow down the team, and damage equipment.

In Japan, asking questions can feel intimidating, especially if there is a language barrier. But supervisors would much rather you ask once and do the job correctly than stay silent and make an error. If your Japanese is limited, prepare a few simple phrases, or ask a coworker who speaks your language to help you communicate.

Tip Learn these basic Japanese phrases: "Mō ichido oshiete kudasai" (もう一度教えてください / Please teach me again) and "Wakarimashita" (わかりました / I understand). Showing effort to communicate in Japanese earns you respect from your team.

7. Misunderstanding Break and Overtime Rules

Break times in Japanese factories are strict. When the break signal sounds, you stop. When the return signal sounds, you are already at your station and ready to work—not walking back. Being even slightly late to return from break is noticed and frowned upon.

Overtime (zangyō / 残業) is common in many factories, especially before deadlines or during busy seasons. Some workers assume they can decline overtime freely, but in many factories, refusing without a valid reason affects how your supervisors view you. If you cannot do overtime, inform your haken-gaisha in advance so they can arrange your schedule accordingly.

8. Summary: Small Habits, Big Impact

Working in a Japanese factory is a real opportunity—good pay, stable hours, and the chance to build skills. But the rules and expectations are different from many other countries, and small mistakes can pile up quickly. By staying punctual, following safety and 5S rules, paying attention during chōrei, and communicating honestly when you need help, you will build trust with your team and supervisors. That trust makes everything easier—your daily work, your schedule, and your future at the company. Start strong, and the rest will follow.
