Social Insurance (Shakai Hoken) for Japan Factory Workers:
A Simple Guide

Last updated: April 2026  |  9 min read

Your first payslip in Japan will probably surprise you. A chunk of your gross salary disappears before it ever reaches your bank account. Some of that is tax — but a significant portion is shakai hoken (社会保険), Japan's social insurance system. Many foreign factory workers see these deductions and feel frustrated. This guide explains exactly what you are paying for, what you get in return, and — importantly — how to get some of that money back when you leave Japan.

1. What Is Shakai Hoken?

Shakai hoken is the umbrella term for Japan's mandatory social insurance programs. If you work full-time (or qualify as a part-time worker with enough hours), your employer is legally required to enroll you. There is no opt-out.

It covers four main areas: health, pension, employment, and workplace accidents. Each works differently and provides different benefits.

3. How Much Is Deducted from Your Pay?

Deduction rates vary slightly depending on your prefecture and employer, but as a rough guide for the employee share:

  • Health insurance: approximately 5% of monthly salary
  • Pension (kōsei nenkin): approximately 9.15% of monthly salary
  • Employment insurance: approximately 0.6% of monthly salary
  • Workers' comp (rōsai): 0% — paid entirely by your employer

Total: expect roughly 14–15% of your gross salary to be deducted for social insurance. Your employer pays a similar amount on top of your salary, so the actual cost to the system is roughly double your contribution.

Tip Check your payslip (給与明細 / kyūyo meisai) every month. The social insurance deductions should match the rates above. If something looks wrong — an unusually large deduction or nothing deducted at all — ask your HR or dispatch company right away. Non-enrollment by your employer is illegal.

4. Your Health Insurance Card (保険証)

Once enrolled, you will receive a health insurance card (保険証 / hokenshō). This is one of the most valuable documents you will carry in Japan. Here is how it works:

  • Show it at any hospital, clinic, or pharmacy in Japan
  • You pay only 30% of the cost — insurance covers the other 70%
  • It covers most medical services: doctor visits, prescriptions, surgery, hospital stays
  • Emergency care is also covered if you show your card or report it afterward

If you lose your card, report it to your employer or dispatch company immediately and request a replacement. Do not delay — you need it for any medical visit.

5. Pension Insurance and the Lump-Sum Withdrawal

This section is especially important for foreign workers who plan to return home. Every month you are enrolled in kōsei nenkin (厚生年金), you accumulate pension credits. Normally these build toward a retirement pension paid in Japan — but if you leave Japan, you have another option.

Dattai ichijikin (脱退一時金) — lump-sum pension withdrawal — allows you to claim a refund of your pension contributions after you leave Japan permanently. Key rules:

  • You must have contributed for at least 6 months
  • You must no longer live in Japan and have no Japanese address
  • You must apply within 2 years of leaving Japan
  • The refund amount depends on how many months you contributed and your salary level
  • A 20% withholding tax is deducted, but you may be able to reclaim some of it

Apply through Japan's Pension Service (日本年金機構 / Nippon Nenkin Kikō) after you leave. Keep your pension number (基礎年金番号 / kiso nenkin bangō) — you will need it for the application.

Warning Do not miss the 2-year deadline for the lump-sum pension withdrawal. Once it passes, you cannot reclaim those contributions. Many foreign workers lose this money simply because they did not know about it or forgot to apply in time.

6. Employment Insurance Benefits

If your contract ends or you are laid off, and you have been enrolled in koyō hoken (雇用保険) for at least 12 months, you can apply for unemployment benefits at the Hello Work (ハローワーク) employment office. Benefits are typically 50–80% of your average daily salary for a set number of days based on your age and years of enrollment.

This benefit applies to foreign workers on eligible visas. Ask your local Hello Work office about your specific situation.

7. What If Your Employer Does Not Enroll You?

Some smaller companies or unscrupulous dispatch agencies try to avoid enrolling workers to save money. This is illegal. If you are working 30+ hours per week and are not enrolled in shakai hoken, you have the right to report this to the Labor Standards Inspection Office (労働基準監督署 / rōdō kijun kantokusho). You are protected from retaliation by law.

8. Summary

Shakai hoken takes a real bite out of your paycheck — but it provides real protection. Your health insurance card makes medical care affordable. Your pension contributions are not lost — you can get them back when you leave. And employment insurance gives you a safety net if your contract ends early. Understand what you are enrolled in, keep your documents, and remember the 2-year deadline for the pension withdrawal. That money is yours.
