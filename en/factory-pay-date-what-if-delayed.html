What to Do If Your Factory Salary Is Delayed in Japan

Last updated: 2026-04-13  |  6 min read

Getting paid on time is your legal right in Japan. If your salary hasn't arrived on the expected pay date, don't panic — but don't stay silent either. Here's exactly what to do.

1. Know Your Pay Date (Kyūryō-bi / 給料日)

Every factory in Japan is required to tell you your kyūryō-bi (給料日) — your designated payday — in writing before you start work. This is usually stated in your employment contract or the factory's work rules (shūgyō kisoku / 就業規則). Common paydays are the 15th or 25th of each month, but some factories pay on the last working day of the month.

If you don't know your exact payday, check your contract or ask HR (人事部 / jinjibu) immediately — you should not have to guess.

Tip Take a photo of the payday clause in your contract so you always have it on your phone. This makes it easy to reference when asking questions.

2. Confirm the Delay Is Real

Before raising a concern, rule out simple explanations:

  • Bank transfer timing: If your pay date falls on a weekend or national holiday, Japanese banks typically process the transfer on the previous business day. Your money may already be there.
  • New bank account: If you recently changed bank accounts or just opened one, there can be a one-cycle delay before the transfer is set up correctly.
  • First-month proration: Your first paycheck may cover only a partial month, so the amount might look different or arrive slightly later.

Check your bank account balance through your passbook (tsūchō / 通帳) or online banking app first. If the money genuinely isn't there after the payday has passed, move to the next step.

3. Ask Your Hanchō or HR — Calmly and Directly

Your immediate supervisor is called your hanchō (班長) or kōchō (工長). Start by politely asking them whether the payment was processed. Use a neutral, professional tone — this is a business matter, not a confrontation.

You can say something like:

「給料日を過ぎましたが、まだ入金されていないようです。確認していただけますか？」
(Kyūryō-bi wo sugimashita ga, mada nyūkin sarete inai yō desu. Kakunin shite itadakemasu ka?)
"The payday has passed but it seems the payment hasn't come in yet. Could you check on it?"

If your hanchō doesn't know, go directly to the HR or payroll department (kyūyo tantō / 給与担当).

Warning Do not shout, threaten, or involve other workers in a group complaint at this stage. Escalating emotionally too quickly can damage your reputation and make resolution slower. Keep the first inquiry calm and formal.

4. Check If There Is a System-Wide Delay

Sometimes a delay affects all workers at the same time — this can happen due to a bank system error, a company accounting problem, or a factory-wide issue. Ask a trusted Japanese coworker whether they also haven't been paid. If the delay is system-wide, the company is almost certainly already working to fix it.

5. Put Your Inquiry in Writing

If you don't get a clear answer within 2–3 business days after the payday, follow up in writing. Send an email or hand-written memo to HR that states:

  • Your name and employee number
  • Your expected payday
  • The amount you expected to receive
  • A polite request for explanation and a date by which you expect the payment

Keep a copy. A written record protects you if the situation escalates and you need to involve outside support.

Tip In Japan, a written record carries much more weight than a verbal complaint. Even a simple email creates a paper trail that HR must respond to officially.

6. Know Your Legal Rights

Japanese labor law (Rōdō Kijunhō / 労働基準法) is very clear: wages must be paid in full, on the scheduled date, in cash or bank transfer. Delaying salary without justification is a violation of the Labor Standards Act.

Key legal points:

  • Employers cannot delay wages without the worker's agreement.
  • If wages are unpaid, workers can demand payment plus damages.
  • Repeated or intentional non-payment can result in criminal penalties for the employer.

This law applies to all workers in Japan, regardless of nationality or visa type.

7. Contact the Labor Standards Inspection Office

If the company does not pay after your written request, or gives you vague non-answers, you have the right to contact the Rōdō Kijun Kantoku-sho (労働基準監督署) — the Labor Standards Inspection Office. This is a government body that investigates labor law violations.

You can visit in person, call, or in some offices, use an online consultation service. Bring your employment contract, pay stubs, and any written communication with HR. Consultations are free and interpreters may be available at larger offices.

Warning Do not quit your job out of frustration before receiving your unpaid wages. Once you resign, recovering unpaid wages becomes significantly more complicated. Stay employed while pursuing resolution through official channels.

8. Use Support from Your Dispatch Company or Union

If you are working through a haken (派遣) — a staffing or dispatch company — your primary employer is technically the dispatch company, not the factory. Contact the dispatch company's staff coordinator (tantōsha / 担当者) first. They are legally responsible for your wages.

If you are a union member (kumiai-in / 組合員), inform your union representative immediately. Labor unions in Japan can negotiate directly with management on your behalf and have significant legal standing to demand prompt payment.
