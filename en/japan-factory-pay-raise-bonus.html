Pay Raises and Bonuses in Japanese Factories: What Foreign Workers Need to Know

Last updated: April 2026  |  9 min read

You've been on the line for a year. Your attendance is good, your work is accurate, you do what you're asked. Will your pay go up? Will you get a bonus? And if so, how much? These are questions many foreign factory workers have but rarely ask. This guide explains how pay raises and bonuses work in Japanese manufacturing — the evaluation system, the bonus calendar, and what actually determines whether your pay increases.

1. How Japanese Factory Pay Raises Work

Most Japanese factories operate on an annual pay review system called 人事考課 (jinji kōka / personnel evaluation). Once a year — typically in April for the upper half-year evaluation, or according to your company's cycle — your performance is assessed and a decision is made about your pay for the next period.

There are two main types of raises:

  • 定期昇給 (teiki shōkyū / regular annual raise): A fixed raise given automatically to most workers who have completed a year of service, regardless of performance. Many companies offer a small base raise of ¥1,000–¥5,000/month as a starting point.
  • 査定昇給 (satei shōkyū / merit-based raise): An additional raise based on your performance evaluation score. Higher scores = higher raise. This is where attendance, attitude, skill level, and output quality affect your pay directly.

2. The Evaluation Calendar

Japanese companies typically divide the year into two evaluation periods:

PeriodJapanese TermEvaluation WindowPay Reflected
Upper half-year上半期 (kamihankī)April–SeptemberOctober onwards (or next April)
Lower half-year下半期 (shimohankī)October–MarchApril onwards

In many factories, the actual pay raise is applied once per year in April (春闘 / shuntō / spring wage negotiation season), while the evaluation scores from both halves influence the total raise amount.

3. What Gets Evaluated

Factory performance evaluations typically assess:

  • 勤怠 (kinтai / attendance and punctuality): This is often the most heavily weighted factor in factory settings. Missing work, arriving late, or leaving early has a direct negative impact on your score.
  • 作業品質 (sagyō hinshitsu / work quality): Defect rate, accuracy, adherence to standard procedures.
  • 生産性 (seisansei / productivity): Cycle time, output vs. target.
  • 安全 (anzen / safety compliance): Following safety rules, reporting hazards.
  • 態度・協調性 (taido / attitude and teamwork): Cooperation with colleagues and supervisors, willingness to learn.

Your 班長 (hanchō / team leader) typically submits your initial evaluation to HR, which is then reviewed by the section manager (課長 / kachō). As a foreign worker, you may not see the full evaluation form — some companies share it, some don't. You can always ask HR: 「評価結果を見せていただけますか？」(hyōka kekka o misete itadakemasu ka / may I see my evaluation results?).

Tip The single biggest factor in factory evaluations is 勤怠 (kintai / attendance). A worker with perfect attendance and average output will almost always score higher than a worker with outstanding output but frequent absences. If you want a raise, protect your attendance record first.

4. How Bonuses Work

Japanese companies typically pay 賞与 (shōyo / bonus) — also called ボーナス (bōnasu) — twice per year:

BonusPayment MonthCovers Performance Period
夏季賞与 (kaki shōyo / summer bonus)June–JulyOctober–March (previous lower half-year)
冬季賞与 (tōki shōyo / winter bonus)November–DecemberApril–September (upper half-year)

The typical bonus amount in manufacturing is expressed as a multiple of your monthly base pay. Industry averages in Japanese manufacturing range from about 1.5 to 3 months' salary per bonus, so 3–6 months' total bonus per year. The exact amount depends on company performance, your evaluation score, and your length of service.

5. Bonus Eligibility for Foreign Workers

Most Japanese factories pay bonuses to all regular workers regardless of nationality. However, there are important eligibility rules:

  • Service requirement: You typically need to have been employed on the 在籍日 (zaishoku-bi / reference date) — usually June 1 for the summer bonus and December 1 for the winter bonus. Workers who resign before this date may not receive the bonus.
  • Contract type: Direct-hire (正社員 / seishain or 契約社員 / keiyaku shain) workers usually receive full bonuses. Haken (dispatch) workers' bonuses depend on their dispatch agency's contract terms, not the factory's own bonus system.
  • Pro-rated amounts: If you started mid-year, your bonus will typically be prorated based on months of service during the evaluation period.

6. Haken Workers: A Different System

If you work through a dispatch agency (派遣会社 / haken gaisha), your pay raise and bonus structure is determined by the agency, not the factory. Some dispatch agencies offer end-of-year bonuses or periodic pay reviews. Others offer a higher hourly rate in place of a traditional bonus. Read your dispatch contract carefully and ask your agency directly about their bonus and raise policies.

Warning Do not assume that because a Japanese coworker received a bonus, you will automatically receive the same amount. Bonus amounts vary significantly by evaluation score, length of service, and contract type. If you believe you were incorrectly excluded from a bonus you're entitled to, ask HR in writing for clarification and check your contract terms. Verbal assurances are not enough — make sure anything about pay is confirmed in writing.

7. Summary: How to Maximize Your Pay Growth

Pay raises and bonuses in Japanese factories are real and meaningful — but they go to workers who show up reliably, follow procedures accurately, and cooperate with the team. Focus on these fundamentals:

  • Protect your attendance record above everything else
  • Learn and follow standard procedures exactly
  • Know your evaluation cycle and ask your supervisor for feedback before the review period ends
  • Confirm your bonus reference dates so you don't accidentally resign just before eligibility
  • If you're haken, ask your agency about their specific raise and bonus structure

The system rewards consistency and reliability. If you understand how it works, you can work it.
