Haken vs Direct Hire: What Foreign Factory Workers Need to Know

Last updated: March 2026  |  8 min read

When you start looking for factory work in Japan, you will quickly come across two very different types of employment. Some workers are hired directly by the factory. Others are sent there through a staffing agency. On the surface, both groups may be doing the same job on the same floor — but their contracts, pay structures, rights, and futures inside that factory are very different. Understanding which type of contract you are on is one of the most important things you can do as a foreign worker in Japan.

1. What Is Haken (Dispatch Employment)?

派遣 (haken) means "dispatch" in Japanese. When you work as a haken employee, you have a contract with a staffing agency (派遣会社 / haken-gaisha), not with the factory itself. The agency finds you work at a client factory, sends you there, and continues to pay your wages. The factory tells you what to do every day, but legally, your employer is the agency.

This arrangement is extremely common in Japan's manufacturing sector. Major factories — including automotive suppliers, electronics plants, and food processing facilities — rely heavily on haken workers to handle fluctuating production volumes. For foreign workers, many entry points into factory work in Japan are through haken agencies, particularly those that specialize in placing foreign nationals.

Tip: If you are new to Japan and looking for factory work, starting through a reputable haken agency is often the fastest way in. Many agencies provide housing support, Japanese language assistance, and help with visa paperwork — services a direct-hire factory may not offer.

2. What Is Direct Hire (Chokusetsu Koyō)?

直接雇用 (chokusetsu koyō) means your employment contract is directly with the factory or manufacturing company. You are their employee, not a third party's. Within direct hire, there are two main types:

  • 正社員 (seishain) — Full-time permanent employee. This is the most secure type of employment in Japan. Benefits are comprehensive, job security is high, and career advancement is possible.
  • 契約社員 (keiyaku-shain) — Fixed-term contract employee. You are hired directly by the company, but for a specific period — typically 6 months to 1 year, with the possibility of renewal.

As a foreign factory worker, being offered a direct hire contract — even a fixed-term one — is generally seen as a step up from haken. It signals that the company values you enough to take on the legal and administrative responsibility of employing you directly.

3. Key Differences at a Glance

Category Haken (Dispatch) Direct Hire

Who gives you daily instructions? The factory The factory

Job stability Lower — agency can reassign or end contract Higher — especially as seishain
Bonuses (賞与 / shōyo) Rarely included Often included (seishain)

4. Pay: Which Type Pays More?

Many haken workers are surprised to find that their hourly rate can be competitive with — or even higher than — some direct hire positions. This is because haken agencies need to attract workers, and the hourly 時給 (jikyū) rate is their main selling point.

However, comparing raw hourly wages misses the bigger picture. Direct hire employees — particularly 正社員 (seishain) — receive benefits that are not reflected in the hourly rate:

  • 賞与 (shōyo) — Semi-annual bonuses, typically 1–3 months of salary per payment, twice a year
  • 退職金 (taishoku-kin) — Severance or retirement pay accumulated over years of service
  • 交通費 (kōtsūhi) — Commuting allowance, which is often fully covered by the company
  • Paid leave (有給休暇 / yūkyū kyūka) that is easier to actually use without fear of contract non-renewal

When you add these up over a full year, a direct hire seishain earning a lower base rate can easily take home more total compensation than a haken worker on a higher hourly rate.

Warning: Some haken agencies advertise high hourly rates but deduct housing, food, or equipment fees from your paycheck. Always ask for a written breakdown of what will be deducted before accepting a contract. Make sure you understand your actual 手取り (tedori) — take-home pay — not just the advertised rate.

5. Job Security and Contract Risks

One of the biggest practical differences between haken and direct hire is what happens when the factory slows down production or faces economic difficulties.

In a haken arrangement, the factory can reduce or stop using dispatch workers with relatively little notice. Your agency may try to place you at another client factory, but if no placement is available, your work — and income — can disappear quickly. This happened on a large scale during the 2008 financial crisis in Japan, when mass cancellations of haken contracts made international news under the term 派遣切り (haken-giri), or "dispatch cuts."

Direct hire employees, especially 正社員, have much stronger legal protections. Japan's labor laws make it very difficult and costly for companies to dismiss full-time employees without valid reason. Fixed-term direct hire contracts (keiyaku-shain) also have protections — in particular, a contract that has been renewed multiple times may qualify the worker for permanent status under certain conditions.

6. The 3-Year Haken Rule You Need to Know

Japanese law places a limit on how long a haken worker can be assigned to the same workplace in the same role: a maximum of 3 years. This rule is sometimes called the 3年ルール (sannen rūru).

When that limit is reached, the factory has several options:

  • Offer you a direct hire contract (either fixed-term or permanent)
  • Assign you to a different department or role to reset the clock
  • End the arrangement — meaning you return to the agency without a placement

From a foreign worker's perspective, the 3-year mark is an important milestone. If the factory values your work, this is typically when they will offer you a direct hire position. If they do not, you should start planning ahead with your agency well before the deadline.

Tip: If you are approaching 3 years at the same factory and want to stay, let your agency coordinator know early. Ask directly whether there is a possibility of direct hire conversion (直接雇用への転換 / chokusetsu koyō e no tenkan). Raising this topic shows initiative and gives both sides time to prepare.

7. Social Insurance: Are You Covered?

Both haken and direct hire workers in Japan should be enrolled in 社会保険 (shakai hoken) — the Japanese social insurance system — if they work enough hours. This includes:

  • 健康保険 (kenkō hoken) — Health insurance
  • 厚生年金 (kōsei nenkin) — Employees' pension
  • 雇用保険 (koyō hoken) — Employment insurance (unemployment benefits)

For haken workers, enrollment is managed by the agency. For direct hire workers, it is managed by the company. As long as you are working 20+ hours per week and meet other criteria, you should be enrolled regardless of contract type.

The key difference comes when you lose your job. Direct hire employees who are dismissed or whose contracts end may be eligible for 失業給付 (shitsugyō kyūfu) — unemployment benefits — more readily. Haken workers whose contracts are not renewed also qualify, but the process and benefit period may differ.

Warning: If your haken agency is telling you that you are not eligible for social insurance enrollment, this may be a legal violation. Workers who are employed for more than 2 months and work sufficient hours are generally required to be enrolled. If you are unsure, contact your local ハローワーク (Hello Work) — Japan's public employment office — for free guidance.

8. Which Is Better for a Foreign Worker?

There is no single right answer — it depends on where you are in your work life in Japan and what you need right now.

Haken may be the better choice if:

  • You are just starting out and need housing support and a fast placement
  • You are still learning Japanese and want an agency to handle administrative tasks
  • You want flexibility to try different factories or locations

Direct hire is generally better if:

  • You have found a company you want to stay with long-term
  • You want access to bonuses, retirement pay, and full company benefits
  • You want a clear path toward permanent residency (long, stable employment helps)
  • You want greater job security and predictability

Many foreign workers in Japan start with haken, build skills and experience, earn the trust of a factory, and are then offered direct hire over time. Think of haken not as a trap, but as a possible entry point — as long as you understand the rules and your rights from the start.

Know what type of contract you are signing. Ask questions before you start. Keep copies of all your documents. And remember: understanding your situation is the first step to improving it.
