Basic Earthquake Response for Factory Workers in Japan

Last updated: 2026-04-04  |  6 min read

Earthquakes can happen without warning in Japan, and factory workers need to know exactly what to do. The right response can prevent injuries, reduce panic, and help everyone evacuate safely.

1. Why earthquake readiness matters in a factory

Japan experiences frequent earthquakes, and factories often have heavy machines, moving equipment, glass, chemicals, and narrow walkways. That means a small mistake during shaking can quickly become a serious injury. If you work in a factory, you should not wait until an emergency to learn the basics. Knowing the correct response helps you protect yourself, support your team, and keep damage as low as possible.

In many workplaces, the first few seconds matter most. People may hear a rumble, feel the floor shake, or see tools and shelves moving. Your goal is simple: stay calm, protect your head, and avoid anything that can fall, break, or block your escape route. Even if you have worked in Japan for a long time, it is worth reviewing the process regularly because earthquakes are different from fires, typhoons, or everyday accidents.

Tip During orientation, ask where the emergency exits, assembly point, first-aid kit, and evacuation routes are located. Do not rely on memory alone.

2. What to do the moment shaking starts

When an earthquake begins, stop what you are doing as quickly as possible and protect yourself first. If you are near a machine, press the stop button only if you can do it immediately and safely. Then get low, cover your head and neck, and hold on to a stable object if one is available. If there is no desk or sturdy cover, crouch beside a strong structure and shield your head with your arms. Stay away from windows, glass panels, shelves, forklifts, and stacked materials.

Do not run at the first shake. Running while the floor is moving can cause falls, collisions, and confusion. If you are carrying sharp tools or heavy items, put them down if you can do so safely. If you are on stairs, hold the rail and move carefully. If you are in a moving vehicle inside the site, follow your workplace rules and stop only when it is safe to do so. The main principle is to reduce immediate danger before trying to move anywhere.

Warning Do not rush toward exits during strong shaking. Crowding, falling objects, and broken glass can cause more injuries than the quake itself.

3. Safe actions around machines and equipment

Factories have special risks because machines can keep moving, restart unexpectedly, or create secondary hazards after the shaking starts. If a machine has an emergency stop button and you can reach it safely, use it. If stopping the machine would put you in danger, protect yourself first and wait for instructions. Never try to repair a jammed machine or pick up fallen parts while the shaking continues.

Be especially careful around forklifts, conveyors, presses, cutting tools, welding areas, and chemical storage. A quake can cause loads to fall, gas lines to leak, or hot materials to spill. If you smell gas, hear unusual alarms, or see sparks or leaking liquid, move away and report it immediately. Do not assume someone else has already called it in. In an emergency, clear communication prevents bigger accidents.

4. Evacuation after the shaking stops

Once the shaking stops, check yourself and the people nearby for injuries. If someone is trapped, badly hurt, or unable to move, call for help immediately and follow the workplace emergency plan. Then listen for announcements from your supervisor, safety officer, or disaster leader. In Japan, workplaces often have a designated person responsible for guiding evacuation, so follow that direction instead of improvising.

Leave the area in an orderly way. Walk, do not run. Avoid elevators unless your company has a specific emergency instruction that says otherwise. If you must pass through an area with debris, broken glass, or fallen items, move slowly and watch your footing. Go to the designated assembly point and check in so the company knows who is safe and who may still need help. Do not go back inside until the all-clear is given.

Tip Keep your shoes nearby during work hours if your workplace allows it. In an evacuation, sturdy shoes can protect you from glass and debris.

5. Communication, reporting, and team coordination

After a quake, clear communication helps prevent panic and delays. If you are a foreign worker, make sure you know the key words used at your workplace for evacuation, shutdown, assembly, and headcount. If you do not understand an instruction, ask for it to be repeated slowly. Simple phrases can save time when everyone is stressed. Confirm important information instead of guessing.

Report damage, missing coworkers, spills, fire, gas smell, or blocked exits as soon as possible. Do not spread rumors or post unverified information in group chats. If your factory uses radios, intercoms, or a disaster app, learn how to use them before an emergency happens. Team coordination is not only about following orders; it is also about speaking up quickly when you notice a real problem.

6. Common mistakes that make earthquakes more dangerous

People often make the same mistakes when they are frightened. One common error is grabbing personal belongings before protecting the body. Another is running to the exit during strong shaking. Some workers try to continue production because they do not want to stop the line, but that can lead to severe injuries. Others assume that if the shaking feels small, nothing serious can happen. Even a moderate quake can topple objects, break glass, and knock people down.

Another mistake is returning to the work area too soon. Aftershocks are common in Japan, and conditions can change fast. A machine that looked stable may have shifted. A shelf that seemed secure may now be leaning. Treat the area as unsafe until the workplace gives a clear all-clear. Safety is not over when the first shaking ends.

Warning Never re-enter a damaged area on your own to check tools, phones, or documents. Aftershocks and hidden hazards can cause serious injury or death.

7. Simple preparation you can do before the next quake

Good earthquake response starts before the ground moves. Learn your workplace map, the sound of emergency alarms, and the location of safety equipment. Know who your supervisor, hanchō (班長), or safety leader is during a disaster. Keep your work area tidy so aisles and exits stay clear. Secure items that can fall, and report damaged racks, loose cables, or unstable storage before they become a problem. If your factory has drills, participate seriously instead of treating them as a formality.

It also helps to think through your personal routine. Where do you stand during your shift? Which tools are most dangerous if they fall? Which route is safest from your station? Small habits make a big difference. When workers prepare in advance, they can respond faster, stay calmer, and help protect the entire team.

Earthquakes are stressful, but they are manageable when people know what to do. Protect yourself first, follow the workplace plan, communicate clearly, and wait for the all-clear before resuming work. That simple discipline can prevent injuries and keep a difficult situation from becoming much worse.
