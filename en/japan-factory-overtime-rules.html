Overtime in Japanese Factories: Rules, Pay, and Your Rights as a Foreign Worker

Last updated: April 2026  |  9 min read

The shift is supposed to end at 5:30 pm, but your 班長 (hanchō / team leader) tells everyone to stay. Nobody moves. You're not sure if overtime is mandatory, how much extra you'll be paid, or whether you can say no. This situation plays out every week in factories across Japan — and most foreign workers stay silent because they don't know the rules. This guide explains everything: the legal limits, how your overtime pay is calculated, and what to do if your employer breaks the law.

1. What Is Zangyō? Japan's Overtime Basics

残業 (zangyō / overtime) refers to any working hours that exceed Japan's legally defined standard work hours. Under the 労働基準法 (rōdō kijunhō / Labour Standards Act), the legal maximums are:

  • 8 hours per day
  • 40 hours per week

Any time worked beyond these limits is legally "overtime" and must be paid at a premium rate. This law applies to every worker in Japan — full-time, part-time, haken (dispatch), and foreign workers on any visa. There are no exceptions for nationality or visa type.

Note that your work contract may define a "scheduled work time" that is shorter than 8 hours — for example, 7.5 hours per day. In that case, the time between 7.5 and 8 hours may be paid at your regular rate. Only hours beyond 8 per day (or 40 per week) trigger the overtime premium. Read your contract carefully to understand this distinction.

2. How Overtime Pay Is Calculated

Japan's overtime pay system has three distinct categories, each with its own premium rate. These rates are minimums set by law — your employer can pay more, but never less.

Type of OvertimeWhen It AppliesMinimum Premium
Regular Overtime (時間外労働 / jikangai rōdō)Beyond 8 hrs/day or 40 hrs/week+25% of hourly rate
Late Night Work (深夜労働 / shin'ya rōdō)Between 22:00 and 05:00+25% of hourly rate
Late Night OvertimeOvertime AND between 22:00–05:00+50% (both premiums stack)
Legal Holiday Work (法定休日 / hōtei kyūjitsu)Work on a legally designated weekly day off+35% of hourly rate
Holiday Overtime at NightHoliday work AND between 22:00–05:00+60% (both premiums stack)

To calculate your overtime pay for one hour: Hourly Rate × (1 + Premium Rate). For example, if your base hourly rate is ¥1,200 and you work regular overtime, you receive ¥1,200 × 1.25 = ¥1,500 per overtime hour.

If you earn a monthly salary rather than an hourly wage, your effective hourly rate is calculated by dividing your monthly salary by the number of scheduled working hours per month. This calculation is printed on your payslip — check it against the formula above whenever overtime appears.

Tip Japan uses two types of holidays: 法定休日 (hōtei kyūjitsu / legally mandated day off — usually Sunday) and 所定休日 (shotei kyūjitsu / contractually scheduled day off — often Saturday). Working on a hōtei kyūjitsu earns the 35% holiday premium. Working on a shotei kyūjitsu earns the regular 25% overtime premium, not 35%. Your contract should specify which day is which.

3. Legal Overtime Limits: How Much Can They Ask You to Work?

Japan's Labour Standards Act sets a default overtime cap of 45 hours per month and 360 hours per year. However, most factories use a legal agreement called the 三六協定 (sanjū-roku kyōtei / Article 36 Agreement) to push beyond this limit.

The sanjū-roku kyōtei is a written agreement between the employer and worker representatives (usually the company union) that sets higher overtime limits. It must be filed with the Labour Standards Inspection Office. Once it is in place, factories can legally require overtime up to these higher caps:

  • Up to 80 hours per month in months with heavy workload
  • Up to 720 hours per year
  • An absolute hard cap of 100 hours per month (this cannot be exceeded under any circumstances — it was set by the 2019 Work Style Reform law)
  • The average over any consecutive 2–6 month period cannot exceed 80 hours per month

These limits were introduced after a long campaign following cases of 過労死 (karōshi / death from overwork). They are real legal limits — exceeding them can expose the employer to criminal penalties.

4. Can You Refuse Overtime?

This is the question most workers are afraid to ask. The answer depends on what your contract says and whether your company has a valid sanjū-roku kyōtei in place.

If both of the following are true — your employment contract specifies that overtime may be required, AND your company has filed a valid sanjū-roku kyōtei — then you are legally obligated to work when asked, within the legal caps. Refusing without a valid reason (illness, childcare emergency, etc.) can be treated as insubordination and may lead to disciplinary action.

However, even with a valid 36 agreement, you can legally refuse overtime in these situations:

  • The requested overtime would push you over the legal monthly or annual caps
  • You have a health condition documented by a doctor that prevents extended work
  • The overtime would violate a legally protected reason (e.g., pregnant workers have specific protections)
  • Your contract does not include an overtime clause at all
Warning Some supervisors say "zangyō is voluntary" while making it clear that refusing will hurt your evaluation, renewal prospects, or standing on the team. This informal pressure is very common in Japanese factories. If overtime is legally required under your contract and 36 agreement, you cannot simply refuse. But if you are being coerced into overtime beyond the legal cap — or into unpaid overtime — document everything and consult a labor union or the Labour Standards Inspection Office.

5. Sābisu Zangyō: Unpaid Overtime Is Illegal

サービス残業 (sābisu zangyō / "service overtime" — literally "free overtime") is the practice of working beyond your scheduled hours without pay. It is widespread in Japan and often treated as a cultural norm of "dedication." It is also completely illegal.

Common forms of sābisu zangyō at factories include:

  • Being expected to arrive early to prepare your station or uniform before your paid shift starts
  • Cleaning up, filing paperwork, or attending 朝礼 (chōrei / morning meetings) off the clock
  • Supervisors telling you to "clock out" but continue working
  • Your official time card showing fewer hours than you actually worked

If you are working unpaid hours, you have the right to claim back wages for up to 3 years of unpaid overtime (extended from 2 years under the 2020 Civil Code amendments). Keep your own records — a simple notebook or phone note with your actual start/end times each day is powerful evidence.

6. Overtime and Your Visa: Does It Matter?

For 技能実習生 (gino jisshūsei / technical intern trainees), there are additional restrictions. Your training plan (実習計画 / jisshū keikaku) specifies the hours and activities you are approved for. Overtime that exceeds approved limits can jeopardize your intern status and your company's authorization to host interns. This does not mean you should accept illegal unpaid overtime to "stay safe" — it means both you and your employer have extra obligations to manage working hours carefully.

For workers on Specified Skilled Worker (特定技能 / tokutei ginō) visas or other work visas, overtime rules are the same as for regular Japanese workers. Your legal status does not limit or expand your overtime rights.

7. How to Check If Your Overtime Pay Is Correct

Each month when your payslip (給与明細 / kyūyo meisai) arrives, check these numbers:

  1. Find your base hourly rate (or calculate it from your monthly salary ÷ scheduled hours)
  2. Count how many overtime hours are listed — and cross-check against your own records
  3. Verify that regular overtime hours are paid at 1.25× and late-night hours at 1.5× (or 1.25× if not overtime)
  4. Check that holiday work hours appear with the 1.35× rate
  5. If numbers do not match, first ask your HR or payroll department in writing (email is best for keeping a record)

If your employer denies or ignores a legitimate correction request, your next step is the Labour Standards Inspection Office (労基署 / rōkisho). They can audit payroll records and order back pay.

8. Summary: Know the Numbers, Protect Your Paycheck

Overtime is a fact of life in Japanese factories — but it should always be paid at the correct rate, and it should never exceed the legal limits. The core rules are simple: 25% premium for regular overtime, 25% for late night, 35% for legal holidays, and caps enforced by the 36 agreement. Foreign workers have exactly the same rights as Japanese workers. You do not have to accept unpaid overtime or illegal pressure. Keep your own time records, check your payslip every month, and do not hesitate to escalate if something looks wrong. You earned that money — make sure you receive it.
