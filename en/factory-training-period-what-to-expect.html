What to Expect During the Training Period at a Japanese Factory

Last updated: 2026-04-07  |  6 min read

The first few weeks at a Japanese factory can feel overwhelming, but knowing what to expect makes a huge difference. Training periods are structured, repetitive, and very detail-oriented — and that's intentional.

1. How Long Does Training Last?

Training periods vary by company and job type, but most factories run a formal kenshū (研修) period lasting anywhere from 3 days to 4 weeks. During this time, you may be assigned to a dedicated trainer or work alongside an experienced colleague on the line.

In some factories — especially those following strict quality standards like automotive or electronics — the training period can extend to 2–3 months before you're considered fully independent on your station.

Tip Ask your supervisor or HR on your first day: "How long is the training period?" and "How will I know when it ends?" This helps you set expectations and shows initiative.

2. Safety Training Comes First

Before you touch any machine or step onto the production floor, you will go through safety training. This is non-negotiable at Japanese factories. Expect to learn:

  • Emergency exit locations and evacuation procedures
  • How to wear PPE (personal protective equipment) correctly
  • Machine lockout/tagout rules (shutting down machines safely)
  • What to do if you're injured

You may be asked to sign a form confirming you've understood the safety rules. Keep a copy if possible.

Warning Never skip or rush through safety training, even if a supervisor seems to be in a hurry. If you're injured on the job due to skipping a safety step, it can affect your compensation claim. Safety rules protect you.

3. You Will Repeat the Same Task Many Times

One thing many foreign workers don't expect: Japanese factory training is highly repetitive by design. You may spend an entire day — or several days — practicing just one motion or one step in the production process.

This isn't because they think you're slow. It's because Japanese manufacturing culture values kaizen (改善) and consistency. Mastering one step perfectly before moving on is the standard approach.

Be patient, focus on doing it right, and don't rush. Rushing is often seen as a sign of carelessness.

4. Understanding the Chain of Command

During training, you'll quickly meet the key people in your workplace hierarchy:

Role (Japanese)What They Do
班長 / hanchōTeam leader — your direct supervisor on the line
係長 / kakarichōSection chief — manages several teams
現場リーダー / genba rīdāFloor leader — handles daily operations issues
教育担当 / kyōiku tantōTraining coordinator — manages your onboarding schedule

During training, your hanchō is your most important contact. If you have questions or problems, always go to them first.

5. Documentation and Sign-Offs

Japanese factories are paperwork-heavy. During training, you'll likely fill out or sign multiple documents, including:

  • Safety acknowledgment forms
  • Training checklists (one item per skill learned)
  • Work rule confirmations (attendance, clothing, phone use, etc.)
  • Your shift assignment and locker number forms

Don't just sign without reading. If something is in Japanese and you don't understand, it is completely acceptable to ask for an explanation before signing. Most factories with foreign workers will have a bilingual staff member or a translated summary available.

Tip Keep a small notebook during training. Write down Japanese terms your trainer uses, machine names, and steps in the process. Reviewing it each evening will speed up your learning significantly.

6. Attitude and Behavior During Training

In Japanese workplace culture, taido (態度) — your attitude — matters as much as your skill. Supervisors are watching how you behave, not just what you produce. Key behaviors that are noticed:

  • Punctuality: Arrive before your shift, not exactly at start time
  • Attentiveness: Look at the trainer when they're explaining, nod to show you're listening
  • Asking questions: It's expected and welcomed — silence is sometimes mistaken for not understanding
  • Cleanliness: Keep your workspace tidy at all times, even during breaks

Avoid using your phone during training, even during slow moments. This is considered disrespectful in most Japanese factory environments.

7. What Happens After Training Ends

Once your training period is complete, you'll usually receive a formal sign-off — either verbal from your hanchō or a written form. After that:

  • You'll be assigned your permanent station or rotation
  • Your output will begin to be measured (speed, accuracy, defect rate)
  • You may go through a short evaluation after 1–3 months
Warning Don't assume training is over just because no one told you — always confirm with your supervisor. Working independently before you're approved can lead to quality issues that reflect badly on you.

Training is your opportunity to ask every question you have without judgment. Use it fully. Once you're on the line independently, the pace picks up and there's less time to stop and ask.
