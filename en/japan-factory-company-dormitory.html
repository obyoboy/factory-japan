Living in a Japanese Factory Dormitory (寮): Rules, Costs, and What to Expect

Last updated: April 2026  |  8 min read

The recruiter told you that the company provides housing. That sounds like a great deal — and in many ways it is. Living in a 寮 (ryō / company dormitory) saves you the stress of apartment hunting, cuts your living costs significantly, and puts you close to your workplace. But it also means sharing space with strangers, following house rules you may not have expected, and understanding exactly how much rent is coming out of your paycheck each month. This guide covers everything you need to know before you move in.

1. What Is a Company Dormitory (寮 / ryō)?

A 寮 (ryō) is housing provided or arranged by your employer specifically for workers at their facility. It ranges from a simple room in a shared house near the factory to a purpose-built multi-story residence on or adjacent to the factory site. Not every company offers one, and not every worker at a company that has one is automatically entitled to it — availability often depends on your contract type, how far you've moved to take the job, and whether there are vacancies.

The most common setup you'll encounter is a single private room in a shared building. You have your own space to sleep and store belongings, but kitchens, bathrooms, showers, laundry rooms, and common areas are shared with other residents — usually fellow workers from the same factory or dispatch company (派遣会社 / haken gaisha).

Some larger companies, particularly in automotive and electronics manufacturing, have full residential complexes with cafeterias, vending machines, and recreation rooms. These can feel almost self-contained — which is comfortable but can also make it harder to build a life outside the factory environment.

Shared-room arrangements (two or more people per room) exist but are becoming less common. If you're offered a shared room and prefer privacy, it's worth asking whether a private room will be available in the future or at additional cost.

2. Costs and Deductions: How Much Will You Actually Pay?

Company dormitory rent is almost always deducted directly from your monthly salary rather than billed separately. This is convenient — you never miss a payment — but it also means you need to understand what you're being charged for, because the deductions appear on your 給与明細 (kyūyo meisai / payslip) and can be confusing if you're not prepared.

Typical rent for a company dormitory room runs between ¥10,000 and ¥30,000 per month. This is substantially below market rate for an equivalent private apartment in the same area, which is a genuine financial benefit. However, the total deduction from your pay can be higher once utilities and other costs are added.

Utilities — electricity, water, gas — are sometimes included in the base rent figure, and sometimes charged separately based on usage or as a flat add-on. Internet is often available in dormitories but may cost extra. Laundry machines in shared laundry rooms typically operate on a coin or IC card system, so that cost comes out of pocket.

Look for these line items on your payslip under the deductions section (控除 / kōjo). If you see a charge you don't recognize — particularly anything labeled 住居費 (jūkyo-hi / housing cost), 光熱費 (kōnetsu-hi / utility cost), or similar — ask HR or your派遣会社 coordinator to explain it in detail.

Item Typical Monthly Cost
Rent (room) ¥10,000 – ¥30,000
Electricity ¥2,000 – ¥5,000 (or included)
Water ¥500 – ¥2,000 (or included)
Internet ¥0 – ¥3,000
Coin laundry (shared) ¥2,000 – ¥4,000 (out of pocket)

3. The House Rules: What You Must Know Before You Move In

Every company dormitory has a set of rules, often written in a document called the 寮規則 (ryō kisoku / dormitory regulations). If you're given this document, read it carefully — even if it's in Japanese and you need to use a translation app. Breaking these rules can have serious consequences, including eviction.

Curfews: Many dormitories, especially those run by dispatch companies for younger or newer workers, have curfew rules specifying when the building's entrance is locked or when noise must stop. Common quiet hours are from 22:00 or 23:00 to 6:00 or 7:00. If you work night shift (夜勤 / yakin), make sure the rules account for your schedule, and if they don't, raise this with the dorm manager before it becomes a problem.

Guests: Most company dormitories have strict rules about overnight guests. In many facilities, overnight guests of any kind are prohibited. In others, daytime visits by guests are permitted but must be registered or limited to common areas. Do not assume guests are welcome without checking the rules explicitly. Violating guest policies is one of the most common reasons workers are asked to leave company housing.

Alcohol: Dormitories typically don't prohibit alcohol entirely, but drinking in common areas, loud behavior related to drinking, or returning drunk and disturbing other residents can all be rule violations. Know where you're allowed to drink and be aware of noise levels.

Shared facilities: Kitchen and bathroom etiquette is a frequent source of tension in shared housing anywhere in the world. The general expectation in Japanese dormitories is that you clean up after yourself immediately — leave no dishes in the sink, no personal items scattered around the bathroom, and no food smells lingering. There may be a cleaning rotation (掃除当番 / sōji tōban) that assigns residents responsibilities for common areas.

Noise: Thin walls are common in older dormitory buildings. Be mindful of TV and music volume, alarm sounds, and phone call volume, especially during quiet hours.

4. Sharing Space with Coworkers from Different Countries

Japanese factories employ workers from many countries — Vietnam, the Philippines, Brazil, Indonesia, China, Nepal, and more. In a dormitory, you may find yourself living alongside people whose cultural habits around food, cleanliness, sleeping schedules, and communal space are quite different from yours.

The most common friction points are cooking smells (particularly pungent spices or fish sauce that linger in shared kitchens), noise at different hours of the day (especially when workers are on opposite shift patterns), and different standards for how clean shared spaces should be kept.

The best approach is direct but polite communication early, before tensions build. A friendly introduction and a brief conversation about each other's schedules can prevent most problems. If there's a shared kitchen schedule or a posted set of rules about cooking times, follow it. If there isn't one and conflicts are arising, suggest one to the dorm manager rather than trying to resolve it unilaterally.

Resist the temptation to form exclusively national or linguistic cliques within the dormitory. Workers who make an effort to get along with people from different backgrounds tend to have a much smoother experience — and often develop lasting friendships.

Tip Keep a photo record of your room's condition on the day you move in. Photograph every corner, including any existing scratches, stains, or damage to walls, floors, and furniture. Send the photos to yourself by email or save them to the cloud so they have a timestamp. This protects your deposit if there's a dispute when you move out.

5. What to Do When There's a Problem

If something goes wrong in the dormitory — a maintenance issue, a conflict with a neighbor, a rule violation by someone else — the right person to contact is the 寮長 (ryōchō / dorm manager). Every company dormitory should have one. The ryōchō may be a full-time staff member, a senior worker who takes on the role as a side responsibility, or a property management company representative. Ask who yours is on your first day.

For maintenance problems (broken heating, water leaks, faulty locks), report them in writing if possible — a LINE message, email, or written note — so there's a record. For interpersonal conflicts, go to the ryōchō before the situation escalates. Describe what happened factually, without exaggerating or making accusations you can't support. The ryōchō's job is to keep the dormitory running smoothly, not to take sides.

If the problem involves your rights as a worker — for example, if you believe you're being charged more for housing than what was in your contract, or if a rule is being applied only to certain nationalities — escalate to HR or your dispatch company coordinator. Keep all relevant paperwork and communications.

6. Moving Out: What You Need to Know

When your contract ends or you decide to leave the company, you will need to vacate the dormitory. The required notice period before moving out is usually specified in your contract or the dormitory regulations — commonly one to two months. Do not assume you can leave on short notice; you may be charged rent for the notice period even if you've already left.

When you move out, the company will conduct a 退去立会い (taikyo tachiai / move-out inspection) to assess the condition of your room. This is where your move-in photos become critical. "Normal wear and tear" — faded paint, minor scuffs from regular use — should not be charged to you. Actual damage (large holes in walls, broken fixtures, stains from negligence) typically is your responsibility.

If a deposit (敷金 / shikikin) was collected, it should be returned within a reasonable timeframe after move-out, minus any legitimate deductions. Get the deduction breakdown in writing. If you dispute any charge, ask for the specific clause in the dormitory rules or your contract that justifies it.

Warning Breaking dormitory rules — especially around noise or guests — can result in eviction. If you are evicted from company housing, you may also lose your job. The two are often tied together in your contract. Understand the rules before you move in, and if you're unsure about anything, ask before you do it.

7. Making the Most of Dormitory Life

For many foreign workers in Japan, the company dormitory is not just a place to sleep — it's the foundation of their entire life in the country, at least in the first months. Housing costs are low, the commute is short, and you're surrounded by colleagues who understand your work life. That's a real advantage.

The workers who get the most out of dormitory life are the ones who treat it with the same professionalism they bring to the factory floor: following the rules, communicating clearly with neighbors, maintaining their space, and speaking up early when something isn't right. Do that, and company housing becomes one of the best deals working in Japan has to offer.
